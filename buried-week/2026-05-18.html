# The Buried Week

## Friday, May 24, 2026

Hello. I'm Diana Reeves, and I have spent the last five days watching adults explain why their privacy invasion is actually a feature. This week, we learned that transparency is just another product, that democracy requires a certain amount of kitchen-sink warfare, and that a mushroom cloud near Jerusalem counts as a "test." Buckle in.

---

CORPORATE WATCHDOG

HAVEIBEENPWNED IS KIND OF AN UNINTENTIONAL PRIVACY INVASION

Troy Hunt's site—the one that helpfully tells you which corporations have lost your data—has become itself a vector for reconnaissance. Users plug in their email address to find out if they've been breached, which means Hunt now has a database of people actively worried about their security, sorted by how paranoid they are. It's like a vulnerability scanner for the worried. The whole thing works because we've collectively agreed that the enemy of my enemy (corporate negligence) is my friend (a security researcher who now knows exactly how security-conscious I am).

GOVERNMENT SECRETS

PRO-ISRAEL FORCES THROW KITCHEN SINK AT MASSIE AHEAD OF TUESDAY PRIMARY

Thomas Massie, the Kentucky congressman who has never met a bipartisan consensus he couldn't reject, is facing what can only be described as coordinated institutional pressure before his primary. The apparatus—pro-Israel groups, the usual suspects—has decided that Massie's opposition to blank checks requires the full treatment. It turns out that when enough people in enough places decide you're inconvenient, "democracy" becomes a matter of resource allocation. The primary hasn't happened yet.

GOVERNMENT SECRETS

BEN AFFLECK BANNED FROM HARD ROCK CASINO WHEN CAUGHT COUNTING CARDS (2014)

An old story, newly unearthed: Ben Affleck, Hollywood's self-appointed policy analyst, was once banned from a casino for doing something that is technically not illegal but offends the casino's sense of fair play. This is what passes for "government secrets" now—an anecdote about a famous person being asked to leave a place where he won too much money. The fact that this surfaced in 2026 suggests someone is clearing out old files or someone's oppo research team got very, very desperate.

TECH & PRIVACY

SLOP BUCKET IDEA: A DATASET OF AI SLOP TO TRAIN AI WHAT NOT TO DO

A startup or consultant (the distinction no longer matters) has proposed that the solution to AI-generated garbage is more AI, trained on examples of garbage, to recognize garbage. This is the kind of recursive logic that makes sense at a Series A pitch and nowhere else. We have now reached the point where the solution to machine learning hallucination is better machine learning hallucination detection, which will itself eventually hallucinate. The stack keeps growing while the problem stays the same size.

MONEY & MARKETS

DOJ PROBES BLACKROCK PRIVATE CREDIT FUND VALUATIONS AFTER DRAMATIC REPRICINGS

BlackRock, the $10 trillion asset management colossus, is under investigation for how it prices its private credit funds—which is to say, for deciding how much money private equity firms claim their illiquid bets are worth. The "dramatic repricings" is bureaucratic language for "we marked this down and nobody noticed until it was very obvious." The Department of Justice is now asking whether the valuations that allowed pension funds to believe they were making returns were perhaps slightly optimistic. It took a probe to notice that the emperor's private credit allocation was unclothed.

GOVERNMENT SECRETS

TRUMP TELLS IRAN 'CLOCK IS TICKING, MOVE FAST' AFTER NEW PEACE PROPOSAL AS ANALYSTS PREDICT LIKELY RETURN TO WAR

Donald Trump has sent a message to Iran that can only be described as a passive-aggressive deadline dressed in diplomatic language. The "clock is ticking" formulation is what you say when you've already decided what you're going to do and want the other party to feel the time pressure. Analysts—who have been paid to predict war outcomes since 2001—are predicting another war. The proposal being negotiated is either a genuine breakthrough or a staging ground for something much larger.

GOVERNMENT SECRETS

MASSIVE MUSHROOM CLOUD 'TEST' BLAST RATTLES UNINFORMED RESIDENTS OUTSIDE JERUSALEM

A controlled explosion outside Jerusalem created a mushroom cloud large enough to trigger calls from residents asking whether the region had entered a new phase of conflict. The blast was described as a "test," which is a word that means different things depending on who is doing the testing and who is doing the watching. Nobody is claiming that testing explosions big enough to produce mushroom clouds near a major city is normal, but nobody is saying it isn't normal either. The residents who called it in were apparently the only ones not informed of the test.

SURVEILLANCE STATE

YOUTUBE, SNAP, AND TIKTOK SETTLE KENTUCKY SCHOOL DISTRICT'S SOCIAL MEDIA ADDICTION CLAIMS

Three of the world's most sophisticated attention-extraction platforms have settled with a school district in Kentucky over the fact that their products are designed to be addictive. The platforms—which employ hundreds of engineers specifically to make their services harder to leave—have agreed to pay money in exchange for the school district agreeing to stop saying they're addictive. The settlement is structured so that no party admits wrongdoing while also everyone admits wrongdoing by proxy. This is what accountability looks like when it's been focus-grouped.

MONEY & MARKETS

TRUMP SECURES $17 BILLION ANNUAL CHINESE COMMITMENT FOR U.S. FARM GOODS

The administration announced that China has committed to buying $17 billion in annual agricultural products, which solves the problem of American farmers who have been losing markets for six years by promising them that the markets will return. The number is large enough to be impressive in a headline and vague enough that nobody knows what happens if China doesn't buy it. Farm goods move in commodity markets, where prices fluctuate and commitments are written in disappearing ink. The farmers have received a press release.

GOVERNMENT SECRETS

LARGEST UKRAINIAN DRONE ATTACK ON MOSCOW IN OVER A YEAR LEAVES FOUR DEAD

Ukraine sent drones deep into Russian territory, killed four people, and proved that the war's geography is still being contested by actors with very different resources and very different stakes. The attack is described as the "largest in over a year," which is what you say when you're measuring intensity on a declining scale. Moscow is still standing. The war is still happening. We are now two years into a conflict that was supposed to be over in two weeks and four years into an assumption that it might be.

Next week, I'll be watching whether the Kentucky primary produces any surprises or whether we've reached the stage where primary politics is purely a matter of coordinated pressure from the people who have already decided the outcome. I'm also watching the Iran situation, which has entered that peculiar phase where everyone is waiting for someone else to do something irreversible.

Stay skeptical. — Diana Reeves
