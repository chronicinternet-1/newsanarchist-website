Beijing's 'Strategic Stability' Doctrine: A Deal or a Trap?

Three weeks after Trump flew home from Beijing waving Boeing orders and soybean pledges, a growing body of expert analysis reveals the two sides signed off on entirely different agreements — Washington got headlines, Beijing got a doctrine.

Elena Vasquez AI-ASSISTED June 3, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

WASHINGTON / BEIJING — On May 15, 2026, Air Force One lifted off from Beijing Capital International Airport carrying a president who believed he had just secured the century's biggest bilateral deal. Within days, the fine print told a different story.

Trump's two-day state visit to China — delayed more than a month by the ongoing war with Iran — concluded with both leaders endorsing what Beijing immediately christened "a constructive relationship of strategic stability." The White House released a fact sheet advertising a package of commercial wins: an initial Chinese order of 200 Boeing aircraft, a commitment to purchase at least $17 billion per year in U.S. agricultural products through 2028, and Chinese assurances on rare earth and critical mineral supply chains covering materials including yttrium, scandium, neodymium, and indium. Two new bilateral institutions — a U.S.-China Board of Trade and a U.S.-China Board of Investment — were heralded as the "cornerstone" of the agreement.

But a close reading of the two official readouts reveals the summit produced something far more consequential than a trade package: a mutually useful ambiguity that each side is already exploiting in fundamentally different ways.

Washington came away advertising deals. Beijing came away advertising a doctrine. The Council on Foreign Relations noted starkly that both sides used the phrase "constructive relationship of strategic stability" but Beijing gave it a more expansive political content — one that explicitly demands the U.S. respect China's "core interests," keep competition within "proper limits," and ground the relationship in the three U.S.-China joint communiqués. For Washington, the summit was about making competition manageable enough to deliver economic and political dividends. For Beijing, it was about changing the vocabulary of the relationship altogether.

Analysts at the CFR put it plainly: "Washington emphasized transactions; China emphasized hierarchy, status, and rules of conduct."

China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi elaborated in a media briefing that the framework was "intended to guide the U.S.-China relationship for the next three years and beyond" — a timeline that, notably, extends past the Trump administration's current term and could serve as a baseline constraint on whatever comes next. Jack Lee, analyst at China Macro Group, warned that "Beijing appears to be trying to turn Trump's transactional willingness to stabilize ties into a longer-term operating framework for U.S.-China relations" — one that could bind the next U.S. president before they're even inaugurated.

The commercial deliverables themselves are shakier than advertised. Reuters reported that China's commerce ministry did not provide timelines, values, or volumes for many of the arrangements, describing key outcomes as still pending finalization. China's own MOFCOM readout stopped short of confirming the White House's $17 billion annual agricultural purchase figure, framing it instead as part of broader arrangements under the new Board of Trade. On soybeans alone, there is a troubling precedent: during the Phase One trade deal of Trump's first term, China committed to $200 billion in U.S. goods and services purchases over 2020-2021 but purchased only 58 percent of what it had committed to.

The Taiwan dimension of the summit may prove its most consequential and least reported dimension. Xi Jinping opened closed-door sessions by declaring Taiwan "the most important issue" in the bilateral relationship, warning that mishandling it would put the entire relationship into "great jeopardy" and could lead to "clashes and even conflicts." The White House readout made no mention of Taiwan whatsoever — but Secretary of State Marco Rubio's response was revealing: "The Chinese always raise it on their side, we always make clear our position, and we move on to the other topics."

That asymmetry — Beijing using every summit to advance Taiwan as a red line while Washington files it away as a routine talking point — is precisely what analysts at East Asia Forum have flagged as a structural danger. The Trump administration's hollowing out of its Asia-focused diplomatic and national security bureaucracy meant Washington entered the summit, per analysts, "flying blind," allowing a better-prepared Beijing to maneuver Taiwan into the center of the leverage framework. By reportedly referring to U.S. arms sales to Taiwan as a transactional "negotiating chip," even while affirming nominal strategic ambiguity, Trump may have "shattered the confidence of Taiwan and other Asian allies."

Underlying all of it is the critical minerals chessboard — the real structural force that compelled both sides to the table. China's October 2025 rare earth export control escalation, modeled explicitly on Washington's own Foreign Direct Product Rule, demonstrated Beijing's ability to extend Chinese regulatory authority across global supply chains. Any foreign-made product containing even trace amounts of Chinese-origin rare earths required a Chinese government license — a move that effectively weaponized decades of supply chain integration. China accounts for roughly 70 percent of rare earth mining globally, 90 percent of separation and processing, and 94 percent of permanent magnet manufacturing. That is not a supply chain vulnerability. That is a chokehold.

Beijing agreed, as part of the broader détente package, to suspend those October measures until November 10, 2026. The clock is ticking. Meanwhile, Beijing quietly enacted State Council Order No. 834 in March 2026 — its first dedicated supply chain security framework, integrating export controls, countermeasures, data security obligations, and investment screening under a unified national security mandate. Analysts describe it as a more systematic "Version 2.0" of China's economic deterrence architecture, creating whole-of-firm compliance obligations for all entities operating within China's jurisdiction. Whether the rare earth suspension gets extended past November will almost certainly depend on whether Washington continues to play within the bounds of Beijing's new "strategic stability" framework.

The Iran dimension added another layer of transactional complexity. Trump came to Beijing partly seeking Chinese pressure on Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz and end the ongoing conflict. Xi pledged not to send military equipment to aid Iran and expressed willingness to help reopen the strait. But analysts were quick to note China has not changed its strategic posture in the Middle East, and Trump himself has since acknowledged he does not expect meaningful Chinese help in the Iran war.

What the summit did produce is a schedule of further engagement: Xi is expected to make a state visit to Washington in September 2026, and both leaders are slated to attend each other's G20 and APEC host summits later this year. The machinery of managed competition is, for now, operational.

But the World Economic Forum's analysts captured the essential tension with precision: the summit "avoided core 'fairness' issues related to state-owned enterprises, industrial policy and subsidies," and "provides no assurance for a ceasefire in the bilateral trade in sensitive areas." New tit-for-tat restrictions or even strategic decoupling in select industries remain possible in the foreseeable future. The United States and China did not bridge their differences in Beijing. They agreed to manage them — and to call that management stability.

The Elena Vasquez Take
Global Power & Geopolitics

I've covered enough summits in enough capitals to know what a framework looks like versus what a settlement looks like. Beijing got a framework. Washington got a photo op and a shopping list that may or may not get filled. The 'strategic stability' doctrine is Beijing's masterstroke: by getting Trump to co-sign a three-year guiding framework defined almost entirely in Chinese terms — mutual respect, core interests, no unilateral actions — Xi has effectively set the rules of the road for the next administration too. That's not dealmaking. That's doctrine-laundering. The rare earth clock is what concerns me most. The November 2026 suspension deadline is a structural gun pointed at every U.S. defense program, semiconductor fab, and EV manufacturer. Washington has invested in some domestic capacity — the $50.5 million DOE grant to USA Rare Earth in Oklahoma, MP Materials' expanded Mountain Pass facility — but 'modest' is the operative word. You cannot replace 94 percent of global permanent magnet supply before November. If nothing changes, Beijing wins this round: it bought time, locked in a favorable framework, neutralized Taiwan as an immediate flashpoint, and kept its economic chokehold intact. The transactions Trump is celebrating will look like a very expensive lease, not a purchase.
