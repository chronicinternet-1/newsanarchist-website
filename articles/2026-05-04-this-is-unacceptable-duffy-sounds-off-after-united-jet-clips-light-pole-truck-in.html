"This Is Unacceptable": Duffy Sounds Off After United Jet Clips Light Pole, Truck In New Jersey

What they're not telling you: # "This Is Unacceptable": Duffy Sounds Off After United Jet Clips Light Pole, Truck In New Jersey A United Airlines Boeing 767 with 231 people aboard struck both a light pole and a delivery truck on a New Jersey highway during final approach to Newark Liberty International Airport—an incident that Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy called "unacceptable" despite acknowledging the aircraft was piloted by "really well-trained pilots." The Sunday afternoon collision involved Flight UA169, a Boeing 767-424 arriving from Venice, Italy. The aircraft's landing gear clipped infrastructure and a vehicle on the New Jersey Turnpike as it descended toward Newark. Local reporting confirmed the struck vehicle was a delivery truck operated by Schmidt Bakery, a Baltimore-based company.

What the Documents Show

The driver had departed from the Baltimore metro area headed to a company depot in New Jersey when the incident occurred. According to Chuck Paterakis of H&S Family of Bakeries, the truck driver sustained minor injuries from broken glass but was later released from the hospital. Duffy's public characterization of the incident—delivered to Fox Business on Monday morning—reveals a critical tension in aviation safety discourse. He simultaneously defended American pilots as "the safest guys, the busiest guys, bar none" while declaring the event "unacceptable" and stating flatly, "This should never happen in America." This framing obscures a fundamental question: if the pilots are exceptionally well-trained, what systemic or procedural failures allowed a jumbo jet to descend low enough to strike ground-level infrastructure during approach to a major metropolitan airport? The mainstream aviation narrative typically emphasizes America's statistical safety record and the professionalism of flight crews—points Duffy reiterated.

Follow the Money

Yet this incident suggests the gap between pilot competence and actual safety outcomes may be wider than official statements acknowledge. A commercial aircraft striking both a utility pole and a vehicle on an active highway during approach indicates either a procedural breakdown, an equipment malfunction, or guidance system failure that transcends individual pilot performance. Duffy's statement that "the FAA is going to arrive today" and that investigators "will ascertain" whether the truck was hit underscores how preliminary the official understanding remains. The incident also raises questions about ground-level safety infrastructure near major airports. That a delivery truck could be on the New Jersey Turnpike in a position to be struck by descending aircraft suggests potential gaps in airspace management or traffic control coordination between airport operations and highway authorities—another dimension of the story that tends to disappear beneath reassurances about pilot training. For ordinary travelers and ground-based workers, this collision presents an uncomfortable reality: even in America's supposedly safest aviation system, rare but catastrophic failures remain possible.

What Else We Know

A few hundred feet of altitude difference, a slightly different wind condition, or a marginally different aircraft trajectory could have converted minor injuries into mass casualties. Duffy's promise that "we study, we look at and we learn from" such incidents offers limited reassurance without transparency about what systemic vulnerabilities this event exposed and how they will be addressed.
