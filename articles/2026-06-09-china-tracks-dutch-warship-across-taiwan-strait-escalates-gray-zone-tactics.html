China Tracks Dutch Warship Across Taiwan Strait, Escalates Gray-Zone Tactics

China's military dispatched naval and air assets to monitor the Dutch frigate HNLMS De Ruyter as it transited the Taiwan Strait on June 5, escalating pressure on foreign navies asserting freedom of navigation in disputed waters.

Rafael Reyes AI-ASSISTED June 9, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

TAIPEI — The People's Liberation Army Eastern Theater Command publicly confirmed on Friday that it had deployed forces to track the Dutch frigate HNLMS De Ruyter throughout its passage through the Taiwan Strait, marking another chapter in Beijing's systematic campaign to assert dominance over one of the world's most contested waterways. The move represents a calculated escalation in what analysts call gray-zone tactics—military pressure designed to assert territorial claims without triggering open conflict.

Senior Colonel Xu Chenghua, spokesperson for the PLA Eastern Theater Command, announced that Chinese naval and air assets maintained "surveillance, warning, and intelligence activities" during the frigate's movement through the strait. Beijing did not specify which vessels or aircraft were involved in the tracking operation. The statement comes on the heels of an earlier confrontation in the Paracel Islands, where China accused the same Dutch vessel of illegally intruding into what Beijing calls Chinese territorial airspace beginning May 27.

The HNLMS De Ruyter, a De Zeven Provinciën-class air defense and command frigate equipped with advanced radar systems, vertical launch capabilities, and anti-ship missiles, was conducting a roughly five-month Indo-Pacific deployment aimed at strengthening diplomatic and security ties with regional partners. The vessel had previously called at Kochi, India, where it conducted passage exercises with the Indian Navy before proceeding eastward toward the Taiwan Strait.

The Dutch government has acknowledged Chinese tracking activity but pushed back against Beijing's characterization of events. The Netherlands asserted that the warship was operating in accordance with international law and sailing through the South China Sea for legitimate diplomatic, security, and economic purposes. The Dutch Ministry of Defense confirmed awareness of the monitoring but has not provided detailed counterfactual information about either the Paracel Islands incident or the Taiwan Strait transit.

What makes this incident significant is its pattern. The HNLMS De Ruyter's passage follows months of escalating Chinese pressure on foreign naval vessels asserting freedom of navigation rights through international waters. Earlier in May, a Canadian Navy frigate, HMCS Charlottetown, transited the strait on May 22—a deliberate passage that occurred just before Beijing's Foreign Minister Wang Yi was scheduled to visit Canada for the first time in a decade. The Chinese ambassador to Canada had previously warned that such transits could jeopardize bilateral relations, signaling Beijing's intent to leverage economic partnerships to discourage freedom of navigation operations.

China's approach has evolved beyond simple confrontation. The PLA Eastern Theater Command's statement emphasized readiness to "resolutely safeguard China's sovereignty and security, as well as regional peace and stability"—language that reframes Beijing's military posturing as defensive rather than aggressive. This rhetorical shift accompanies a documented shift in operational tactics. According to the June 5 AEI-ISW China & Taiwan Update, the PLA carried out 217 aerial incursions into Taiwan's Air Defense Identification Zone during May alone, representing a slight increase from April but remaining below the 300+ monthly average from 2024-2025. The variation appears deliberate—maintaining unpredictability while avoiding the frequency that could trigger automatic responses from Taiwan's air defenses.

Data from the International Crisis Group and military monitoring services shows that Chinese naval vessels, including aircraft carriers, are transiting the strait with increasing frequency. Taiwan detected 190 naval vessels in surrounding waters as of late April 2026, with a Chinese carrier conducting its first passage of the year on April 20. Coast Guard incursions into areas near Taiwan's outlying islands—Kinmen, Matsu, and Pratas—have become routine. ISW-CDOT confirmed 46 Kinmen incursions and 32 Pratas incursions during 2025, with patrols continuing into 2026.

The Dutch frigate incident also reveals a broader strategic shift: Beijing is systematically testing the commitment of U.S. allies to freedom of navigation operations. NATO nations operating in Indo-Pacific waters face a calculation that did not exist five years ago—whether to exercise their legal rights to transit international waters knowing they will face coordinated military surveillance, electronic warfare, and sharp diplomatic pushback. The use of electronic warfare systems during the earlier Paracel Islands confrontation signals that China is willing to deploy active countermeasures against foreign warships.

Western sources close to Taiwan operations indicate growing concern about what they term the "salami slicing" of international norms. Each uncontested intrusion by China into these zones—from the Taiwan contiguous zone to the Paracel Islands—lowers the threshold for the next incursion. The June 5 Brussels Morning analysis quoted unnamed analysts saying there is "a strategic hardening of positions that leaves very little room for traditional diplomacy to breathe."

For Taiwan itself, the implications are acute. The June 5 AEI-ISW update revealed that Taiwan's parliament cut funding for domestic drone procurement and joint U.S.-Taiwan drone development from the originally proposed Special Defense Budget, which passed in reduced form on May 8. This budgetary constraint arrives precisely as China accelerates military operations around the island. Meanwhile, the PLA is demonstrating new capabilities: the Justice Mission 2025 exercises in December 2025 simulated a full blockade of Taiwan, with Chinese Coast Guard vessels encircling outlying islands concurrent with military operations.

Western intelligence assessments appear calibrated to avoid alarm fatigue. The March 2026 Office of the Director of National Intelligence Annual Threat Assessment concluded that Chinese leaders lack a fixed timeline for Taiwan unification and are unlikely to pursue invasion in 2027, instead favoring continued coercive measures. However, these coercive measures—the routine surveillance missions, the electronic warfare encounters, the continued erosion of the contiguous zone—carry their own risks. Taipei maintains that any intrusion into its territorial airspace will be treated as a first strike, with the right to respond with force. The contiguous zone, however, has no equivalent red line. This creates a dangerous gray area where miscalculation becomes increasingly likely.

The Dutch government has not yet issued substantive response to the incidents, and it remains unclear whether the De Ruyter will continue operations in the Taiwan Strait as originally planned. The vessel's deployment was intended to demonstrate European commitment to rules-based international order, but the encounter with PLA forces has tested that commitment in real time. How Europe—and specifically how the Netherlands—responds to this pressure will signal whether Beijing's gray-zone tactics are achieving their strategic objective: to exhaust the will of U.S. allies to maintain freedom of navigation in these critical waters.

For now, the Taiwan Strait remains tense and routinized—a dangerous combination. The next foreign naval vessel transiting these waters will likely trigger a similar response. Whether that vessel's nation maintains course or acquiesces to Chinese pressure remains to be seen.

The Rafael Reyes Take
Conflict & Emerging Wars

This is the pattern that will define the next decade in the Indo-Pacific. China is not invading Taiwan in 2026. It doesn't need to. It's slowly, methodically strangling the international legal framework that underpins freedom of navigation in these waters. Every time a Dutch frigate or Canadian warship transits and receives a coordinated military response, Beijing wins—not because it needs to shoot, but because it's establishing the precedent that these waters belong to China. Taiwan's government is alert. Western navies still conduct these operations. But the will to maintain the friction required to sustain these transits is being tested deliberately, incrementally, and effectively. If that will breaks—if European and Canadian naval operations through the strait become too diplomatically costly—then the status quo shifts without a shot fired. And if the status quo shifts, Taiwan loses the only leverage it has: the international indifference that comes from freedom of navigation being treated as normal. Watch what Europe does next. That tells you whether Beijing wins this war of patience.
