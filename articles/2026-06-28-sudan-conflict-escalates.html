Sudan Conflict Escalates

US sanctions Indian firm for fuelling civil war in Sudan, as Rapid Support Forces close in on El Obeid

Rafael Reyes
War correspondent, 11 years covering unreported conflicts
June 28, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

The Sudan conflict has escalated in recent months, with the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) militants closing in on the strategic city of El Obeid, prompting experts and rights groups to warn of a "genocide-like" event, similar to what unfolded in El Fasher, as reported by ndtv.com. The conflict has been ongoing since 2023, with the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and RSF accused of threatening, attacking, and killing journalists and activists, according to the Sudanese civil war (2023–present)) Wikipedia page, which documents over 40 violations in May 2023 alone. Several journalists were injured or killed, and 13 newspapers ceased operations, highlighting the dire situation for media freedom in the region.

The international community has taken notice of the conflict, with the US imposing sanctions on an Indian firm and its CEO, among eight others, for "fuelling" the civil war in Sudan, as reported by Business Standard. The US Department of State spokesperson, Tommy Pigott, stated that these networks supply weapons, explosives, and foreign fighters to both the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces, prolonging a conflict that has created the world's worst humanitarian crisis and provided space for terrorist groups to operate. This move is seen as part of a broader effort to counter the influence of other global powers, such as China, in the region, as discussed in the article US Counters China.

The situation in Sudan is complex and multifaceted, with various factions and interests at play, similar to other ongoing conflicts in the region, such as the Myanmar War Far From Over. The conflict has also drawn comparisons to other global hotspots, such as the tensions between Taiwan and China, where Taiwan Detects Chinese Incursions have become a regular occurrence. As the situation in Sudan continues to deteriorate, it remains to be seen how the international community will respond to the growing humanitarian crisis and the threat of genocide-like violence.

The Rafael Reyes Take
Conflict & Emerging Wars

As I reflect on the escalating conflict in Sudan, I firmly believe that immediate international intervention is necessary to prevent further humanitarian catastrophe. The ongoing violence has devastating consequences for innocent civilians, and it's imperative that we take a stand. If nothing changes, the true winners will be the extremist groups and warlords who continue to exploit the chaos for their own gain. They will reap the benefits of a destabilized region, while the Sudanese people suffer. It's our responsibility to act and support a peaceful resolution, rather than allowing the conflict to escalate further.
