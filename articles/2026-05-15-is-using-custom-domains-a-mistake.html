Follow the Money

The Reddit privacy community has begun articulating what technologists privately acknowledge: custom domains create a trackable anchor point in an otherwise fragmented digital identity. A person using Gmail addresses like john.smith.2024@gmail.com can theoretically create dozens of variations to compartmentalize their online presence. That same person with a custom domain johnssmith.com cannot easily fragment their identity without buying additional domains—an expensive proposition that defeats the purpose of decentralization. More problematically, once a custom domain is tied to one compromised account or data breach, that single point of failure compromises the entire infrastructure. The email address you believed you controlled becomes evidence in your digital dossier. Mainstream privacy guides rarely examine the downstream consequences of domain ownership.

What Else We Know

When you purchase a domain, that transaction creates records with payment processors, domain registrars, and hosting companies—each a potential surveillance chokepoint. A person attempting to minimize corporate tracking often simply relocates their dependency rather than eliminating it. They've traded Google's surveillance for their registrar's surveillance, often without evaluating whether the new entity has stronger privacy practices. The assumption that "owning" infrastructure equals privacy protection obscures a harder truth: visibility and ownership are not synonymous with autonomy online. The broader implication cuts deeper than domain strategy. The custom domain debate exposes a fundamental misunderstanding shaping how people approach digital privacy in 2026.
