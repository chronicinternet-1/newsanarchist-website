It’s easier for Californians to escape data brokers following a Markup investigation

The Markup and CalMatters showed how website code could make it harder for Californians to exercise their right to remove personal data. Now much of that code has disappeared.

In the spring of 2025, reporters at The Markup and CalMatters conducted a technical audit of websites operated by data brokers registered under California law. What they discovered was systematic obstruction: thirty-five companies had embedded "no-index" code into their data deletion pages—invisible instructions to Google and other search engines to prevent these pages from appearing in search results.

What the Documents Show

The practical effect was clean and calculated. A California resident exercising their legal right to request deletion of personal data would have to know the exact URL, navigate directly to it, and complete the request. The law itself—California's Consumer Privacy Act—required these companies to provide deletion mechanisms. The law never said those mechanisms could be hidden from the internet's most basic discovery tool. The Markup and CalMatters published their findings in 2024, and the consequences were immediate and measurable.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Within months, twelve of the thirty-five brokers removed the blocking code. By May 2026, when the outlets conducted a follow-up investigation, only eight companies remained in violation of this transparent obstruction. The shift accelerated after New Hampshire Democratic Senator Maggie Hassan, the ranking member of the Senate Joint Economic Committee, sent letters directly to five major data brokers: IQVIA Digital, Comscore, Telesign Corporation, 6sense Insights, and an unnamed fifth company. Within the Senate inquiry's scope, four of those five engaged with Hassan's committee and agreed to make their deletion pages visible in search results. What the official timeline obscures is elementary: California law required these deletion mechanisms to exist since 2020.
