What they're not telling you: # AI Note-Takers in Legal Meetings Could Destroy Attorney-Client Privilege—And Nobody's Talking About It Lawyers are discovering that the same AI recording tools their clients use to boost productivity might be systematically dismantling one of the law's most fundamental protections: the confidentiality between attorney and client. The concern centers on a legal doctrine so foundational that it predates modern privacy law by centuries. Attorney-client privilege exists to allow clients to speak freely with their lawyers without fear that those communications will be used against them in court or by adversaries.

What the Documents Show

It's the linchpin of the entire legal system. But when a client brings an AI note-taking device into a confidential legal meeting—or worse, when a client shares those AI-generated transcripts with third parties—the privilege can evaporate. Courts have consistently held that privilege is waived when confidential communications are disclosed to people outside the attorney-client relationship. An AI note-taker in the room, recording and potentially storing data on external servers, creates exactly this vulnerability. What makes this particularly urgent is the gap between what's happening on the ground and what's being publicly discussed.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Tech companies market these tools as harmless productivity enhancers—perfect for busy professionals who want to outsource the drudgery of meeting notes. Users are encouraged to deploy them everywhere, in every meeting. The mainstream tech press has largely celebrated AI note-takers as unambiguous wins for efficiency. But legal professionals are quietly panicking. According to discussions among lawyers navigating this problem, the tools introduce liability that clients often don't understand they're accepting. The mechanics of the problem vary depending on the specific service.

What Else We Know

Some AI note-takers process audio locally, while others transmit recordings to cloud servers for transcription. Even locally processed data creates risk if the client later shares those notes in discovery or with opposing counsel. But cloud-based processing introduces an additional nightmare scenario: the AI company itself becomes a third party who has heard the confidential communications. That's almost certainly privilege waiver right there. Some services retain audio indefinitely, creating a permanent record of attorney-client conversations stored outside anyone's control. The situation reveals a classic regulatory lag.
