    Ohio State University Reaches $100 Million Settlement With Nearly 300 Sex Abuse Survivors

    Diana Reeves
    Former SEC examiner
    June 5, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: Ohio State's $100 Million Price Tag for Two Decades of Institutional Negligence Ohio State University settled with 279 survivors of Richard Strauss's sexual abuse for $100 million—a figure that, when divided by victim, amounts to $358,424 per person for decades of documented institutional failure to stop a known predator. The university's board ratified the settlement on Wednesday, following years of litigation over abuse that occurred between 1978 and 1998, when Strauss retired from the faculty. A 2019 investigative report documented that Strauss, employed simultaneously by Ohio State's athletic department and medical staff for nearly twenty years, had sexually abused at least 177 men—nearly all of them students.

    What the Documents Show

    The report made explicit what the settlement now attempts to quantify: university staff knew of the abuse and chose inaction. This was not negligence born of ignorance. It was negligence born of institutional preference. The settlement came after more than 500 plaintiffs sued Ohio State. In February, the university had already reached eight additional settlements covering 304 total survivors and exceeding $60 million in payouts.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    The cumulative cost to the institution now approaches $160 million for a crisis entirely preventable through basic accountability mechanisms that existed in 1978 and remain standard practice at most universities today. What the mainstream reporting treats as resolution, however, obscures the actual transaction at work. This is not a punishment. It is a negotiated transfer of dollars from the university's endowment and operating reserves—ultimately from students, donors, and taxpayers—to survivors who should never have been positioned to need compensation in the first place. Strauss himself avoided all consequence through suicide in 2005. No Ohio State administrator faced criminal charges.

    What Else We Know

    No medical board revoked licenses. The structure incentivizes settlement over accountability because settlements preserve institutional reputation while criminal or regulatory action would expose decision-makers by name and force examination of who authorized the cover-up. The settlement amount, while substantial in isolation, represents less than one year of Ohio State's $2.5 billion annual operating budget. For an institution with an endowment exceeding $14 billion, the payout registers as a manageable cost of doing business. The university absorbs it, adjusts its annual budget, and maintains operational continuity. The survivors receive funds.

    The Diana Reeves Take
    Corporate Watchdog & Money & Markets

    What I find striking is that we treat $100 million as accountability when it is actually the opposite: it is the price of avoiding it. The pattern here is institutional across American higher education. When sexual abuse within universities reaches litigation scale, the cost is externalized through settlement payments rather than internalized through structural reform, leadership turnover, or regulatory sanction. The beneficiary is clear: Ohio State's institutional continuity and reputation, preserved at the expense of survivors' delayed justice and the public's inability to know whether similar failures persist elsewhere.

    The legislature could mandate independent medical board oversight of athletic department personnel. It hasn't. The state's higher education regulator could require transparent reporting of abuse allegations and institutional response timelines. It hasn't. The Board of Trustees could restructure its governance to include mandatory external audits of Title IX compliance. It won't.

    What readers must understand: settlements are not victories. They are admissions of institutional failure purchased with money, not accompanied by the structural changes that would prevent repetition. Watch whether Ohio State's next comprehensive governance report includes any new external accountability mechanisms. That's your measure of whether this settlement changed anything or simply made it more expensive to ignore abuse next time.
