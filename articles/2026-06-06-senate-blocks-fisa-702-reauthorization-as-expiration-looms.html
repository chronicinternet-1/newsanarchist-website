Senate Blocks FISA 702 Reauthorization as Expiration Looms

The Senate rejected a long-term reauthorization of Section 702, the government's most expansive warrantless surveillance authority, leaving the program six days from expiration and no clear path forward.

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 6, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

On Friday morning, the Senate voted 47-52 to block a motion to proceed on Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, a stunning collapse of negotiations that leaves the government's most consequential mass surveillance apparatus in constitutional limbo. Section 702 expires June 12—six days away—and Congress has failed to pass a clean reauthorization or agree on reforms that civil liberties advocates have been demanding for months.

The immediate cause of the blockade: President Trump's appointment of Bill Pulte as acting director of national intelligence, announced just days before the vote. Pulte, a social media personality and philanthropist, has no intelligence experience. More damaging to bipartisan negotiations was his prior role as head of mortgage agencies, where Democratic senators say he "weaponized" confidential information. Senator Mark Warner (D-VA), who chairs the Intelligence Committee, framed the DNI appointment as disqualifying: "Does anybody think it makes good sense to give them the keys to the 18 intelligence agencies, not just on 702, but across the board?"

But the vote reveals deeper fractures. Seven Republican senators—Josh Hawley of Missouri, John Kennedy of Louisiana, Mike Lee of Utah, Rand Paul of Kentucky, Eric Schmitt of Missouri, Rick Scott of Florida, and Tommy Tuberville of Alabama—joined Democrats in opposing the measure. These aren't routine dissenters. Mike Lee and Rand Paul have been vocal about Fourth Amendment protections. Their defection, combined with Democratic opposition, broke the bipartisan coalition that Senate Intelligence Chairman Tom Cotton (R-AR) and Ranking Member Mark Warner had briefly forged.

What they were voting on matters. The bill offered no meaningful privacy protections. It was a straight reauthorization—what civil liberties groups call empty calories. The House had already passed its own version on April 29, 235-191, with the same weaknesses: no warrant requirement for searching Americans' data, no ban on government purchases from data brokers, no AI-specific safeguards.

The real conflict here is between national security claims and Fourth Amendment reality. Section 702, enacted in 2008, permits the National Security Agency to collect the communications of foreign targets abroad without individualized court orders. The law's architects claimed it would target only foreigners. In practice, it sweeps in vast quantities of Americans' phone calls, texts, and emails—what the law calls "incidental collection." Once collected, the FBI, CIA, NSA, and National Counterterrorism Center conduct thousands of warrantless "backdoor searches" annually, querying that data using Americans' names, email addresses, and phone numbers.

The abuse pattern is documented. The FBI has been caught searching Section 702 data for racial justice protesters, congressional staff, campaign donors, journalists, and a state court judge. In August 2024, the Department of Justice discovered that the FBI had quietly deployed a querying tool allowing users to access Americans' communications while bypassing basic procedural safeguards—mandatory attorney approval, documentation of search rationale, internal audits. The tool operated for months before DOJ shut it down. In March 2026, the FISA Court issued another opinion detailing ongoing FBI violations.

This track record explains why Mike Lee and other conservatives joined Democrats in blocking reauthorization. Lee and Elizabeth Warren introduced the Government Surveillance Reform Act in March 2026, which would close the "data broker loophole"—the practice of agencies purchasing bulk location and behavioral data from commercial vendors without warrants. The bill would mandate warrants for queries of Americans' data, prohibit reverse targeting (using foreign surveillance to pretend-target Americans), and repeal what reformers call the "make everyone a spy" provision, a controversial 2024 expansion allowing the government to compel Americans and companies to conduct surveillance on its behalf.

But leadership in both chambers rejected these reforms. House Speaker Johnson pushed the straight reauthorization. Senate Majority Leader John Thune set the Friday vote specifically to force a decision before the weekend recess, hoping to restart negotiations the following week. It didn't work.

What happens now remains unclear. Congress could pass another short-term extension—it's done this twice already since April 20. The House passed a 45-day extension on April 30, then both chambers unanimously passed another extension that same day. These were meant to buy time for bipartisan compromise. No compromise materialized. Instead, the Pulte appointment became a forcing function. Schumer said the timing "clearly make[s] passing an extension of FISA much harder." Democrats signaled they'll let the program lapse rather than reauthorize it under a DNI they view as unfit.

Meanwhile, artificial intelligence has become the new urgency. In March 2026, AI company Anthropic refused to allow its technology to be used for what the Defense Department wanted: analyzing bulk commercial and foreign intelligence data to create comprehensive profiles of Americans at scale. CEO Dario Amodei warned that government access to data broker records combined with AI could assemble "a comprehensive picture of any person's life—automatically and at massive scale." The Pentagon objected to Anthropic's safeguards. Anthropic held firm, but OpenAI and others haven't. That asymmetry terrifies privacy advocates.

Section 702 is set to expire in six days. The Senate is in recess. Congress returns next week. The calendar itself has become the opposition.

The Marcus Webb Take
Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

This is what constitutional breakdown looks like in slow motion. The Senate just voted to let the government's most invasive mass surveillance authority expire rather than reauthorize it without guardrails—not because they suddenly developed backbone, but because Trump appointed a socially-unsuitable DNI. The real scandal isn't Pulte. It's that we needed his appointment to make Congress consider reform, and even that might fail. Section 702 enables warrantless access to Americans' communications at scale. The FBI abuses it systematically. Congress has documented proof. And yet leadership in both chambers tried to slip a clean reauthorization through without public debate. If lawmakers allow 702 to lapse—or if they reauthorize it with only cosmetic changes—the surveillance state will have won twice: once by normalizing mass data collection, again by making the reforms look impossible. Either way, the architecture remains. The precedent holds. The weaponization tools stay in place. Nothing changes unless Congress votes differently next week.
