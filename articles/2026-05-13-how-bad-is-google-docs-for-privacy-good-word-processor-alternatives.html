Follow the Money

When Google Docs users create documents, Google retains comprehensive metadata: keystroke timing patterns, revision histories, collaboration networks, and content analysis. Unlike Microsoft Office, which offers offline functionality and local storage options, Google Docs operates as a surveillance-friendly cloud service by design. The source material doesn't detail Google's specific data monetization practices here, but the architecture itself creates persistent digital records of how people think, write, and collaborate—information far more valuable than basic demographic data. For a generation taught to trust Google implicitly from elementary school onward, this feels normal rather than invasive. The mainstream tech press frames this as a simple productivity choice: cloud-based collaboration versus desktop software. This framing obscures the actual story.

What Else We Know

Google didn't win market share through superior features; it won through institutional capture. Public schools adopted Chromebooks partly because they were cheap, yes, but also because Google provided the complete ecosystem—hardware, operating system, applications, and the email accounts to tie them together. Once schools standardized on Google, teachers designed curriculum around Google tools, textbooks assumed Google access, and graduating students carried their Google dependency into universities and workplaces. The switching costs became psychological and institutional, not just technical. Alternative word processors exist—LibreOffice, Nextcloud, Joplin, and open-source options provide offline-first or privacy-respecting alternatives—but none have benefited from a decade of government-subsidized classroom conditioning. This isn't a failure of competing products; it's a failure of antitrust enforcement that allowed one corporation to use public institutions to build irreversible market dominance.
