Uber Builds Large Stake In Germany's Delivery Hero As Takeover Speculation Builds

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist
May 22, 2026 · 4 min read min read

What they're not telling you: # UBER'S 25% GAMBIT: HOW MORGAN STANLEY QUIETLY BUILT A TAKEOVER MACHINE WHILE REGULATORS SLEPT Uber Technologies has assembled a 25.1% controlling stake in Germany's Delivery Hero through a coordinated strategy involving Morgan Stanley traders, yet disclosed this position only after accumulating 19.5% equity plus 5.6% in options—a threshold-gaming approach that appears deliberately designed to navigate regulatory disclosure requirements without triggering immediate antitrust scrutiny. The mechanics are clean, which makes them damning. Morgan Stanley's traders executed the accumulation, according to people familiar with the matter, meaning a Wall Street investment bank served as the operational architect of what Uber now frames as a casual exploration.

What the Documents Show

This wasn't passive portfolio management. This was structured acquisition planning conducted through intermediaries. Uber subsequently disclosed on Monday that it holds 19.5% directly plus 5.6% in call options—a combined position that gives it veto power over major corporate decisions at a company operating across 60 countries. The official story from Uber is anodyne: the stake reflects strategic interest in Delivery Hero's global footprint and represents a hedge against DoorDash's competitive dominance outside the United States. Uber stated it "currently" has no intention of increasing its stake beyond 30%.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Notice the qualifier. "Currently" is present-tense permission for future acquisition. The 30% threshold itself—a carefully chosen ceiling—appears calibrated to avoid triggering mandatory takeover bid requirements under German securities law, which typically require an offer at 30%. Uber's positioning suggests institutional knowledge of these thresholds. What deserves scrutiny is not just Uber's ambition but the absence of preemptive regulatory intervention. The European Commission, already investigating Uber's market practices across multiple jurisdictions, did not publicly condition this acquisition on competitive analysis before the stake was announced.

What Else We Know

Germany's Federal Cartel Office (Bundeskartellamt), which has authority over merger control, appears to have been presented with a fait accompli. Delivery Hero CEO Niklas Östberg and the board accepted the stake without public disclosure of communications with antitrust authorities beforehand. The market reaction reveals what regulators missed: Delivery Hero shares jumped nearly 50% this year and doubled from March lows around 15 euros. This wasn't a market validating Uber's "strategic interest." This was the market pricing in a takeover that consolidates global food delivery into fewer hands. JPMorgan analysts assigned explicit takeover value to Delivery Hero assets. Berenberg analyst Wolfgang Specht wrote that scenarios involving a potential acquisition now merit valuation weight.

The Jordan Calloway Take
Government Secrets & FOIA

What I find striking is how this move reveals the gap between antitrust law written for the 20th century and acquisition strategies built for the 21st. Uber didn't violate rules—it exploited the space between them.

The pattern here is institutional: regulatory agencies operate on disclosure timelines and public announcements, while sophisticated capital markets operate on information asymmetry and threshold-gaming. Morgan Stanley understood German securities law better than German regulators understood Uber's intentions. That's not a failure of individual officials—it's a structural failure of oversight designed to react rather than anticipate.

The beneficiary of the official narrative—"strategic interest," "currently no plans to increase"—is Uber itself. These formulations preserve optionality while building an irreversible position.

Watch whether the European Commission conditions approval of any takeover bid on structural divestitures in European markets. If it doesn't, you'll know that consolidation in food delivery has already won.
