Grusch Goes Back to the Capitol — This Time With a Deadline

Three years after testifying under oath that the U.S. government is hiding non-human craft, whistleblower David Grusch returns to Capitol Hill on June 9 alongside four members of Congress to demand specific named files be released — as two tranches of newly declassified UAP footage have already left the disclosure community arguing over whether America is getting transparency or theater.

ORIGINAL REPORTING

WASHINGTON — On June 9, 2026, at 1:00 PM on the steps of the U.S. Capitol, the most consequential press event in the modern history of UAP disclosure is scheduled to take place — and depending on whom you ask, it is either the beginning of the end of a decades-long cover-up, or an increasingly frustrated community pushing against a wall that keeps moving.

Whistleblower David Grusch, a former Air Force major and senior intelligence officer who first told Congress in 2023 that the U.S. government was concealing recovered non-human spacecraft, will take center stage alongside four bipartisan members of Congress: Reps. Tim Burchett, Jared Moskowitz, Anna Paulina Luna, and Eric Burlison. The event will be hosted by investigative journalist Leslie Kean and documentary filmmaker James Fox.

The backdrop for the June 9 gathering is unprecedented. In February 2026, President Trump ordered federal agencies to identify and declassify government files related to UAPs and extraterrestrial life. The Department of War — Trump's executive-order rebranding of the Pentagon — launched the Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters, PURSUE, on May 8, releasing the first tranche of 162 declassified files from the FBI, Department of Defense, NASA, and State Department. The portal, housed at war.gov/ufo, drew more than one billion page hits following the first release alone.

The first batch included military pilot reports, eyewitness testimony, Apollo mission photographs, NASA transcripts of astronauts discussing lunar anomalies, and law enforcement footage of what AARO — the Pentagon's All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office — described as "among the most compelling" cases in the archive: orbs launching other orbs. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth framed the release as ending an era of denial. The documents, he said, had been "hidden behind classifications" for too long.

But scientists were unmoved. Astrophysicist David Whitehouse, reviewing the materials publicly, concluded the imagery showed "some are optical artefacts, others fuzzy blobs, and some light smears." Astronomer Michael Narlock of the Cranbrook Institute of Science said the documents largely contained eyewitness transcripts, which are "notoriously unreliable," while Seth Shostak of the SETI Institute reaffirmed his view that "there is no compelling evidence for extraterrestrial life thus far."

Two weeks later, on May 22, the Pentagon dropped a second, larger tranche: 222 files, including 51 videos, 7 NASA mission audio recordings, and contributions from the CIA, ODNI, and Department of Energy — making it the first multi-agency PURSUE release. This batch hit differently. Notably clearer footage than the first drop included video of a four-UAP formation over Iran, a Syrian UAP case catalogued as exhibiting "instant acceleration" with no visible propulsion, and — most explosively — footage of a USAF Air National Guard F-16C engaging and shooting down an unidentified aerial object near Lake Huron. A 2025 senior intelligence officer account described two large orbs chasing U.S. fighter jets after being scrambled, leaving personnel, in the officer's own words, "virtually speechless."

For the first time, the second batch also included color video of a UAP — a black sphere — rather than the now-familiar black-and-white thermal imagery that has characterized virtually every previously released clip. Some of the encounter footage, like the Lake Huron shoot-down, had been previously acknowledged publicly, but this marked the first time the actual video was released.

Still, the critical caveat embedded in the Pentagon's own assessments: most cases carry prosaic explanations. AARO attributed a UAP sighted near Eglin Air Force Base in Florida to a commercial lighting balloon. The official line has been consistent since day one — the War Department says it is providing no comment or assessment on the files overall, so "the American people can make up their own minds."

That studied ambiguity is exactly what the June 9 coalition is trying to puncture.

The three concrete demands being formalized at the Capitol press conference: presidential declassification of specific UAP files Grusch has identified by name to intelligence oversight bodies; congressional passage of the UAP Disclosure Act, which would require every federal agency to publish its UAP records on a public website; and strengthened whistleblower protections against administrative retaliation, plus international coordination encouraging allied governments to contribute their own UAP evidence.

Rep. Burlison has been the most pointed about what the coalition believes is the current chokepoint: not Trump, but the career intelligence apparatus beneath him. "It's the obligation of Congress to make sure career intelligence officials don't obstruct his directive, in order to prevent selective disclosure or another dead end," Burlison said in a statement ahead of the event.

Grousch himself has characterized the retaliation he and others have faced as "administrative terrorism" — a pattern of clearance complications, professional isolation, and institutional pressure designed to keep credible witnesses silent.

The Disclosure Foundation, formerly the UAP Disclosure Fund and chaired by Christopher Mellon, former deputy assistant secretary of defense for intelligence, has been operating on multiple parallel tracks. In May, the NSA produced more than 300 pages of historical UAP records following a FOIA appeal by the Foundation — records previously classified at the "TOP SECRET UMBRA" level, one of the most sensitive classification tiers in signals intelligence. Despite being decades old, many of those records remain heavily redacted.

The international dimension is also accelerating. France's National Assembly is scheduled to host a UAP conference on June 29, 2026, backed by the Space Studies National Center (CNES) and GEIPAN, France's official UAP study group, which has existed since 1977.

Release 03 of the PURSUE files is currently in development, according to tracker sites monitoring the portal. Chief Pentagon spokesman Sean Parnell confirmed on May 18 that more documents are "actively being processed for publication."

What June 9 cannot do is force executive action. What it can do — and what the coalition is clearly engineering — is create the kind of public pressure moment that is increasingly difficult to ignore when the government's own portal is crashing under a billion hits and a former Air Force intelligence officer is standing on the Capitol steps naming classified files by reference.

The Casey North Take

Here's what bothers me about the current moment: the architecture of PURSUE is almost perfectly designed to look like disclosure without being disclosure. You release blurry blobs alongside one genuinely arresting video — a fighter jet shooting something down over Lake Huron — and you let the public argue about balloons. You say you're not providing any official assessment. You let people make up their own minds. That is not transparency. That is managed ambiguity at industrial scale. Grusch on the Capitol steps with four members of Congress demanding specific named files is the correct escalation. If those files exist and contain what Grusch has claimed under oath, then what we've seen so far from PURSUE is the overture, not the opera. The UAP Disclosure Act — requiring every agency to publish records on a public website — is the legislative lever that could actually force the issue past the classification bureaucracy. If nothing changes after June 9, the winners are the same people who have always won this game: the career classification officers who outlast every administration and every news cycle. That's not a conspiracy theory. It's just how bureaucratic inertia works.
