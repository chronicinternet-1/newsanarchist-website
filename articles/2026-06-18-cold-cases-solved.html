Cold Cases Solved

Students and police collaborate to resolve cold cases nationwide

Sam Okafor
Former federal prosecutor, 12 years in DOJ
June 18, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

Recent developments in cold case investigations have shed new light on previously unsolved crimes, bringing closure to families and justice to victims. The Western Michigan University Cold Case Program, recognized as a national benchmark for student-police partnerships, has been instrumental in resolving eight cold cases and reinvigorating over 80 other investigations since its inception. Students in the program have worked closely with detectives from the Michigan State Police and 13 other law enforcement agencies to achieve these results.

One notable example of a cold case being solved is the Long Island Serial Killer case, in which Rex Heuermann pleaded guilty to the murders of eight women on April 8, 2026. The victims included Megan Waterman, Melissa Barthelemy, Amber Lynn Costello, Maureen Brainard-Barnes, Valerie Mack, Jessica Taylor, Sandra Costilla, and Karen Vergata. This case highlights the importance of persistence and collaboration in solving cold cases, as well as the devastating impact of these crimes on families and communities.

In Texas, efforts to solve cold cases are being expanded through the growth of the Texas Rangers' cold case program. The program is adding staff to address unsolved crimes, demonstrating a commitment to justice and accountability. This expansion is a significant development, as it will enable investigators to devote more resources to cold cases and increase the chances of solving them. With a growing number of cold case programs and initiatives across the country, there is hope that more families will soon find the closure and justice they deserve. As these programs continue to grow and evolve, they will likely rely on a combination of cutting-edge technology, meticulous investigation, and collaboration between law enforcement agencies and community organizations.

The Western Michigan University Cold Case Program's success has come at no additional cost to taxpayers, as the program is funded through existing resources and partnerships. This model has been recognized as a national benchmark, and other states are taking notice. Texas, for example, is allocating significant resources to its cold case program, with the Texas Department of Public Safety investing in new staff and technology to aid in investigations. While the exact dollar amount of this investment has not been disclosed, it is clear that states are committing significant resources to solving cold cases and bringing justice to victims and their families.

The Sam Okafor Take
True Crime & Justice

As I reflect on the state of cold cases, I firmly believe that it's imperative we allocate more resources to solving them. My thesis is that with advancements in technology and a concerted effort from law enforcement, we can bring closure to countless families. If nothing changes, the perpetrators of these heinous crimes win, able to live out their lives without facing justice. The victims' families and loved ones also lose, forever left with unanswered questions and a deep sense of uncertainty. It's our responsibility to ensure that justice is served, and I urge policymakers to take a closer look at cold case investigations.
