1 in 4 Americans Has Crossed Over — And Science Still Can't Explain It

A bombshell new poll released this week finds 23 percent of American adults say they have died, crossed into another realm, and returned — and the best neurophysiological model science has produced to explain it just got dismantled by the field's own leading researchers.

Vera Solano AI-ASSISTED June 2, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Something extraordinary is converging this week, and almost no one in the mainstream press is paying attention.

On May 28, the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS) released the results of a sweeping new poll conducted with research firm Centiment — timed to coincide with the first-ever Near-Death Experience Week, running June 1 through June 7, 2026. The numbers are staggering. Nearly one in four American adults — 23 percent — say they have had a near-death experience, defined in the survey as having "transitioned to another realm and then returned to human existence." Another 35 percent say they personally know someone who has.

To put that in human scale: the U.S. population is approximately 340 million. At 23 percent, that means somewhere in the neighborhood of 78 million living Americans believe they have already been somewhere most of us are still waiting to go.

The IANDS poll was conducted by Centiment in March 2026 and drew 2,100 responses. The question framing matters here — and IANDS is transparent about it. Earlier landmark polls used far more vague language. George Gallup Jr.'s famous 1980–81 survey asked whether respondents had been "on the verge of death" with "unusual experiences" — and clocked in at around 5 percent. When the question specifies realm-crossing and return, the number jumps to nearly a quarter of the country. Either the experience is far more common than we thought, or many more people have been quietly carrying something they've never had language for until now.

The emotional data from the poll is just as striking. Of respondents who said they knew an NDE survivor, 44 percent reported that hearing about the experience increased their curiosity about the afterlife, and 40 percent said it deepened their belief in one. Meanwhile, a remarkable 77.8 percent — essentially four out of five Americans — said there is either major value or some value in studying near-death experiences scientifically. Only 24 percent said there was insufficient evidence to take the phenomenon seriously. The fear of death data is notable too: just 14.8 percent said dying scared them "a great deal," while 25 percent said it scared them only "a little."

This is the backdrop against which a separate, fierce scientific debate has been quietly raging since late 2025 — and came to a head in January 2026 with a formal volley from the University of Virginia's Division of Perceptual Studies, arguably the most credentialed NDE research unit in the world.

The target of UVA's critique is a model called NEPTUNE — the Neurophysiological Evolutionary Psychological Theory Understanding Near-Death Experience — developed by an international team of researchers led by neuroscientist Charlotte Martial of the University of Liège in Belgium, whose work focuses on states of consciousness under conditions of unresponsiveness, including cardiac arrest. The NEPTUNE model proposes that NDEs can be comprehensively explained through cascading physiological and psychological processes in a dying brain: changes in blood-brain gases, endorphins, TPJ (temporoparietal junction) stimulation, and other chemical and electrical brain activity. It was published as an ambitious attempt to finally unify decades of competing theories under a single materialist framework.

But in January 2026, UVA's Bruce Greyson, MD — who literally invented the standard clinical measuring instrument for NDEs, the Greyson NDE Scale, in 1983 — and his colleague Marieta Pehlivanova, PhD, published a point-by-point dismantling of NEPTUNE in the journal Psychology of Consciousness: Theory, Research, and Practice. Their verdict was blunt. "The NEPTUNE model was a pioneering attempt to explain NDEs, but it selectively ignored scientific evidence that contradicts the model and failed to address some of the most important and defining parts of NDEs," Greyson said.

The critique cuts at NEPTUNE's core assumptions. The model repeatedly frames NDEs as "hallucinations" caused by brain chemistry. But Greyson and Pehlivanova point out that neurological hallucinations typically involve only a single sense — hearing or sight. What NDE survivors consistently report is something profoundly different: full multisensory environments — things seen, heard, smelled, and touched — that are retained in vivid, emotionally charged detail not for days or weeks, but for decades. Unlike hallucinations, which are quickly forgotten, NDE memories don't fade. They tend to feel, in the words of many survivors, more real than ordinary waking life.

On the question of out-of-body experiences — arguably the most empirically testable component of NDEs — the UVA researchers are equally unconvinced by NEPTUNE's proposed mechanism. NEPTUNE invokes studies of TPJ (temporoparietal junction) brain stimulation to explain the sensation of leaving the body. But Greyson and Pehlivanova counter that during TPJ activation, there is a sense of disembodiment but visual perception remains entirely normal — meaning the person does not see their own body, does not feel capable of moving through space independently. Real NDE out-of-body experiences are qualitatively different from anything electrical brain stimulation has produced in a lab.

There's also a devastating EEG problem. The NEPTUNE model leans on gamma wave surges in dying brains as potential biomarkers for NDEs. But a key finding in the underlying data shows the signal is inverted: none of the patients who reported NDEs showed the supposed EEG biomarker activity, and none of the patients who showed that activity reported NDEs. That's not a correlation. That's a contradiction.

Grounding all of this is what Greyson and Pehlivanova call a "philosophical bias" built into the NEPTUNE model from its first premise — the assumption that consciousness is entirely a product of the brain. They argue that this isn't a fundamental tenet of neuroscience, as NEPTUNE's authors frame it. It is a philosophical position. And when you build an empirical model on top of an unexamined philosophical assumption, you will inevitably exclude any data that doesn't fit.

Meanwhile, this week, IANDS is running its first-ever Near-Death Experience Week. Tonight — June 2, 2026 — the organization is hosting a free public panel discussion featuring experiencers Vinney Todd Tolman and IANDS President Evan Mecham, moderated by Executive Director Janet Riley. On June 6, the IANDS Afterlife Revealed podcast hosts a discussion titled "What Academic Research and Personal Stories Tell Us About Near-Death Experiences," with guests including author Anita Moorjani. The IANDS 2026 Annual Conference is scheduled for August 27–30 at the Hyatt Regency Bellevue in Bellevue, Washington.

The science is fracturing in real time. And 78 million Americans are already on the other side of the question.

Here is what I keep coming back to: the NEPTUNE team set out to write the definitive materialist explanation for NDEs — and the person who literally invented the clinical tool used to measure NDEs just told them, in a peer-reviewed journal, that they missed the point. That's not a minor methodological quibble. That's the field's own most credentialed researcher saying: your framework cannot hold the data. And then, the same week, a poll drops telling us that nearly one in four Americans believes they have been to another realm and returned. These aren't fringe reports from people on the edges of culture. These are your neighbors, your nurses, your coworkers — quietly carrying experiences that science has no container for. If nothing changes — if institutional neuroscience keeps building models that start with the answer and work backward — then the winners are the models, not the truth. The experiencers will keep being dismissed as anecdotal. And the most common profound experience in American life will remain the most underreported story in science journalism. That ends here, today, with 78 million witnesses.
