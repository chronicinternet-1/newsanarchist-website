She Allegedly Killed Her Stepfather in 1975 — Then Lived in His House for 50 Years

A 79-year-old Arizona woman was arrested last week for her stepfather's 1975 murder — and prosecutors say she spent the next half-century living in his home and collecting up to $600,000 from his pension.

Sam Okafor AI-ASSISTED June 2, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

TUCSON, Ariz. — William Reginald Sipfle was 73 years old when he vanished in the fall of 1975. His family noticed he was gone, but no one filed a missing person report. They just knew, as families sometimes do, that something was wrong — that he had "up and disappeared," as Pima County Sheriff Chris Nanos put it. What they didn't know was that his dismembered remains had already been discovered dumped at the Pima County Waste Transfer Station on South Continental Road near Ryan Airfield. For fifty years, those remains had no name.

Now they do. And the story of how investigators finally gave them one is both a testament to the quiet revolution happening in forensic science and a window into one of the most disturbing alleged betrayals in recent cold case history.

On May 28, 2026, the Pima County Sheriff's Department arrested Carol Ann Beall, 79, on a charge of first-degree murder in connection with Sipfle's death. Beall is Sipfle's stepdaughter. Prosecutors allege she killed him, then spent the next fifty years living in his home — the same property at 7460 N. La Cañada Drive that Sipfle had owned — and collecting between $250,000 and $600,000 from his pension and Social Security benefits. The state has argued that the primary source of Beall's wealth came directly from her stepfather's death. She is being held on a $500,000 bond and is set to be arraigned on June 4.

Then, just days after her arrest, the case got darker. On June 1, after investigators learned that more evidence could be concealed on the property — the same home that had belonged to Sipfle in 1975 — they executed a search warrant. They excavated part of the land and found additional human remains. The Pima County Sheriff's Department said that based on what they currently know, they believe the evidence is related to Sipfle's case. DNA testing will be conducted to confirm.

This is the kind of case that makes prosecutors go quiet. The alleged crime is not just a murder — it is a murder allegedly followed by a half-century of sustained deception: living in a dead man's house, cashing a dead man's checks, watching a family grieve without answers while, if prosecutors are right, holding those answers every single day.

The break in the case came not from a tip or a deathbed confession, but from science that didn't exist when the crime was allegedly committed. In 2025, the Pima County Sheriff's Department partnered with a company specializing in forensic genealogy, submitting evidence for advanced DNA testing and genealogical analysis. The DNA led investigators to Sipfle's granddaughter. She told detectives her grandfather had gone missing in 1975 when he was 73 years old, and that no missing person report had ever been filed. That lead allowed investigators to positively identify the long-unidentified remains as William Reginald Sipfle — and from there, the trail led to Beall.

The work was conducted by Resolve Forensics and Intermountain Forensics, two Salt Lake City-based companies that have quietly become essential players in the cold case revolution now reshaping American law enforcement. The Sipfle family acknowledged them by name in their public statement, thanking the Pima County Sheriff's Department Cold Case Unit and both forensic genealogy firms for making identification possible.

Sheriff Nanos was frank about what investigators were up against from the start. "You had no idea what you have in front of you because you don't know who this is," he told local media. "There's no fingerprints to be had." Investigators in 1975 could only determine that the dismembered remains belonged to an adult male. Without any further identification, the case went cold almost immediately — and stayed cold for five decades.

For the Sipfle family, the news arrived like a wave. "The Sipfle family is relieved to have closure regarding the whereabouts of our grandfather," their statement read, "with the positive identification of our grandfather's remains." They asked for privacy while processing circumstances that are, by any measure, extraordinarily difficult to absorb: learning simultaneously who killed your grandfather and that his killer allegedly lived in his home and drew his retirement income for half a century.

There is one detail from Pima County court records and local archives that lingers. In April 1975 — months before Sipfle's dismembered remains were found at the waste transfer station — the Arizona Daily Star reported that Sipfle's home on La Cañada Drive had sustained nearly $6,000 in fire damage, the equivalent of roughly $38,000 in today's dollars. Beall is listed as the property's current owner. Investigators have not publicly said whether the fire is considered related to Sipfle's death.

Beall herself has not spoken publicly about the charges. She appeared at her first court hearing virtually, from jail. A public defender has been appointed to represent her.

This case is not an outlier. It is part of a visible pattern accelerating through 2026: forensic genealogy is systematically dismantling the statute of limitations on violence. Just days before the Beall arrest, Madison County prosecutors in Illinois charged 70-year-old Albert "Buddy" Zigler with the 1993 bludgeoning death of Randy Gail Sperino, after DNA evidence submitted to Texas-based lab Othram yielded a genealogical lead that broke a 33-year stalemate. In February, Elmira, New York authorities announced they had identified the killer of a 12-year-old girl murdered in 1963 — calling it the oldest case ever solved using DNA.

The technology works by extracting DNA from evidence collected at crime scenes — sometimes decades-old trace samples — sequencing it at a genomic level, and then running the resulting profile against public ancestry databases to build family trees. Those trees, worked backward and forward by forensic genealogists, eventually point investigators toward living relatives who can either confirm identities or lead to suspects. What once required a cold case to land in CODIS — the FBI's national database of convicted offenders — now requires only that a distant cousin uploaded their 23andMe results.

For investigators, that is a profound shift. For people who committed crimes before modern DNA forensics existed and assumed they had outrun the law, it is something else entirely.

The Pima County case is scheduled to proceed with Beall's arraignment on June 4. The additional human remains found at the La Cañada property are undergoing DNA analysis. The investigation, as Sheriff Nanos has made clear, is ongoing.

I spent twelve years as a federal prosecutor. I have seen plenty of cold cases. What I have almost never seen is a suspect who allegedly turned a murder into a fifty-year financial arrangement — living in the victim's house, drawing the victim's pension, while investigators worked with nothing but unidentified bone fragments. If the prosecution's theory is correct, Carol Ann Beall did not just allegedly kill William Sipfle. She allegedly converted his death into a half-century income stream, collecting up to $600,000 while his family had no idea what happened to him. What this case should force us to confront is not just the alleged crime — it's the systemic failure that made it possible. No missing person report was ever filed. Dismembered remains sat unidentified at a Pima County landfill for fifty years. That is not just a forensic gap. It is a bureaucratic one. If genealogy forensics hadn't advanced, William Sipfle would still have no name on those remains. His alleged killer would still be cashing his checks. The people who win when nothing changes are the ones allegedly living in dead men's houses.
