    Surveillance State

    With US spy laws set to expire, lawmakers are split over protecting Americans from warrantless surveillance

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    April 20, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: The Expiration Deadline Congress Won't Meet: Section 702 of FISA Enters Final Reauthorization Window Without Clarity on Warrantless Surveillance Limits Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, which authorizes the NSA to conduct warrantless surveillance on communications flowing through US telecommunications infrastructure, is set to expire unless Congress reauthorizes it before the deadline—and lawmakers remain fundamentally divided over whether to impose new restrictions on how the government accesses Americans' communications without court approval. The statute, enacted in 2008 and last reauthorized in 2017, permits the NSA to compel telecommunications carriers and internet service providers to grant access to their networks for collection of foreign intelligence targets. The mechanism operates without individual warrants.

    What the Documents Show

    Instead, the government certifies to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court that it is targeting non-US persons reasonably believed to be outside the United States. The problem, according to declassified documents and prior surveillance audits: the NSA has repeatedly collected communications of US citizens incidentally—and in some documented cases, intentionally—during this collection. The Office of the Director of National Intelligence released a transparency report in 2022 showing that Section 702 collection touched thousands of Americans' communications annually, though the precise number remains classified. Internal NSA audits obtained by privacy advocates revealed that between 2017 and 2019, the agency conducted queries of Section 702 data using US citizens' identifiers without establishing probable cause or obtaining judicial authorization—a practice the agency had previously assured Congress was rare. When confronted with the audit findings, the NSA stated the queries constituted permissible "upstream" collection under existing rules, but did not disclose the full scope of how many times such queries had occurred.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Congress is now split between those demanding explicit statutory language requiring a warrant before the government can search Section 702 databases for Americans' communications, and those arguing that existing FISA Court oversight is sufficient. Senator Ron Wyden and others have circulated draft language requiring judicial approval before querying Section 702 data for US persons. The intelligence community, including the NSA and CIA, has opposed warrant requirements, arguing they would slow counterterrorism and counterintelligence operations. The House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence has indicated it favors reauthorization with minimal new constraints. The deadline creates a procedural pressure that historically favors the intelligence agencies: if Congress does not pass reauthorization legislation before the expiration date, the entire surveillance authority lapses, creating a gap that no administration—Democratic or Republican—has allowed to occur since 2008. That structural urgency has, in past reauthorizations, pushed Congress toward accepting the administration's preferred language rather than negotiating new restrictions.

    What Else We Know

    Technical specifics matter here: any new warrant requirement would need to define whether it applies to "about" collection (communications merely mentioning a target) or only "to/from" collection (direct communications with a target). The NSA has historically conducted vastly more "about" collection, meaning the scope of any warrant requirement directly determines the impact.

    The Marcus Webb Take
    Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

    The reauthorization dance reveals something the mainstream coverage typically obscures: Congress does not actually control the scope of mass surveillance—institutional inertia does. I find striking the basic asymmetry here. The intelligence agencies have a finite, public deadline to argue their case. Lawmakers have nothing to lose by delaying debate until crisis pressure forces them to accept whatever compromise the agencies prefer. The pattern here is that every FISA renewal becomes a referendum not on whether surveillance will continue, but on how much paperwork agencies must generate to do it.

    What benefits from the official framing—"lawmakers are split"—is precisely this: political theater that masks consensus. Democrats and Republicans genuinely disagree on warrant requirements. The intelligence agencies, however, do not disagree. They uniformly oppose them. And historically, when institutional consensus exists in the executive branch, Congress eventually accommodates it, usually by the moment the clock runs down.

    Readers should watch whether any reauthorization bill that passes includes enforceable audit mechanisms with independent oversight—not NSA self-audits released years after the fact. That single detail determines whether this authority will face future consequences or simply continue expanding.
