Facial Recognition Expands

Meta is testing facial recognition for police and military, raising concerns about privacy and surveillance

Marcus Webb
Former NSA contractor turned privacy watchdog
June 27, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

Facial recognition technology is expanding its reach, with Meta testing the feature for police and military use, as reported by Schneier on Security, which has raised concerns about privacy and surveillance. This development comes as the use of facial recognition in public safety and retail is becoming more widespread, with 63% of deployments using unencrypted Bluetooth Low Energy beacons, making them vulnerable to spoofing or interception, according to a report by world-today-news.com. The normalization of facial recognition technology is also evident in its use at events like the 2026 World Cup, where fans can opt into face-based entry, as well as in airports, where passengers can use Google Wallet to authenticate using facial recognition without needing to scan their ID.

The expansion of facial recognition technology has sparked debate about the need for clear regulations and guidelines to ensure the rights of the public are protected. Liberty, a civil liberties organization, has called for the police use of facial recognition technology to be put on pause until a dedicated legal framework is established, as reported in Lawmakers Must Act. The head of the Met Police has made a case for the use of Live Facial Recognition (LFR) to stay ahead of criminal advances, but this has been met with criticism from privacy advocates. Meanwhile, the ATF Cancels Phone Tracker decision has been seen as a positive step towards protecting individual privacy, but more needs to be done to address the growing use of surveillance technology.

The use of facial recognition technology is not limited to law enforcement and public safety, as it is also being used in other areas, such as retail and transportation. For example, Kansas City is pushing ahead with the use of facial recognition on buses, as reported by PCMag. The use of this technology raises concerns about privacy and surveillance, and it is essential to have a clear understanding of how it is being used and what safeguards are in place to protect individual rights. As Meta Tests Facial Recognition for police and military use, it is crucial to consider the potential implications of this technology and ensure that it is used in a way that respects individual privacy and civil liberties.

The Marcus Webb Take
Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

As I consider the rapid expansion of facial recognition technology, I firmly believe that our personal freedoms are at risk. My thesis is that without strict regulations, this technology will continue to erode our right to privacy. If nothing changes, corporations and governments will be the winners, gaining unprecedented access to our personal lives and behaviors. They will use this information to manipulate and control us, further solidifying their power and influence. Meanwhile, ordinary citizens like myself will be left vulnerable to surveillance and exploitation, our autonomy and individuality slowly being stripped away.
