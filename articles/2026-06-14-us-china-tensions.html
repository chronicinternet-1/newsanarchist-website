US-China Tensions

China expels US journalists amid rising tensions

Elena Vasquez AI-ASSISTED June 14, 2026 4 min read

As a seasoned foreign correspondent, I have been covering the escalating tensions between the US and China for years, and the situation continues to deteriorate. The latest salvo was fired by the US when it added several large Chinese companies to the Pentagon's list of firms it says are aiding China's military, a move that Beijing has described as "strongly dissatisfying". This development has significant implications for the already strained relations between the two superpowers, with many foreign policy analysts warning that a Chinese attack on Taiwan could draw the US into a destructive and costly war with China.

In recent months, we have seen a series of tit-for-tat moves by both sides, with the US imposing restrictions on Chinese media outlets and China retaliating by expelling American correspondents from The New York Times, The Wall Street Journal, and The Washington Post. This has led to a significant deterioration in the working conditions for journalists on both sides, with the US moving Chinese citizens at non-American news outlets from open-ended work visas to extendable 90-day work visas. The State Department has also designated several Chinese media outlets as foreign embassies, further straining relations.

The tensions between the US and China are not just limited to media outlets, but also extend to trade and security issues. The US has been increasingly critical of China's human rights record, particularly in Xinjiang, and has imposed sanctions on several Chinese companies. China, on the other hand, has accused the US of interfering in its internal affairs and has called for the US to treat Chinese companies fairly. The Chinese commerce ministry has urged the US to reconsider its decision to add Chinese companies to the Pentagon's list, warning that it could have significant consequences for bilateral relations.

As the situation continues to escalate, there are growing concerns about the potential for a military conflict between the US and China. Many analysts believe that a Chinese attack on Taiwan could be the spark that sets off a wider conflict, with the US committed to defending the island under the Taiwan Relations Act. The US has also been strengthening its military presence in the region, with several high-profile naval deployments in recent months. The cost of such a conflict would be catastrophic, with estimates suggesting that it could run into trillions of dollars.

In the midst of these tensions, there are some signs of diplomacy, with Chinese mainland actor Zhang Linghe calling for more cross-Straits exchanges during the 18th Straits Forum on Saturday. However, such gestures are likely to be overshadowed by the larger geopolitical tensions between the US and China. As the situation continues to unfold, it is clear that the US and China are engaged in a high-stakes game of diplomatic brinksmanship, with significant implications for global stability and security. With the US presidential election looming, it remains to be seen how the situation will play out, but one thing is certain - the US-China tensions will continue to be a major story for years to come.
