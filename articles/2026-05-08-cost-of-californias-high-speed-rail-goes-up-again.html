Cost Of California's High-Speed Rail Goes Up Again

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist
May 8, 2026 · 4 min read

What they're not telling you: # Cost Of California's High-Speed Rail Goes Up Again California's high-speed rail project has ballooned to at least $126 billion—nearly four times the $33 billion price tag voters approved in 2008—with no firm timeline for laying a single track. The project represents a spectacular failure of government accountability. When Californians voted for Proposition 1A in 2008, they were promised 65.5 to 96.5 million intercity riders annually by 2030.

The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

# THE TAKE: California's High-Speed Rail Is a $113 Billion Lie Voters got played. Period. In 2008, the pitch was clean: $33 billion. Today? The California High-Speed Rail Authority buried *another* cost explosion—now $113 billion minimum, with completion nowhere near sight. That's a **242% lie**. Where's the accountability? The Authority's own documents show endemic scope creep, environmental litigation they should've anticipated, and land acquisition disasters. Yet we get press releases, not prosecutions. Gavin Newsom *knows*. His 2023 "revised" plan admitted the Central Valley route is financially dead. He pivoted to LA-SF instead of killing the zombie project entirely. This isn't infrastructure failure—it's institutional corruption dressed in green politics. Billions vaporized. No one fired. No one charged. California voters deserve either a functional train or their money back. They're getting neither.

What the Documents Show

Today, the High-Speed Rail Authority (HSRA) pushes the "full service start" date to 2040—a decade delay that the agency glosses over in glossy press releases. The cost explosion dwarfs nearly every infrastructure megaproject in American history, yet most voters remain unaware of how dramatically the promised system has transformed. Thompson, a 15-year member of the California High Speed Rail Peer Review Group created by legislation, issued a withering assessment in a letter to lawmakers. Thompson, who helped create Amtrak, stated plainly: the current plan describes "a project that has reached a dead end" and is "not, not even remotely, the system the voters approved." His verdict carries particular weight—he wasn't a skeptic hired to criticize, but an insider watching the project deteriorate from within. Yet mainstream coverage largely ignored his assessment, treating the project's repeated failures as administrative growing pains rather than fundamental collapse.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Governor Gavin Newsom's public promises reveal the gap between rhetoric and reality. Last year, he vowed visible progress soon. Months later, HSRA CEO Ian Choudri promised track-laying would begin "next year." When neither materialized, the authority quietly adjusted expectations. The Draft 2026 Business Plan contains only a vague three-and-a-half-year timeline starting in July for "Track & Systems Design & Construction" of the first Central Valley section—with no hard deadline for actual track laying. The move provides convenient cover should the agency miss another deadline, a pattern that has repeated for over a decade. What the mainstream press downplays is the absence of any credible path forward.

What Else We Know

The project has undergone repeated design revisions and cost recalculations so severe that the current iteration bears little resemblance to what voters authorized. Trip times have lengthened. Ridership projections have faced mounting skepticism. Even a neighboring state's editorial board recently questioned whether "there has ever been a greater fraud perpetrated on the taxpayers than California's high-speed rail travesty," following a "60 Minutes" report. For ordinary Californians, this matters far beyond abstract budget debates. Billions committed to a perpetually delayed rail project are billions unavailable for schools, roads, housing, or water infrastructure.
