Canada’s Bill C-22 Is a Repackaged Version of Last Year’s Surveillance Nightmare

What they're not telling you: # Canada's Bill C-22 Demands Companies Store a Year of User Metadata While Banning Public Disclosure of Surveillance Orders Bill C-22, formally titled The Lawful Access Act and currently before the Canadian Parliament, requires digital service providers—including telecommunications companies, messaging applications, and internet platforms—to record and retain metadata on all users for a minimum of twelve months, according to the Electronic Frontier Foundation's analysis of the legislation. This represents a direct continuation of last year's failed Bill C-2, which the Canadian government withdrew from committee consideration after sustained opposition from the privacy advocacy community. The Ministry of Public Safety, the primary agency driving C-22, has made tactical revisions to language around "systemic vulnerabilities" but preserved the core surveillance architecture that civil liberties organizations found unacceptable.

What the Documents Show

The bill's metadata retention mandate alone affects millions of Canadian citizens and creates an unprecedented centralized archive of communication patterns, location data inferred from connection timing, and contact networks. The legislation grants the Minister of Public Safety unilateral authority to compel digital service providers to engineer backdoors into encrypted systems, provided these mandates do not introduce what the bill defines as a "systemic vulnerability." Here the legislative language becomes deliberately ambiguous: C-22 does not clearly delineate what constitutes either a systemic vulnerability or encryption itself. This textual imprecision creates administrative space for the ministry to demand circumvention of encryption protections under the argument that specific backdoor implementations remain technically contained. The bill simultaneously prohibits companies from publicly disclosing the existence of these ministerial orders, preventing the transparency necessary for independent technical audits or public accountability mechanisms. The metadata retention requirement compounds this structural problem.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

By mandating year-long storage of communication metadata across digital platforms, the legislation creates a consolidated target for unauthorized access. Metadata—which documents whom users contact, when contact occurs, and geographical patterns inferred from connection data—reveals intimate details of association, ideology, and movement. The bill's architects frame this as a border security mechanism, but the surveillance infrastructure itself contains no technical limitations restricting its application to border-related investigations. Canadian government officials have publicly stated their position that surveillance backdoors can be implemented without introducing systemic vulnerabilities. This claim contradicts established cryptographic principles: any engineered access mechanism that permits law enforcement to decrypt communications creates a security weakness exploitable by actors beyond the intended government user. The distinction between targeted access and systemic weakness exists only in legislative language, not in the actual technical architecture of encrypted systems.

What Else We Know

A backdoor, by definition, cannot discriminate between authorized and unauthorized access attempts. The bill's information-sharing provisions expand data exchange with foreign governments, including the United States, without establishing equivalent privacy protections in receiving jurisdictions. This represents a unidirectional weakening of Canadian privacy standards through international data transfer agreements. --- THE TAKE --- I find it striking that surveillance legislation advances through technical obfuscation rather than transparent policy debate. The pattern here is consistent across jurisdictions: legislators draft statutes with undefined terms, hand enforcement authority to executive agencies, and then rely on that definitional ambiguity to expand application beyond the stated purpose. What this reveals about institutional failure is simple: parliamentary oversight of surveillance expansion has become rhetorical rather than substantive.
