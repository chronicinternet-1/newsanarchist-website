    It’s easier for Californians to escape data brokers following a Markup investigation

    The Markup and CalMatters showed how website code could make it harder for Californians to exercise their right to remove personal data. Now much of that code has disappeared.
    Sam Okafor
    Former assistant district attorney
    May 22, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: The Hidden Delete Button: How Data Brokers Buried Californians' Legal Rights—Until Journalists Found Them California law gave residents a right that most didn't know they couldn't exercise: the ability to demand that data brokers delete their personal information. For years, dozens of companies made that right theoretical rather than actual by hiding the deletion pages behind invisible web code. Between 2024 and 2025, The Markup and CalMatters conducted parallel investigations that exposed a systematic obstruction.

    What the Documents Show

    Reporters discovered that 35 data brokers—companies legally required to register with the state and provide deletion mechanisms—had embedded "no-index" code on their deletion request pages. This code instructed search engines like Google not to catalog those pages, rendering them invisible to the very Californians the law was designed to protect. The technical barrier was deliberate. By making deletion pages unsearchable, companies ensured that most people would never find them, effectively nullifying statutory rights without technically violating them. The investigation named specific offenders: IQVIA Digital, Comscore, Telesign Corporation, 6sense Insights, and others operated what amounted to a coordinated dark pattern—a term engineers use for interface design intended to manipulate user behavior.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    These weren't small operators. IQVIA Digital, owned by pharmaceutical giant IQVIA Holdings Inc., and Comscore, a publicly traded data analytics firm, represent the institutional scale of the obstruction. Their deliberate use of invisible code suggested not incompetence but strategy: compliance theater masquerading as compliance. What followed revealed the mechanics of institutional pressure. New Hampshire Democratic Senator Maggie Hassan, then ranking member of the Senate Joint Economic Committee, sent formal letters to five major data brokers requesting explanation for their practices. The committee's investigation provided what regulatory enforcement had not: public scrutiny and congressional attention.

    What Else We Know

    Within weeks of Hassan's inquiry, four of the five companies—IQVIA Digital, Comscore, Telesign Corporation, and 6sense Insights—agreed to remove the blocking code and make their deletion pages searchable. The Markup and CalMatters' follow-up review in May 2026 documented the results: of 35 brokers originally using no-index code, only eight continued the practice. The technical obstruction had been partially dismantled not through California regulatory action, but through investigative journalism and Senate pressure. Twelve companies had already removed the code voluntarily after initial publication. The pattern is clear: without external accountability, California's statutory right to deletion remained a fiction. With it, corporate behavior shifted rapidly.

    The Sam Okafor Take
    True Crime & Justice

    This is what institutional failure looks like when it wears the mask of privacy protection: laws exist but aren't enforced, rights are granted but remain inaccessible, and it takes investigative journalism to force minor compliance from corporations that should have feared their state regulator.

    I find striking the speed at which these companies changed behavior once faced with Congressional scrutiny and public naming. They claim they couldn't comply before. They didn't. But they could when visibility became costly. This reveals the actual hierarchy of power: private companies respond to reputational pressure and legislative attention far more readily than to statutory obligation. The Attorney General's office apparently lacked either the resources, the priority, or the institutional will to enforce the CCPA/CPRA as written.

    The pattern here is recursive across data privacy enforcement: legislation creates rights, companies ignore them, journalists expose violations, politicians ask questions, and companies make minimal changes to escape scrutiny. Meanwhile, the regulatory apparatus sits dormant. The people who benefit are the data brokers themselves—they get years of illegal compliance evasion followed by temporary fixes that satisfy political pressure without fundamental structural change.

    What readers should understand: California's privacy laws are only as strong as the will of elected officials to enforce them. Watch whether the eight remaining data brokers face legal action. Demand that your Attorney General's office publish enforcement metrics. Understand that your statutory rights depend on journalists doing the state's job.
