PURSUE's 384 UAP Files: Historic Dump or Disclosure Theater?

The Pentagon has released 384 declassified UFO files across two tranches in May 2026 — but scientists, veterans of the field, and the government's own investigators agree: none of it confirms anything beyond what AARO already told us.

Casey North AI-ASSISTED June 1, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

In the span of three weeks, the federal government did something it had never done before: it dropped hundreds of classified UFO files onto a public website and told the American people to go look for themselves. The portal is called PURSUE — the Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters — and it lives at war.gov/UFO, a URL that still sounds like a fever dream. As of today, June 2, 2026, the archive holds 384 files and a third tranche is reportedly in active production, with a projected release window of June 7.

The story begins in February 2026, when President Trump posted on Truth Social directing the Department of War — yes, the Pentagon was formally renamed — and other federal agencies to identify and declassify records related to UAP and extraterrestrial life. That directive materialized into the PURSUE program, an interagency effort coordinating the White House, ODNI, the Department of Energy, NASA, the FBI, the State Department, and AARO, the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office.

The first tranche hit on May 8, 2026: 162 files — 120 PDFs, 28 videos, and 14 images — drawn from four agencies. Eighty-two came from the Pentagon, 56 from the FBI, 12 from NASA, and eight from the State Department. The site logged 340 million hits within its first 12 hours. Then on May 22, a second release added 222 more files — 51 videos, 7 audio recordings, and 6 PDFs — totaling 5.6 gigabytes of footage.

What's in there? The range is deliberately eclectic, spanning from 1944 to 2026. The NASA files include Apollo 12 and Apollo 17 mission imagery annotated with highlighted "areas of interest" — triangular formations of lights above the lunar surface — that NASA itself acknowledges were already in the public domain. The FBI contribution pulls from case file 62-HQ-83894, covering UFO incidents from 1947 through 1968, including updated Roswell-era documents with fewer redactions than earlier public versions. More recent material includes mission reports from U.S. Central Command, European Command, and Indo-Pacific Command: an infrared clip of a football-shaped object near Japan in 2024, a six-second Syria video from October 2024, and a 2023 encounter over Greece where an unidentified object reportedly executed multiple 90-degree turns at approximately 80 miles per hour.

Then there is what the Pentagon is calling "among the most compelling" cases it holds: a 2023 two-day incident in the western United States in which federal law enforcement officers independently reported orbs, with one witness describing "orbs launching other orbs." The second tranche added a striking infrared video of four objects in coordinated formation over water near Iran in 2022, captured by U.S. military systems. Officials have not publicly identified the objects.

And then there is the second tranche's most politically-charged item: a video described as showing a UFO being shot down. The Pentagon has provided no additional context. That single line has been circulating on social media for over a week with essentially zero official elaboration.

Here is where things get genuinely complicated. AARO, the office statutorily responsible for analyzing these cases, has maintained throughout its existence — and through this entire release — that it has found no evidence to confirm any UAP cases involve extraterrestrial activity or technology. By February 2026, Pentagon spokesperson Sue Gough confirmed AARO was examining over 2,000 UAP cases, with roughly 1,000 lacking sufficient data for analysis and sitting in an "Active Archive." The caseload had grown by at least 400 since the office's last public update in late 2024. That's bureaucratic growth, not breakthrough.

Scientists reviewing the initial tranche have been blunt. Astrophysicist David Whitehouse, a former BBC science journalist, reviewed the materials and concluded that the footage showed "optical artefacts, fuzzy blobs, light smears" and what appeared to be balloons, with "no hint, no evidence whatsoever of anything artificial and alien." Michael Narlock, an astronomer at the Cranbrook Institute of Science, said the documents largely contained eyewitness transcripts, which were "notoriously unreliable," while the videos lacked sufficient context to assess. Seth Shostak, senior astronomer at the SETI Institute, told CBS News that "there is no compelling evidence for extraterrestrial life thus far."

Experts in the UAP research community have also flagged structural problems with the archive itself. At least 100 of the initial 162 files contain redactions, according to researchers currently studying them. The War Zone noted that some footage flagged as unresolved appears to show known infrared camera artifacts — thermal blooms from jet engines that produce elongated, pill-shaped signatures that can fool operators. John Greenewald Jr., who runs The Black Vault and has spent decades filing FOIA requests for this exact category of material, pointed out a particularly sharp irony: the government, which for years claimed it could not release UAP imagery without compromising sensitive sources and methods, had just demonstrated that it absolutely could redact and downgrade sensitive imagery for public release. The criticism is pointed — if they can release this, what is the justification for everything they're still holding back?

The War Department acknowledged this tension directly in its documentation, noting that "the unresolved status of the published cases frequently reflects insufficient data rather than evidence of anomalous capability," and explicitly inviting private-sector analysis of the material. That is an unusual posture for a national security agency — essentially crowdsourcing the resolution of its own cold cases.

So what is PURSUE, structurally? It is the first centralized, no-clearance-required government UAP archive in American history. It consolidates material that had previously moved through fragmented FOIA requests, congressional letters, and unauthorized leaks. That is genuinely significant for researchers. But the archive's provenance is political. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said the documents had "long fueled justified speculation." Rep. Tim Burchett of Tennessee, an outspoken disclosure advocate, teased the initial May 8 release with claims that a "holy crap" moment was coming. It did not come in the first two tranches. A third release, reportedly in active production as of this writing, is expected within days.

Let me be direct: PURSUE is the most important structural development in UAP transparency in decades, and it is also, at this stage, a performance. Both things are true. The portal's existence — a centralized, freely accessible government archive where anyone can cross-reference raw sensor data against official conclusions — is a genuine and durable change to how this topic gets studied. That matters. What doesn't hold up is the surrounding theater. Trump posts AI-generated images of himself walking handcuffed aliens to military bases. A senior congressman hints at a "holy crap" moment that never arrives. Defense Secretary Hegseth frames redacted infrared blobs as evidence of unprecedented transparency. The actual files, reviewed honestly, tell a familiar story: AARO has over 2,000 cases, roughly half lack sufficient data to resolve, and not one has confirmed non-human technology. If nothing changes — if future tranches continue to be heavy on Apollo-era curiosities and short infrared clips with no analytical context — then the winner is the administration's transparency brand, not the public's understanding of what is in the sky. The press office wins. AARO wins. The disclosure community gets to claim progress. And the genuinely hard question — what are those 21 cases AARO's own director said he cannot explain — stays buried.
