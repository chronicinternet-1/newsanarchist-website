Trump's NDA Plan

Trump wants federal employees to sign NDAs, sparking whistleblower protection concerns

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist specializing in FOIA litigation
June 16, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

The Trump administration's plan to require all federal employees to sign non-disclosure agreements has raised concerns among transparency advocates and whistleblowers. This move is seen as an attempt to restrict the flow of information and silence potential whistleblowers. Daniel Ellsberg, the renowned whistleblower who leaked the Pentagon Papers in 1971, had spoken out against such measures before his death in 2023 at the age of 92. Ellsberg's courageous act of leaking classified information had exposed the government's deceit about the Vietnam War, and his legacy continues to inspire whistleblowers today.

The proposed NDA plan has been criticized for its potential to chill speech and deter federal employees from reporting waste, fraud, or abuse. While the order claims to exempt legally protected whistleblower complaints, the administration's track record on weakening whistleblower protections has raised suspicions. The rules already in place restrict federal employees from disclosing classified information and sensitive data, but the new NDA policy could have a broader impact on transparency and accountability. On June 16, 1963, Valentina Tereshkova became the first woman in space, marking a historic milestone in space exploration, but today, the focus is on the Trump administration's efforts to restrict information flow.

Whistleblower advocacy groups have expressed concerns that the NDA plan could be used to silence employees who report misconduct or wrongdoing. The case of Shane Cuthbert, who was arrested and charged with breaching a suppression order in Queensland, Australia, highlights the risks faced by whistleblowers. Cuthbert had exposed the name of a high-profile individual accused of cheating on his wife, and his arrest has raised questions about the limits of free speech and the protection of whistleblowers. In the United States, the Whistleblower Aid organization has been working to support whistleblowers, including those who have reported sensitive labor data, such as the DOGE whistleblower who claimed that his brake line was cut after Elon Musk posted about him on social media.

The Trump administration's NDA plan has been met with resistance from transparency advocates, who argue that it could undermine the public's right to know and hold government officials accountable. The plan's potential impact on whistleblowers and the flow of information is still being debated, but one thing is clear: the administration's efforts to restrict transparency and silence potential whistleblowers will be closely watched by the public and the media. With the existing rules already restricting federal employees from disclosing classified information, the new NDA policy could have far-reaching consequences for transparency and accountability in government.
