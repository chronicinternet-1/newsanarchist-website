A Reddit user recently asked a simple question: how do ordinary people keep their financial information private? The question itself reveals the core scandal.

What the Documents Show

We've reached a point where financial privacy requires deliberate, sophisticated effort—not because technology makes it impossible, but because the regulatory structure has been systematically dismantled to serve data aggregators, financial institutions, and the surveillance apparatus built around them. The baseline answer the Reddit user intuited is correct: cash transactions remain the most straightforward privacy tool. But here's what the mainstream financial press won't tell you—the infrastructure that made cash-based privacy feasible is actively being eroded. The Federal Reserve's push toward digital payments, combined with Bank Secrecy Act requirements that force financial institutions to report transactions over $10,000, creates a surveillance net that catches ordinary commerce. But notice who profits: payment processors like Visa and Mastercard (combined 2023 revenues: $85 billion), which capture 2-3% of every digitized transaction while simultaneously selling behavioral data to hedge funds and data brokers.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 promised financial privacy protections. Instead, it created the conditions for the modern financial data industry. Banks can share your information with "affiliated companies" and "service providers"—categories so broadly defined that your mortgage lender can sell your spending patterns to insurance companies. The Federal Trade Commission, theoretically charged with enforcing privacy rules, has a budget of approximately $420 million annually to oversee an economy generating $28 trillion in GDP. That's one part regulator to 67 million parts market. What regulatory capacity exists is concentrated on supervision of the largest institutions—JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup—whose compliance budgets dwarf the entire FTC appropriation.

What Else We Know

Smaller banks and fintech companies face lighter scrutiny, which means the most aggressive data harvesting often happens in the least-monitored sectors. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, created after 2008 with a mandate to protect consumer financial privacy, operates with a budget of $650 million, a fraction of what it needs to examine the 5,200+ banks and 200,000+ mortgage companies operating in the United States. The data broker industry—including companies like Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion—operates with almost no transparency. These three firms control credit reporting data on approximately 220 million Americans but face minimal disclosure requirements about what information they collect, how they sell it, or who buys it. A 2023 Senate investigation found that data brokers sold financial information to scammers, yet enforcement actions remained anemic. Because the payment processors, fintech companies, and financial services firms that benefit from unrestricted data flows have spent more than $2.8 billion on lobbying over the past decade, according to OpenSecrets analysis.
