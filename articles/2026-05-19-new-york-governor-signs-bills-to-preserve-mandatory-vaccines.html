New York Governor Signs Bills To Preserve Mandatory Vaccines

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist
May 19, 2026 · 4 min read

What they're not telling you: # New York Governor Bypasses Federal Health Authority To Lock In Vaccine Mandates Governor Kathy Hochul has just handed New York's health commissioner unilateral power to mandate vaccines without federal approval—a structural shift that decouples state requirements from federal oversight and centers authority in a single appointed official. On May 15, Hochul signed Assembly Bills 10711 and 10710, legislation that fundamentally restructures how vaccines enter New York's mandatory schedule. The critical language in AB 10711 removes the requirement that vaccines be "approved by federal regulators," replacing it with a standard that children must receive vaccines "in accordance with regulations issued by the commissioner of health of New York." James McDonald, New York's health commissioner, an appointed position answerable to the governor. AB 10710 creates a parallel mandate for insurance coverage. Health insurers must now cover whatever vaccines New York's health commissioner recommends, regardless of CDC guidance. This means a single state official, McDonald, can now compel both medical administration and insurance reimbursement without the intermediate check of federal scientific consensus. Hochul's stated rationale rests on opposing Trump administration vaccine policy changes.

Follow the Money

She claimed the legislation "protects access to lifesaving vaccines" and characterized the action as New York "fighting back" against an "anti-science administration." McDonald echoed this framing, invoking "trusted medical guidance" and "misinformation." But the legislation's actual mechanism does not preserve existing federal standards—it replaces them. Before these bills, New York aligned with CDC recommendations. After, New York's requirements are whatever McDonald decides. The bills were explicitly authored to function when federal recommendations narrow. They activate precisely when federal guidance contracts, meaning they're designed to override federal authority during moments of policy disagreement, not to preserve some stable scientific baseline. The thrust is institutional: Hochul has centralized vaccine mandate authority in her health commissioner while severing the requirement that federal scientific review validate those mandates.

What Else We Know

This creates a structure where political opposition to federal policy can be converted directly into state medical mandates without the intermediate friction of federal approval or the transparency of CDC recommendation processes. What's striking is the framing obscures the actual power transfer. The language about "fighting back" and protecting "lifesaving vaccines" presents this as defensive—a preservation of status quo science. It is an expansion of state executive authority over medical requirements, with the governor's appointed health commissioner now functioning as the final arbiter of what counts as necessary medical intervention for children attending school. The bills passed during a moment when federal vaccine policy was contracting under Trump administration review. They were explicitly designed to function as a counterweight.

What I find striking is how completely the official narrative inverts the actual power move. Hochul's office has dressed up an expansion of executive authority as a defense of settled science, when the legislation explicitly activates when scientific consensus fractures between federal and state levels.

The pattern here is institutional: Democratic officials are using popular opposition to specific Trump policies as political cover to consolidate medical authority at the state executive level. This works rhetorically right now because most readers trust New York's health commissioner more than Trump appointees. But the structure they're building—a governor empowered to impose medical mandates through an appointed health official, decoupled from federal review—is exactly the kind of unchecked executive power that becomes dangerous when wielded by someone you don't trust.

What you should watch: which governors copy this model, and whether future legislatures have the spine to restore federal scientific review as a check on state executive medical authority. Until then, medical mandates in New York depend entirely on trusting that whoever controls the governor's office will appoint a trustworthy health commissioner. That's not oversight. That's faith.
