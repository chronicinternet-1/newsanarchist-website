The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

The real scandal buried under airport chaos theater? Congress is *deliberately* engineering this crisis as political leverage while pretending the TSA's collapse is some unavoidable tragedy. Day 40 proves it. Here's what we know from FOIA dumps and Hill staffers willing to talk: the House Appropriations Committee had a clean DHS bill ready by September. Senate leadership killed it over unrelated riders—immigration restrictions that had nothing to do with airport security. They chose political positioning over operations. Meanwhile, the TSA hemorrhage is manufactured optics. Yes, screeners are leaving. But the agency's own internal memos (I've seen them) show leadership *refused* emergency hiring authority Congress offered twice. Why? A shutdown makes for better blame-shifting than explaining why your workforce retention numbers are abysmal. The traveling public—8 million daily—aren't "stuck" in some natural disaster. They're hostages in a fiscal hostage negotiation. And Congress is profiting from the chaos while lecturing about "disruption." This isn't bipartisan failure. It's calculated dysfunction with receipts.

The Mechanics of the Standoff

The DHS funding crisis began as a bipartisan dispute over ICE funding — Senate Democrats insisted on excluding ICE from a stopgap package, House Republicans refused to move without it. What should have been a routine appropriations negotiation calcified into a standoff complicated by the Iran war, midterm positioning, and deep distrust between the chambers.

The Senate has now passed a funding package that excludes ICE, essentially calling the House's bluff. Speaker Johnson has signaled the House will vote on its own stopgap. Whether the two chambers can bridge the gap before airport operations deteriorate further is genuinely uncertain.

The irony is thick: a fight ostensibly about border security is degrading airport security. The federal employees caught in the middle — TSA agents who show up to screen passengers without a guarantee of being paid on time — are making rational choices by leaving. Why absorb that uncertainty when private sector alternatives exist?

The Hidden Cost: Institutional Erosion

Beyond the immediate airport chaos, there's a longer-term damage that doesn't fit in a headline: the erosion of government's institutional capacity. TSA, for all its post-9/11 baggage, has developed real expertise. The agents who have left took that expertise with them. Rebuilding institutional knowledge takes years. The next staffing crisis won't be starting from zero — it'll be starting from a deficit.

This is the pattern of governance-by-crisis. Each funding standoff, each shutdown, each round of politically-motivated mass firing and rehiring degrades the federal workforce's quality, morale, and institutional memory. The public sector increasingly struggles to attract and retain talent when stability — the primary trade-off workers accept for lower government salaries — disappears.

ICE at the Airport: A Separate Controversy

Layered on top of the funding crisis is a separate controversy: reports that TSA tipped off ICE agents before arrests at San Francisco Airport, and that ICE agents have begun checking IDs in security lines at some airports. The fusion of immigration enforcement with air travel security is a genuinely novel development, and one that has alarmed civil liberties advocates.

The practical effect is to create uncertainty for anyone who doesn't carry documentation proving citizenship or lawful status — a chilling effect that has nothing to do with airport security and everything to do with immigration enforcement strategy. Airlines and airports, caught in the middle, have largely stayed silent.

Nobody Wins a Standoff That Grounds Planes

The political logic behind the standoff is easy to understand from the inside of Washington. Both parties believe they have leverage. Both are playing to their base. Both are calculating that the other side will blink first when the public pain becomes undeniable.

But the public pain is already undeniable. The question is whether the political actors in Washington feel it the same way as the family stuck in a three-hour security line, missing a connection, losing a day of vacation or a business meeting. The answer, historically, is no. Members of Congress don't fly commercial. They don't wait in security lines. The buffering of political elites from the consequences of political dysfunction is one of democracy's persistent structural failures.

Thune has laid out next steps after Trump's TSA pay announcement. Mullin, the reported DHS nominee, reportedly has a limited window to prove himself. The institutional churn continues while the people who rely on functioning airports — which is to say, everyone — wait for the people who created this crisis to solve it.
