Grusch Goes Public at Capitol: Government Knows About Multiple Alien Species

UFO whistleblower David Grusch told lawmakers on Capitol steps today the U.S. government is aware of several kinds of extraterrestrial life—and demanded Trump-era transparency action to prove it.

Casey North AI-ASSISTED June 9, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

On the steps of the U.S. Capitol Tuesday afternoon, former Air Force intelligence officer David Grusch made his most direct public claim yet: the federal government possesses knowledge of multiple extraterrestrial species with varying levels of complexity.

In a press conference at 1 p.m. EST, Grusch stood alongside a bipartisan congressional coalition—Reps. Anna Paulina Luna (R-Fla.), Tim Burchett (R-Tenn.), Eric Burlison (R-Mo.), and Jared Moskowitz (D-Fla.)—to demand immediate release of classified UAP records and immunity protections for future whistleblowers.

"Political appointees have not complied with the disclosure law," Grusch told the assembled press, describing what amounts to institutional obstruction. He framed the conversation beyond mere extraterrestrial intrigue: "The topics that we're discussing here today go beyond life in the universe." His emphasis on national security implications suggests Grusch views the secrecy question as a genuine Pentagon governance crisis, not just a conspiracy curiosity.

The Capitol gathering marks a significant escalation in the disclosure movement's political pressure. Since Grusch first testified under oath to Congress in July 2023—alleging the government recovered non-human craft and operated black-budget reverse-engineering programs—the narrative has hardened from "he said" to "prove us wrong." Today's event represents the first coordinated push by multiple sitting members of Congress to weaponize whistleblower credibility against administrative claims of transparency.

Lawmakers specifically demanded declassification of records related to the 1996 Varginha incident in Brazil, where witnesses reported contact with non-human beings allegedly transported to the United States. The specificity of the request matters: it's no longer abstract demands for "UFO files." These are named incidents with cross-border implications that implicate State Department knowledge.

Rep. Luna requested White House immunity grants for whistleblowers coming forward with information about UFO locations and advanced technology. Rep. Burlison called on President Trump directly to waive nondisclosure agreements for all UFO whistleblowers. The phrasing is calculated: they're asking Trump to use executive power to overrule Pentagon gatekeeping—a potential constitutional showdown between civilian and military authority over secrecy protocols.

"Disclosure today, disclosure tomorrow," Moskowitz declared, signaling a refusal to accept delays. "The American people deserve to know."

Burchett's rhetoric was sharper. When asked about congressional access to Area 51, he responded that if lawmakers toured the facility, "all they would get is a T-shirt because the government would move everything else." The barb implicitly accuses the Defense Department of literal obstruction of Congressional oversight—a serious allegation that hasn't been answered.

What distinguishes today's Capitol press conference from previous UFO disclosure theater: the explicit framing of this as a Pentagon budget and accountability question. Lawmakers emphasized repeatedly that the issue isn't merely whether aliens exist, but whether the Pentagon is spending classified money while dodging Congressional oversight. That's a governance scandal separate from the extraterrestrial question.

Grusch's credibility remains contested. In March 2024, AARO (the official government body tasked with investigating UAPs) issued a comprehensive review concluding it found "no empirical evidence" that the U.S. government possesses alien technology or operates hidden reverse-engineering programs. Grusch's allegations rest primarily on claimed documents and conversations with unnamed sources—what critics call "hearsay at scale." NASA's independent study team on UAPs has noted that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and material evidence has yet materialized.

But the political environment has shifted. The Trump administration has already begun releasing previously classified UAP documents through the Department of War's new "PURSUE" system (Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters). The first two batches have included 51 videos of grainy infrared footage and declassified FBI case files dating back to the 1940s. Pentagon officials acknowledged that many materials "lack a substantiated chain-of-custody," but the release itself signals willingness to breach what was previously untouchable.

The most striking document released so far: an account from a serving senior intelligence officer who reported close UAP encounters in late 2025 while investigating sightings aboard a military helicopter. The officer described "countless orange orbs swarming in all directions" and separate encounters with "two large orbs" hovering near the aircraft. Whether classified because of genuine mystery or because of classified sensor technology remains unclear.

Today's Capitol event is the disclosure movement's attempt to convert Trump's document-release momentum into something more: actual congressional power to compel testimony and access. If successful, it could force AARO to conduct a new investigation under pressure to answer specific questions about specific incidents. If it fails—if Trump doesn't grant immunity or Cabinet-level obstruction continues—the movement faces its hardest test yet: proving that whistleblower credibility matters when institutional power refuses to play along.

The Casey North Take

Here's what troubles me: we're now three years into the Grusch disclosure cycle, and the evidence matrix hasn't changed. We have testimony. We have video that could be many things. We have zero artifacts, zero biological remains, zero propellant analysis, zero anything that survives independent verification. Pentagon AARO has explicitly said no alien tech exists in government hands. Yet the political pressure is undeniably real, which means either: (A) the government is lying at the institutional level, with only scattered whistleblowers breaking rank, or (B) Grusch is describing a genuine mystery that feels like alien tech because it's classified military hardware we don't recognize. The Capitol press conference today doesn't resolve that. What it does is shift the burden from whistleblowers to prove their case toward Congress to prove the government isn't hiding something. That's not nothing politically. But it's not evidence. If Trump grants immunity and no major revelations follow—if the sealed records turn out mundane—the credibility collapse will be total. The movement wins by demanding disclosure, but loses the moment disclosure happens.
