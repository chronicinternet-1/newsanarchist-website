    What the Documents Show

    1318 (the Fallen Servicemembers Religious Heritage Restoration Act), sought to require judicial approval before the National Security Agency and FBI could access raw FISA-collected communications containing US person identifiers. The amendments also mandated that the Director of National Intelligence report quarterly to Congress on the number of Americans whose communications were accessed under Section 702 of the FISA Amendments Act—data the intelligence community has historically withheld or reported only in aggregate form that obscures actual surveillance volume. According to the source material, the bill's actual legislative text concerns military burial site religious ceremonies and contains no surveillance provisions whatsoever. The accountability amendments were subsidiary measures, which allowed the Senate to proceed without formal debate on the intelligence authorities themselves. This procedural maneuver—attaching oversight language to unrelated legislation—is standard practice when surveillance bills face anticipated opposition.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    The Senate killed both amendments through what the source identifies as parliamentary rejection. The rejection preserves the current Section 702 framework, which permits NSA and FBI personnel to query communications collected from foreign intelligence targets without demonstrating that those queries will reveal information about foreign intelligence threats. The practical effect: any employee with FISA access credentials can retrieve Americans' emails, messaging applications, and phone calls if they believe the subject of the query has any connection to a foreign power. The standard is "about" collection, not "to or from" collection, meaning communications discussing foreign intelligence matters are fair game even if the American participant has no direct involvement in intelligence activities. This is not theoretical constraint. The NSA's own Office of Inspector General disclosed in 2013 that over 3,000 queries of Section 702 data violated internal querying standards in a single year.

    What Else We Know

    By 2019, the FBI was conducting tens of thousands of queries monthly. No public data has been released since then. The rejected amendments would have provided Congress—and by extension, the public through FOIA—concrete numbers on access patterns, something the intelligence agencies have successfully prevented for over two decades. The rejection also preserves a second structural advantage for surveillance authorities: there is currently no mechanism requiring judicial review when NSA or FBI officials access raw intercepts. The amendments would have required at least a showing of probable cause or reasonable suspicion before Americans' communications could be reviewed in full text. Under existing rules, metadata and preliminary database searches require no judicial authorization whatsoever.
