Illinois Makes Historic Push to Compensate the Wrongly Convicted

After decades of leaving exonerated prisoners to rebuild lives without recognition, Illinois courts and lawmakers have just fundamentally transformed how the state acknowledges—and pays for—innocent years stolen by conviction.

Sam Okafor AI-ASSISTED June 11, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The momentum was unmistakable. In late May, the First District Illinois Appellate Court handed down a ruling that will reshape how exonerated people reclaim their records and their dignity. Then, within days, the state legislature passed new compensation legislation. What looks like ordinary legislative rhythm is actually a profound rebuke to a system that has warehoused the innocent with barely a nod to their pain.

The court ruling centered on a deceptively technical question: Who automatically qualifies for a certificate of innocence? These documents matter enormously—they come with state compensation, the right to expunge criminal records, and something harder to quantify but just as precious: official acknowledgment that the system got it catastrophically wrong.

Cook County prosecutors had been arguing that only people whom a judge had explicitly pronounced "actually innocent" deserved automatic certificates. Most judges never issue such declarations. They simply overturn convictions. The appellate court rejected this Catch-22 logic, holding that people whose convictions were overturned should get certificates without having to persuade a judge to use magic words.

Josh Tepfer, executive director of the Exoneration Project and one of the lawyers fighting for exonerees, didn't mince words: "I have never seen it happen," he said of judges issuing actual-innocence declarations. "This is a clearly correct result."

Within days, attorneys filed motions in at least two additional cases arguing the ruling meant their clients were immediately owed certificates. As of early June, prosecutors hadn't responded. State's Attorney Eileen O'Neill Burke could still appeal to the Illinois Supreme Court, but the momentum is shifting.

One client, identified by court records as "Drew," illustrates exactly why this matters. He got locked up at 18 years old, wrongfully convicted of a 1996 murder. Twenty-five years later, prosecutors dropped the charges and released him. But without an expungement, a criminal record remained—a ghost that could haunt him at traffic stops, job interviews, housing applications. He's 46 now. "At 46, I have no children. You took that from me, man," he said through tears about the prosecutors who convicted him. "It's not fair. It cost me 25 years out of my life."

The legislature's companion move addresses compensation directly. Illinois has paid wrongfully convicted people, but the amounts have been inadequate relative to the theft of freedom. The new bill raises those payments. While specific figures weren't available in early reporting, the legislative action signals clear recognition: The state's coffers are finite, but injustice demands accounting.

This matters nationally. Illinois holds a grim distinction: Since 1989, the state has documented 579 exonerations—more than any other state in America. That's not because Illinois juries are uniquely trigger-happy or evidence uniquely flimsy. It's because the state pioneered the use of conviction integrity units, organizations dedicated to investigating and overturning wrongful convictions. Cook County and other Illinois jurisdictions created the conditions where the innocent could be found. And they found thousands of them.

But finding innocence and compensating it are different projects. For decades, exonerated people walked out of prison with bus fare and a pat on the shoulder. Some states don't compensate at all. In Pennsylvania, one of the oldest and most active exoneration jurisdictions, "no compensation law exists at all." Illinois is changing that calculus.

The Exoneration Project filed those motions within days of the appellate ruling. That tells you something about the legal ecosystem: organizations exist solely to fight these battles, and they move fast when courts open doors. But they're chronically underfunded and understaffed. Every exoneree they help means resources not available for the next client, the next appeal, the next certificate denied.

State's Attorney Burke's Conviction Integrity Unit has not exonerated a single person in her first 10 months in office, according to reporting by Injustice Watch. She has also done little to confront more than a dozen coercion allegations against a former Chicago police detective and his partners. That institutional resistance remains the hardest barrier—prosecutors defending the convictions that put innocent people away, resisting accountability, fighting certificates and expungements at every step.

The Illinois legislature and courts have now created a vector for change. Certificates will flow more easily. Compensation will be less insulting. But the burden falls on the exonerated themselves and their attorneys to navigate even these improved systems, to file the motions, to argue yet again that the state was wrong.

Illinois has become a national leader in finding the innocent. Now it's taking steps to honor them. That's progress, real and measurable. But every exonerated person who still has to fight for their dignity, every year someone waits for compensation for years stolen, is a reminder that even the system's leaders have far to go.

The Sam Okafor Take
True Crime & Justice

I spent 12 years in the DOJ Appellate Section, watching how hard it is to get courts to simply acknowledge error, let alone compensate for it. Illinois just did both. But here's what kills me: This shouldn't require appeals courts and legislature to move simultaneously just to get basic decency—certificates, money, expungements—flowing to the innocent. If this doesn't happen, who wins? Not the exonerated, still fighting. Not society, which sees institutions stonewalling. The only winner is the prosecution, which gets to avoid accountability while innocent lives stay marginal. Illinois is changing that. Other states should watch and replicate. Because right now, 14 states have no compensation law at all. Fourteen. That's not justice delayed. That's justice denied.
