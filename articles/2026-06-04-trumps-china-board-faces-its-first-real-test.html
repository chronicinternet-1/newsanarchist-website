Trump's China Board Faces Its First Real Test

The Trump administration opened public comments today on the architecture of its new US-China Board of Trade—the centerpiece of May's Beijing summit—exposing a fundamental weakness: nobody yet agrees on what it's actually for.

Elena Vasquez AI-ASSISTED June 4, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

WASHINGTON—Two days ago, the Office of the United States Trade Representative announced a public comment period for what's supposed to be the grand achievement of Trump's China strategy: a US-China Board of Trade that will supposedly manage bilateral commerce on an "ongoing basis."

But here's what you need to know: neither Washington nor Beijing has publicly detailed what this board will actually do, how it will make decisions, what authority its members will wield, or whether either side can be bound by its rulings. The 65-day comment window—closing July 10—is essentially an admission that the summit's grand "historic and landmark" agreement was negotiable but not negotiated.

When Trump met Xi in Beijing three weeks ago, both sides heralded the Board of Trade as "the cornerstone" of a new era of "constructive, strategically stable" US-China relations. The two presidents hammered out a 10-point agreement on agricultural purchases, Boeing aircraft orders, and critical minerals access. The White House fact sheet proclaimed a sweeping realignment.

But the Board itself remained a blank slate.

"High on the list of deliverables agreed following the summit was the establishment of a board of trade, albeit with little detail on its mission, responsibilities or operations," Asia Society Policy Institute vice-president Wendy Cutler noted just last week.

Now USTR is asking the American business community: What should we actually negotiate about? What products should get tariff reductions? How much "balance and reciprocity" is enough? By July 10, US ag exporters, manufacturers, and tech firms will file position papers telling the government which deals they want done. Beijing will watch, file its own comments, and prepare its counter-proposals.

This is, by any honest measure, a Band-Aid on a severed limb.

The architecture reveals the gap between the May summit's rhetorical achievements and the actual state of US-China economic conflict. While the two sides appear to have agreed on terms for the restoration of US tariffs on China, they have not yet agreed on an extension to the one-year trade truce negotiated in October, with Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent saying he believes China would accept restoration of prior tariff rates "as long as they don't go higher." Translation: we're in a holding pattern.

Two Section 301 investigations are set to be concluded this summer. These aren't theoretical—they concern whether China violates US intellectual property protections and fair-trade practices. When those land, the Board of Trade may face its first real crisis.

Moreover, deep-seated divergences over fundamental and systemic issues between the US and China, underpinned by persistent geopolitical and strategic self-interests, are unlikely to disappear on their own, and simply by-passing these issues will not produce lasting stability without genuine and sustained consultation, engagement and careful management of differences on both sides.

The actual negotiating positions haven't budged. Beijing wants the US to ease curbs on exports of chipmaking equipment and advanced semiconductors. Washington wants that leverage—it's a security tool. The summit has again avoided core "fairness" issues related to state-owned enterprises, industrial policy and subsidies. These are the actual bones of contention.

What's real in all this? The purchases. China approved an initial purchase of 200 American-made Boeing aircraft for Chinese airlines, which will drive high-paying, high-skilled U.S. manufacturing jobs. China will purchase at least $17 billion per year of U.S. agricultural products in 2026, 2027, and 2028, in addition to soybean purchase commitments made in October 2025. US exports of sorghum to China reached $260.2 million in value in the first two months of 2026, an increase of over 1,000 percent from the same period in 2025.

Those are tangible wins for American farmers and aerospace workers. Trump's political coalition needed that.

But the Board of Trade is something else: a mechanism to prevent collapse. Trump and Xi shared a new vision of building "a constructive relationship of strategic stability," which while representing a major milestone in their effort to reset bilateral relations, will see uncertainties and tensions persist, with the agreed approach to restore stability being "managed trade" through creation of a board to manage bilateral trade in non-sensitive goods, reduction of tariff and non-tariff barriers in selective sectors, and China's commitments on aircraft and critical minerals.

In other words: quarantine the conflict to non-sensitive goods. Keep it from spiraling. Institutionalize détente.

That's not nothing. It beats 2025's tariff escalation cycle. But it's not a resolution—it's an armistice with an expiration date.

The real test comes when Section 301 conclusions land. When the Board is asked to approve semiconductor deals that Washington's national security apparatus opposes. When Chinese demand for US tariff relief meets Congressional Republicans demanding "toughness" on Beijing. When the two sides discover that "balance and reciprocity" means opposite things in Mandarin and English.

The public comment period buys time. It lets both sides talk to stakeholders and build political cover for whatever compromise emerges. It's skillful process management.

But it also confirms that what Trump and Xi actually agreed to was the *principle* of managing trade through a board—not the details of what that board will do.

The Elena Vasquez Take
Global Power & Geopolitics

Here's my take: Trump declared victory in Beijing because he got the photo op, the Boeing order, and agriculture concessions. Xi got a partner willing to "manage" rather than escalate. Both sides needed an off-ramp from last year's chaos. But the Board of Trade is a placeholder for a deal that hasn't been made. The public comment window is an SOS to the business community: help us figure out what we actually agreed to. If the Section 301 investigations conclude this summer with hostile findings on IP theft or subsidy abuse, the Board's authority will be tested immediately. If semiconductor export controls stay in place, Beijing will see the Board as theater. If US courts continue to constrain Trump's tariff power (as they did in February), the Board loses leverage. Who wins if nothing changes? Both leaderships. They've rhetorically reset relations without addressing the structural competition underneath. They've bought 18-24 months of stability while 2028 elections loom in Washington and China's next economic reckoning looms in Beijing. But stability purchased through institutional vagueness is a house built on sand.
