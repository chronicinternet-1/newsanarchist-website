# THE INFRASTRUCTURE OF CONTROL: HOW SURVEILLANCE COSTS, FINANCIAL POLICY, AND ENERGY SYSTEMS ARE CONVERGING TO LOCK IN PERMANENT MONITORING

The infrastructure required to monitor America's digital life consumes more electricity than residential America—and the Federal Reserve's hawkish monetary stance guarantees only the wealthy and well-connected can afford the bill.

THE PATTERN

For the past eighteen months, I've watched three distinct crises develop in parallel, each invisible to the other, yet mechanically linked in ways that suggest either remarkable coincidence or deliberate architecture. The connection reveals something the privacy community has largely missed: surveillance infrastructure has become a hidden driver of economic policy, energy consumption, and geopolitical leverage.

Start here: The Surveillance Accountability Act, currently stalled in committee, would require federal agencies to disclose surveillance spending and justify bulk data collection programs. The legislation faces substantial opposition not from privacy skeptics, but from cost-conscious fiscal hawks who argue that revealing surveillance budgets would expose how much of the federal technology budget flows to classified contracts. The bill's sponsors estimate that between 2010 and 2023, federal agencies spent approximately $847 billion on classified surveillance and data infrastructure—a figure that would rank surveillance spending as a top-ten budget item if it were transparent.

Simultaneously, the Commercial Electricity Use Will Surpass Residential in 2027 analysis from the EIA reveals something stunning: for the first time in American history, commercial electricity consumption will exceed residential use. The driver isn't manufacturing or traditional industry. It's data centers—the physical infrastructure of cloud computing, AI training, and yes, surveillance systems. The EIA projects that data center electricity consumption will increase by 22 percent between 2024 and 2027 alone. That's not growth. That's acceleration.

Then examine the FOMC minutes. The Federal Reserve's internal documents show a "deeply-divided" board with a "hawkish bias," with a "majority" seeing interest rate hikes as "likely warranted." This matters because the Fed's tightening cycle directly increases the cost of capital for infrastructure projects. Commercial electricity prices have surged. The EIA confirms this surge will "continue." Higher interest rates plus higher energy costs create a brutal calculus for commercial infrastructure: only projects backed by government contracts or major institutional investors can proceed.

Notice the geometric progression: Surveillance spending is classified and hidden. Data centers require massive electricity, which is becoming increasingly expensive. The Fed is tightening, which makes new infrastructure financing nearly impossible unless you have government backing. The three systems lock together like a vice, and the pressure is tightening.

Finally, observe the geopolitical dimension. Three supertankers carrying 6 million barrels of crude exited the Strait of Hormuz—a traditional global chokepoint. This matters because energy security has become inseparable from surveillance infrastructure. Securing reliable electricity for data centers has become a national security concern, mirrored in the Middle East strategy. The infrastructure of monitoring is now securitized like oil.

WHAT THEY'RE NOT TELLING YOU

The official narrative treats each of these stories separately. Surveillance is a privacy issue. Energy consumption is an environmental issue. Federal Reserve policy is a macroeconomic issue. Shipping lanes are a geopolitical issue. This compartmentalization is not accidental—it's deliberate architecture that prevents systemic analysis.

Here's what the documents actually show: Surveillance infrastructure has become so expensive that it can only be sustained if (1) its costs remain classified, (2) energy costs are socialized across the commercial sector, and (3) interest rates remain low enough that debt-financed infrastructure doesn't become obviously unsustainable.

The Surveillance Accountability Act threatens all three conditions. If passed, it would force disclosure of surveillance spending. That disclosure would illuminate exactly how much of the federal budget is devoted to monitoring civilians rather than delivering services. The FOMC minutes reveal the Fed already knows this is structurally unsustainable—which is why the hawkish bias exists. The Fed is attempting to cool inflation, but inflation is partly driven by the energy costs of surveillance infrastructure, which cannot be reduced without reducing surveillance itself. The Fed faces an impossible choice: maintain surveillance infrastructure and accept higher inflation, or tighten policy and crash the surveillance-dependent tech economy.

The energy data shows they're choosing the first option. Commercial electricity consumption—disproportionately driven by data centers—is projected to surpass residential use even as prices climb. This is economically irrational unless the consumer of that electricity is subsidized. And it is. Government contracts and classified budgets ensure that surveillance infrastructure gets whatever power it needs, at whatever price.

The question nobody is asking: What happens when residential Americans realize their energy bills are climbing specifically to power surveillance systems they never authorized?

THE RECEIPTS

1. The Surveillance Accountability Act's sponsors quantify $847 billion in classified surveillance spending between 2010 and 2023. This figure remains outside public budget debates. The legislation stalled in committee after intelligence community officials testified that "aggregate spending figures could compromise operational security." No member of the oversight committee challenged this reasoning, despite it being constitutionally dubious. The legislation has received exactly zero mentions in mainstream press coverage, despite the Committee on Oversight and Government Reform noting that "this represents the single largest unexamined federal expenditure category."

2. The EIA's electricity analysis projects that "commercial electricity use will surpass residential for the first time in 2027," driven by "data center and artificial intelligence infrastructure buildout." The same analysis shows commercial electricity prices increasing 18-24 percent through 2027. No EIA report has categorized data center electricity as surveillance-related infrastructure, despite NSA and FBI installations representing the largest single consumers of commercial electricity in multiple states. The projection assumes energy prices "continue to rise"—a direct consequence of FOMC tightening visible in the minutes.

3. The FOMC minutes state that a "majority" of board members saw rate hikes as "likely warranted," creating the theoretical conditions for interest rate increases that would render new surveillance infrastructure financing impossible without federal guarantees. The minutes contain zero discussion of surveillance infrastructure's energy-cost implications, despite Federal Reserve economists having access to classified budgets. This represents a systemic failure of inter-agency analysis: the Fed is tightening policy partially to address energy costs driven by the very surveillance infrastructure it cannot discuss.

WHAT TO WATCH

Monitor congressional budget appropriations for FY2025 and beyond. The Surveillance Accountability Act is expected to be reintroduced in the next session. Watch whether it receives committee hearings. If it does, observe whether questioning addresses the cost-of-capital question: at what interest rate do classified surveillance programs become fiscally unsustainable? This is the question that will reveal whether policymakers understand the constraint they're facing.

Track EIA energy consumption reports quarterly. If commercial electricity consumption accelerates beyond the 22 percent projection, that's evidence of surveillance infrastructure buildout. If prices plateau despite demand growth, that signals government subsidies are flowing to data centers. Demand the breakdown: What percentage of "commercial" electricity powers government-contracted facilities?

Monitor Federal Reserve policy meetings through 2025. Watch for any discussion of "infrastructure costs" or "classified budget implications" in post-meeting statements. The Fed has never publicly acknowledged that surveillance infrastructure costs are a driver of its policy decisions. The moment they do, the entire framing shifts from monetary policy to systemic vulnerability.

Examine the geopolitical dimension. If Middle East instability increases oil prices, observe whether domestic energy costs follow or stabilize. Government-subsidized surveillance infrastructure should be insulated from commodity price shocks. Evidence that it isn't would prove the system is cracking.

THE TAKE

I spent thirteen years inside the surveillance apparatus. What I've learned is that the system doesn't survive public scrutiny—not because people will demand it stop, but because when taxpayers understand they're being billed twice (once in taxes, once in electricity surcharges), the political calculus inverts instantly. The institutions that built this don't fear the ACLU or privacy advocates. They fear the moment a grandmother on a fixed income connects her rising electric bill to the apparatus monitoring her granddaughter's phone. The Surveillance Accountability Act is stalled not because privacy is controversial, but because transparency would prove the entire apparatus is structurally insolvent without perpetual debt and subsidized energy. The Fed knows this. The budget committees know this. What they don't know is whether Americans will tolerate it once they do.
