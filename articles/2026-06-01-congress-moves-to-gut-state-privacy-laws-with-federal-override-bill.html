Congress Moves to Gut State Privacy Laws With Federal Override Bill

A Republican-only federal privacy bill set for its first Congressional hearing tomorrow would erase 21 state privacy laws — including California's landmark CCPA — strip Americans of the right to sue violators, and hand enforcement to an FTC that has been systematically defunded.

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 1, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The House Subcommittee on Commerce, Manufacturing, and Trade convenes tomorrow morning at 10:15 a.m. EDT in Room 2123 of the Rayburn House Office Building to examine H.R. 8413 — the Securing and Establishing Consumer Uniform Rights and Enforcement over Data Act, or SECURE Data Act. The hearing, announced five days ago by Chairmen Brett Guthrie (R-KY) and Gus Bilirakis (R-FL), is the first formal Congressional examination of what its sponsors are calling the most comprehensive federal privacy proposal in a generation. Civil liberties groups call it something closer to its opposite.

The bill was introduced on April 22, 2026 by Rep. John Joyce (R-PA), the leader of the House Energy and Commerce Data Privacy Working Group. It arrived alongside a companion measure from the House Financial Services Committee — the GUARD Financial Data Act (H.R. 8398) — targeting financial institutions. Together, the two bills are designed to replace the current patchwork of state consumer privacy laws with a single national framework. The working group consulted over 170 stakeholders and received more than 250 written responses over fourteen months before releasing the draft.

The bill's architecture is familiar: data minimization requirements, consumer rights to access and delete personal data, and a national data broker registry administered by the Federal Trade Commission. Companies processing data on more than 200,000 U.S. consumers annually, or those deriving 25 percent or more of their revenue from data sales while processing data on at least 100,000 consumers, would fall under its scope. Small businesses below $25 million in adjusted gross annual revenue are exempt.

None of that is the real story. The real story is Section 15.

Section 15 of the SECURE Data Act contains a preemption clause of near-total scope. It prohibits any state or local government from enacting or enforcing any law that 'relates to' the Act's provisions. The Electronic Frontier Foundation, in a public analysis published May 18, 2026, identified this as the bill's lethal flaw: the provision would kill all 21 state consumer privacy laws passed in the past several years. That includes California's Consumer Privacy Act and its successor the Privacy Rights Act, Virginia's Consumer Data Protection Act, Colorado's Privacy Act, and laws in Connecticut, Texas, Montana, Maryland, Oregon, Indiana, Kentucky, Rhode Island, and more. It would also likely preempt state data breach notification laws across all 50 states — laws that currently form the only enforceable backstop against corporate silence after a hack — as well as state bans on the sale of biometric and geolocation data.

California's Delete Act, which gives residents a single mechanism to instruct data brokers to delete their profiles, would be swept away under the bill's broad preemption language. Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act, the single most litigated consumer privacy statute in American history, faces the same fate. The bill makes federal law not a floor that states can build upon but an absolute ceiling.

This is not a new legislative problem. The 2022 American Data Privacy and Protection Act and the 2024 American Privacy Rights Act both collapsed over the same preemption fault line, with California lawmakers refusing to accept a federal bill that would dilute the CCPA. The SECURE Data Act repeats that pattern, arguably more aggressively, by preempting even the carve-outs those earlier bills preserved. Unlike its predecessors, this bill was drafted entirely by House Republicans without bipartisan input. The IAPP noted this week that the proposal 'is neither bipartisan nor bicameral like predecessor frameworks' — and that it will almost certainly require Democratic Senate votes to reach the president's desk, votes that the preemption language makes structurally unlikely.

The enforcement architecture compounds the problem. The SECURE Data Act contains no private right of action. Consumers who discover their data has been sold, misused, or exposed cannot bring their own claims in court. Enforcement rests entirely with the FTC and state attorneys general operating under federal law rather than the state statutes they currently administer. The Center for Democracy and Technology's Eric Null has described the bill as full of 'easily exploitable loopholes' that allow companies to hide violations behind cookie banners and terms of service agreements. The EFF adds that the data minimization language is structurally weaker than most existing state standards — it restricts data use only to what a company has 'disclosed,' rather than what is strictly necessary.

The surveillance implications of this bill's passage extend beyond consumer commerce. The same data broker ecosystem that feeds commercial advertising also feeds government agencies. A March 2026 NPR investigation documented ICE and other federal agencies purchasing bulk location data from commercial brokers — a practice that sidesteps the Fourth Amendment by converting a warrant requirement into a procurement transaction. Privacy advocates at the Electronic Privacy Information Center have described government data purchases without warrants as 'contributing to an ever-expanding infrastructure of private sector surveillance.' The SECURE Data Act does include a data broker registry at the FTC and requires data minimization, but it contains no provision that would directly restrict government access to commercially purchased data. The Government Surveillance Reform Act, introduced in March 2026 by Senators Mike Lee (R-UT) and Ron Wyden (D-OR) with bipartisan support, directly targets that loophole — the SECURE Data Act does not.

Also conspicuously absent: any provision addressing artificial intelligence and large language models. In an environment where Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei publicly warned in early 2026 that government-purchasable commercial data, combined with AI, allows federal agencies to assemble 'a comprehensive picture of any person's life — automatically and at massive scale,' the SECURE Data Act's silence on AI is not an oversight. It is a design choice. The companion GUARD Financial Data Act adds AI disclosure requirements for financial institutions, but the core bill governing the rest of the data economy says nothing about how AI systems may process, profile, or act on the consumer data it regulates.

The hearing tomorrow is a first step in a legislative gauntlet that has claimed every prior federal privacy bill for eight years. The preemption question, the private right of action, the absence of AI governance, the structured dismantling of state-level consumer protections that took years to build — these are not negotiating chips. They are structural features of a bill that, as written, would govern one of the most consequential legal regimes in American life. The Subcommittee begins at 10:15 a.m. The public can watch it live at energycommerce.house.gov.

I spent years inside the intelligence apparatus watching the government treat the law as a set of obstacles to route around rather than rules to follow. The SECURE Data Act presents a different but structurally identical problem: a law drafted to appear protective while systematically dismantling every mechanism Americans currently have to protect themselves. If this bill passes as written, there are clear winners. Data brokers operating at national scale win — one federal standard, no private lawsuits, FTC enforcement that is already stretched thin and politically compromised. The government surveillance apparatus wins — the commercial data pipeline that agencies currently exploit will be standardized and legitimized without a single warrant requirement added to it. Tech platforms win — preemption of California's opt-out infrastructure, automatic profiling restrictions, and the Delete Act removes the most aggressive privacy enforcement regime in the country. What Americans lose is harder to enumerate because the losses are distributed and deferred. They lose the right to sue. They lose stronger state protections. They lose the ability of state legislatures to respond to emerging privacy threats. In seven years of watching Congress debate federal privacy legislation, I have learned one thing: the bill that eliminates accountability mechanisms while wearing the language of consumer rights is the bill that was written for the industry, not for you.
