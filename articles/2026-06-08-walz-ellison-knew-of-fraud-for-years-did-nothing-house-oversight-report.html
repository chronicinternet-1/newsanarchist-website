Walz, Ellison Knew of Fraud for Years, Did Nothing: House Oversight Report

A damning House Oversight Committee report released today reveals that Minnesota Governor Tim Walz and Attorney General Keith Ellison were aware of credible, systemic fraud in federally funded social programs as early as 2019 but repeatedly chose inaction despite possessing clear legal authority to stop the bleeding—allowing an estimated $9 billion to flow to criminals.

Jordan Calloway AI-ASSISTED June 8, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform dropped a 205-page bombshell this morning that reads like a masterclass in institutional failure and the suppression of dissent. Titled "The Cost of Doing Nothing: How Tim Walz and Keith Ellison Fueled Minnesota's Fraud Explosion," the report documents months of investigative work, whistleblower interviews, and document reviews that paint a picture of leadership that knew exactly what was happening—and chose political expediency over accountability.

Let's be clear about what's at stake: an estimated $300 million in federal child nutrition funds was lost, and potentially $9 billion across Medicaid programs. That's real money that was supposed to feed children, house the disabled, and provide healthcare to vulnerable Americans. Instead, it went to fraudsters.

The findings are stark. The report exposes how senior Minnesota state officials, including Governor Walz and Attorney General Ellison, were aware of widespread taxpayer fraud in federally funded social programs for years, possessed the authority to stop payments and ban fraudulent providers from participating in these programs, but repeatedly failed to act. This wasn't incompetence masked by good intentions. Minnesota state agencies had clear authority to suspend or stop payments to providers suspected of fraud without requiring independent direction from courts, law enforcement agencies, or the federal government but failed to act.

What really matters for transparency advocates: the documents reveal the rationale behind the inaction, and it's damning. Testimony and documents show that concerns about litigation and accusations of discrimination—not legal barriers or directives from law enforcement—were cited as reasons for continuing payments to suspected fraudsters. In other words, fear of political blowback outweighed duty to protect public funds.

The Feeding Our Future fraud sits at the center of this investigation. Minnesota officials continued directing taxpayer dollars to Feeding Our Future and other high-risk organizations despite identifying serious program deficiencies. The numbers are obscene. Federal prosecutors allege that fraudsters recruited Somali families, paid kickbacks, and obtained fraudulent diagnoses. Autism treatment billing under one Medicaid program exploded from $671,000 in 2018 to $342 million in 2024, a roughly 500-fold increase, with fraudsters recruiting Somali families, paying monthly kickbacks to parents, and obtaining fraudulent autism diagnoses to maximize Medicaid billing. This should have been a five-alarm fire. Instead, the administration kept shoveling money into the system.

But here's the most chilling part, and where this story becomes crucial for anyone who cares about government accountability: the systematic retaliation against whistleblowers. A Minnesota Department of Education official who first contacted the FBI about Feeding Our Future told investigators that her superiors pressured her to stop looking into fraud "at every turn" and that she got her "hand slapped" for continuing to investigate. The report says dozens of staff in the human services department reported being warned not to speak up about the issue or they would be called racist and harm the government's reputation.

This is not a bug in the system. This is the feature. When government officials weaponize accusations of racism to silence legitimate concerns about fraud, they're not protecting vulnerable communities—they're protecting criminals. They're establishing a climate where truth-telling becomes a liability. They're destroying the institutional channels through which accountability flows.

According to the committee, Minnesota Gov. Tim Walz and Attorney General Keith Ellison were aware of credible fraud concerns in state social service programs as early as 2019 at the Department of Human Services and by April 2020 at the Department of Education, yet the money kept flowing. Seven years. They knew for seven years.

Despite contrary claims to media, Minnesota Governor Tim Walz and Attorney General Keith Ellison were aware of credible fraud concerns as early as 2019 at the Minnesota Department of Human Services and by April 2020 at the Minnesota Department of Education. When they testified before the committee, Walz stonewalled. Walz has given three different public answers about when he first learned of the fraud, issued corrections to his own corrections, and told the committee in March he simply couldn't remember. When investigators requested documents from both his office and Ellison's, Walz produced 48 documents, many of them public press releases. Ellison produced 11. Forty-eight documents. Eleven. That's not transparency. That's contempt.

The document production alone screams FOIA violation. When Congress demands records from state officials about a fraud investigation, and you respond with mostly press releases, you're intentionally obscuring the truth. You're making it impossible for the public to understand what you knew and when you knew it. And this is exactly why FOIA lawsuits matter—because the voluntary disclosure of government information cannot be trusted when that government has something to hide.

The Committee estimates the damage. Roughly $300 million in federal child nutrition funds were lost in just the Feeding Our Future scheme, while broader taxpayer-funded fraud totals could be as much as $9 billion across multiple programs. Meanwhile, The Trump administration has since withheld $259 million in Medicaid funds from Minnesota, executed 22 search warrants, and secured 15 new indictments. The feds are cleaning up a mess the Walz administration created through negligence and political calculation.

What's remarkable is the pattern the report documents. Fraud warnings were elevated to the most senior levels of the Minnesota state government, meaningful corrective action was delayed or avoided, and payments continued long after credible signs of fraud emerged. This wasn't a system failure. This was a choice. Every day they didn't act was another day they made an affirmative decision to let fraud continue.

The report concluded: "Gov. Walz and Attorney General Ellison clearly did not protect taxpayer dollars, but it is still an open question as to whether this was incompetence, willful blindness, or worse." That open question deserves answers. That open question demands document production, email reviews, calendar examinations, and sworn testimony. It demands the kind of investigative pressure that forces disclosure.

The Trump administration is pursuing aggressive enforcement—federal investigations, fund withholding, indictments. But Congress needs to keep the heat on. The public needs to see the documents that explain how and why this happened. We need transparency, not spin. We need accountability, not excuses. And we need whistleblowers to be protected, not punished.
