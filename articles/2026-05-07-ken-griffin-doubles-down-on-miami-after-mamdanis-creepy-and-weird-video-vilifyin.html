Ken Griffin "Doubles Down On Miami" After Mamdani's "Creepy And Weird" Video Vilifying Him

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist
May 6, 2026 · 4 min read

What they're not telling you: # Ken Griffin "Doubles Down On Miami" After Mamdani's "Creepy And Weird" Video Vilifying Him Billionaire hedge fund manager Ken Griffin is expanding Citadel's Miami headquarters specifically because New York's mayor proposed taxing his $238 million penthouse, marking an escalation in the wealth-flight dynamics reshaping American cities. At the Milken Institute Global Conference this week, Griffin disclosed that New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani's video promoting a pied-à-terre tax—which referenced Griffin's Central Park South property—directly triggered Citadel's decision to enlarge its planned Miami office building. Griffin watched the video three times and characterized it as "creepy and weird," according to Bloomberg.

The Take
Diana Reeves · Corporate Watchdog & Markets

# THE TAKE: Griffin's Miami Gambit Is Pure Regulatory Arbitrage Theater Ken Griffin's "doubling down" isn't conviction—it's math. When a $30B+ hedge fund manager flees high-tax jurisdictions, he's not making a political statement; he's optimizing his balance sheet. Mamdani's video was *irrelevant*. What matters: New York's millionaire tax threatens Griffin's 8-figure annual burden. Miami offers zero income tax. The "creepy" framing obscures the actual scandal—that billionaires possess sufficient capital mobility to simply *leave* when policy displeases them, while working-class New Yorkers cannot. Griffin's move reveals tax policy's fundamental asymmetry: it operates differently for those with geographic exit options. His public defiance isn't bravado; it's leverage. By relocating, he demonstrates that cities now compete for oligarch residency rather than govern them. Mamdani's mistake wasn't the video's tone. It was believing tax policy alone can constrain capital that's already crossed the Florida border.

What the Documents Show

The revelation exposes how tax policy targeting individual wealthy residents can produce the opposite of intended results: instead of generating revenue, aggressive rhetoric accelerates capital flight. Griffin's response mirrors his 2018 departure from Chicago, which he cited as "triggering the trauma" of being publicly criticized by local leadership. That move relocated Citadel and its affiliate Citadel Securities to Florida. The pattern suggests that city leaders face a delicate calculus—tax policy that singles out individuals by name and property can provoke defensive posturing from the wealthy rather than compliance. Mamdani's spokesperson countered that the mayor supports entrepreneurs but believes "New York's tax structure needs reform so wealthy residents contribute more," framing the issue as systemic rather than personal.

Follow the Money

Yet the outcome demonstrates the weakness of that distinction when implementation targets specific individuals. The economic stakes are substantial but complicated. Citadel still maintains nearly 2,500 employees in New York City and is pursuing a separate $6 billion office tower project at 350 Park Avenue—now described by Griffin as "a point of discussion internally." His statement that Miami "unquestionably" represented "the right choice" suggests the company's New York footprint may face contraction. Florida's absence of state income tax and corporate tax structures favorable to financial services firms create genuine competitive advantages beyond symbolic grievances. What remains largely unexamined in mainstream coverage is whether cities can realistically retain financial capital without accommodating the preferences of financial capitalists. Griffin's explicit framing—that states should "embrace business," "embrace education," and "embrace personal freedom and liberty"—reveals how wealthy executives frame tax increases as ideological threats rather than policy disagreements.

What Else We Know

The subtext normalizes capital mobility as a natural consequence of governance choices, obscuring questions about whether cities should restructure themselves around billionaire preferences or whether federal policy should address regional inequality differently. For ordinary New Yorkers, the practical implication is clear: as financial sector jobs migrate, tax bases erode, public services face pressure, and competition intensifies between cities to appear "business-friendly." The leverage has shifted decisively toward capital. Griffin's expansion in Miami isn't merely a business decision—it's a statement about which city leadership was more deferential to his interests.
