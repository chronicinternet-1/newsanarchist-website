What they're not telling you: # AI Note Takers Are Silently Destroying Attorney-Client Privilege—And Nobody's Stopping Them Attorney-client privilege—one of the few legal shields protecting confidential conversations from government subpoena—is evaporating in real time as AI note-taking apps record meetings without meaningful consent protocols or safeguards. This isn't a hypothetical risk buried in terms of service. It's an active vulnerability that could force lawyers to divulge privileged communications, collapse defense strategies, and expose clients to prosecution.

What the Documents Show

The problem is deceptively simple. Services like Otter.ai, Fireflies, and dozens of competitors market themselves as productivity tools for "capturing every word" in meetings. They're framed as conveniences—no more scrambling to take notes, no more missed details. What gets downplayed is the legal catastrophe embedded in the technology. When a client meets with their attorney and an AI recording device is running, courts haven't definitively established whether privilege still applies.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Some jurisdictions assume it does only if all parties explicitly agreed to recording. Others require that recordings be deleted after privileged information is discussed. The liability lies in a gray zone, and lawyers know it. The mainstream narrative treats this as merely a "privacy concern"—a personal comfort issue about what gets transcribed. That framing misses the existential threat to legal defense itself. Consider a corporate client meeting with counsel about a merger, regulatory investigation, or product liability issue.

What Else We Know

An AI transcriber is present. The conversation includes offhand jokes, hypothetical scenarios, candid assessments of legal exposure. Now imagine opposing counsel or government investigators subpoenaing that transcript. Suddenly, the attorney's work product and client strategy are discoverable. The privilege that was supposed to exist doesn't. The client never consented to the recording in any meaningful way—they just assumed their lawyer's office was secure.
