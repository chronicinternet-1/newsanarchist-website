What they're not telling you: # UK Jet Fuel Rationing Risks Emerge As Goldman Warns Of "Extreme Physical Tightness" Goldman Sachs is warning that the United Kingdom could face jet fuel rationing within months as global energy supply chains remain fractured from months of disrupted tanker flows through the Strait of Hormuz. Michele Della Vigna, Goldman's head of EMEA natural resources research, told clients that "extreme physical tightness in summer/early autumn" is a scenario the bank is forecasting for diesel and jet fuel availability across Europe. More strikingly, Della Vigna singled out the UK as particularly vulnerable, stating that "some countries (the UK in particular) could end up with extremely low inventories, and it's possible that rationing measures would be put in place to slow inventory draw." This assessment suggests that while crude oil prices briefly dipped below $100 per barrel this week following reports of potential U.S.-Iran negotiations, the underlying crisis in refined products remains acute and unresolved.

What the Documents Show

The Goldman analysis reveals the scale of the supply gap Europe faces. Della Vigna explained that "jet fuel prices in Europe will need to remain elevated to redirect cargoes from other regions, covering 50% of the shortfall in disrupted volumes from the Middle East through Hormuz and from Asian exporters no longer exporting to Europe." In other words, European markets must pull fuel supplies from competing regions globally—a costly and inefficient workaround that cannot be sustained indefinitely. The remaining 50% of the shortfall has no clear source. What mainstream financial media has largely underplayed is the critical distinction between crude oil availability and refined products availability. While headlines celebrate potential diplomatic breakthroughs that might reopen the Hormuz chokepoint, Goldman's warning indicates that even if negotiations succeed, the damage is already done.

Follow the Money

Months of disrupted flows have depleted inventories to dangerous levels, and reopening a shipping lane does not instantly restore supply chains or replenish storage tanks. The lag time between normalizing flows and stabilizing markets could stretch into months. The UK's particular vulnerability appears tied to its geographic distance from alternative suppliers and its reliance on refined product imports rather than domestic refining capacity. This structural dependency means that any prolonged supply disruption hits British markets harder than better-positioned competitors. For ordinary people, this translates directly to aviation disruptions, higher fuel costs across the economy, and potential rationing that could affect everything from commercial flights to heating oil availability heading into autumn. Goldman's forecast suggests that the energy crisis spreading across global markets is entering a new, more dangerous phase.

What Else We Know

The focus has shifted from crude oil prices to the far more consequential question of whether refined products can physically reach end-users. If the UK and other European nations do implement rationing, it would mark a significant escalation from the current period of elevated prices and tight supplies into actual scarcity management—a scenario most financial markets have not yet priced in.
