    UK Targets Kremlin-Linked Crypto Network In Latest Sanctions Round

    Diana Reeves
    Former SEC examiner
    May 28, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: The $90 Billion Loophole: How A Kremlin Settlement Network Exposed Years of Western Crypto Oversight Failure The A7 network moved $90 billion in a single year while Western regulators watched a ruble-backed token pipeline operate openly across three continents. The United Kingdom's latest sanctions package targets 18 entities tied to Russia's shadow financial infrastructure, but the announcement buried the most damning detail: a crypto-enabled settlement system allegedly connected to state lender Promsvyazbank has been routing money for sanctioned oligarchs and defense contractors at scale while operating in jurisdictions Western intelligence agencies monitor. The A7 network, which claims to move roughly half of Russia's annual military spending through cross-border ruble tokens and exchanges, represents not a sophisticated new Russian innovation but rather the consequence of a decade-long regulatory vacuum that American and European authorities created and failed to fill.

    What the Documents Show

    The mechanism is straightforward. A7A5, a ruble-backed token, connects users through exchanges and settlement hubs based in Kyrgyzstan and Georgia—two countries where cryptocurrency regulation remains deliberately thin and banking secrecy provisions remain thick. A Kyrgyz bank now targeted by UK sanctions allegedly processes flows tied to A7. Meanwhile, a major global cryptocurrency exchange, unnamed in the UK announcement but described as having sent more than $1.5 billion to Kremlin-adjacent entities, operated under licenses in jurisdictions where beneficial ownership reporting is optional. Three Georgian trading platforms targeting Russian clients completed the circuit, allowing sanctioned individuals and state enterprises to move capital without touching SWIFT or any Western-regulated banking infrastructure.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    The timeline matters. Financial crime researchers have documented A7's emergence over two years as Russian sanctions-evasion networks grew more sophisticated. Yet no American financial regulator—not the Treasury Department's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, not the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, not the SEC—issued a public enforcement action against U.S.-accessible exchanges handling A7 flows until after the UK acted. The pattern here is institutional paralysis masquerading as vigilance. Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper framed the sanctions as disruption of Russia's "backbone of illicit finance channels," language designed to suggest the problem is now addressed. The architecture remains intact.

    What Else We Know

    What changed is only that one pathway—the A7 network itself—faces financial exclusion. The exchanges, the Kyrgyz bank, the Georgian platforms: these entities will rebrand, relocate, and reconstitute under new corporate shells. Without simultaneous enforcement against the global financial institutions facilitating dollar conversions for these networks, without naming the exchange executives who approved billion-dollar transfers to blacklisted Russian actors, without examining why American banks processed nostro and vostro accounts for Kremlin-linked entities years after sanctions were imposed, the UK announcement is theater. Russia's own economic ministry estimates $90 billion in capital flight through shadow channels annually. That figure means either Western sanctions are partially a fiction, or the financial institutions processing the dollar and euro exits are complicit through negligence or design. The UK sanctions name the Russian pieces.

    The Diana Reeves Take
    Corporate Watchdog & Money & Markets

    The story regulators and media outlets want to tell is about stopping Russia. The story the data reveals is about institutional failure in the West.

    I find striking the temporal gap between when independent researchers documented A7's operations and when Western law enforcement acted. That lag suggests either U.S. financial regulators lacked the capacity to analyze crypto flows at scale, or they deprioritized it. Both explanations are damning. Treasury and the Federal Reserve have spent a decade insisting they have sophisticated surveillance tools. The A7 network's $90 billion annual throughput contradicts that claim.

    What benefits from this official narrative is institutional continuity. Regulators issue statements about vigilance without deploying enforcement. Global exchanges settle small cases with fines that are statistical noise against their trading volumes. Banks process sanctions-evasion flows and face no individual accountability for their compliance officers or executives. The pattern protects bureaucratic reputations at the expense of actual sanctions efficacy.

    Watch this closely: Which American financial institutions appear in the next tranche of leaked A7 correspondent banking records. That will tell you everything about whether this enforcement is real or choreographed.
