    Foundation for Information Policy Research warns many proposed solutions for age verification will exacerbate harms they are trying to prevent & could expose children to risks of blackmail & abuse

    &;.
    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    June 7, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: Age Verification Schemes Will Hand Children's Biometric Data to Private Contractors While Creating Blackmail Infrastructure, FIPR Warns The Foundation for Information Policy Research has documented that proposed age verification systems for online content—designed ostensibly to protect minors—will require the collection and storage of children's identity documents and biometric markers by private corporations operating without adequate legal safeguards against data breach, sale, or coercion. This finding inverts the stated purpose of age verification legislation advancing through multiple jurisdictions. Rather than shielding minors from harmful content, FIPR's analysis shows these systems create novel attack surfaces: a centralized repository of children's verified identities and facial biometrics becomes precisely the infrastructure that enables blackmail, targeted harassment, and abuse.

    What the Documents Show

    The systems being proposed don't merely fail to prevent harm—they actively manufacture it. The mechanics matter here because they're where institutional failure concentrates. Age verification typically requires one of three approaches: document upload (child submits passport or ID photo to a third-party verifier), facial recognition (child's face scanned and compared against government databases), or age estimation AI (biometric analysis without explicit identity matching). Each route funnels sensitive data through private contractors—companies like Yoti, Verify, or AU10TUX—that operate under looser data protection standards than government agencies. These firms store what they collect.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Breaches are not theoretical: in 2021, Clearview AI's facial recognition database of 20 billion images was accessed by unauthorized parties. The contractors holding children's documents operate in similar conditions. FIPR's warning identifies a second mechanism of harm: blackmail. Once a verified child's identity is linked to their online activity—what content they accessed, when, from which device—that linkage becomes leverage. A bad actor obtaining credentials from a compromised verification platform doesn't need to publish the data. The mere threat of exposure creates coercion.

    What Else We Know

    This is distinct from traditional fraud. It's infrastructural extortion built into the system's design. The policy framing obscures this. Governments and child safety advocacy groups describe age verification as a protective measure, a guardrail against minors accessing adult content. The language emphasizes prevention. But FIPR's technical analysis shows the opposite relationship: the prevention system is the threat.
