What they're not telling you: # After Bizarre CEO Interview On CNBC, Michael Burry Exits GameStop Position Michael Burry, the contrarian investor whose early GameStop bet helped ignite the retail trading frenzy, has abandoned his position after the company's CEO failed to credibly explain how to finance a $56 billion acquisition of eBay—a deal worth nearly five times GameStop's own market value. In a Monday Substack post, Burry stated the proposed eBay acquisition fundamentally altered his original investment thesis. The deal would require GameStop to offer $125 per eBay share through a combination of cash and stock, but the mechanics didn't pencil out.

What the Documents Show

Burry, who had backed GameStop in 2019 and helped spark the stock's trajectory, wrote bluntly: "Wall Street does indeed mistake debt for creativity, and does so constantly. I of all people should have known." His exit signals that even the architect of the GameStop narrative has lost confidence in management's direction. The breaking point came during a 16-minute appearance by CEO Ryan Cohen on CNBC's Squawk Box, where anchor Andrew Ross Sorkin pressed him on a reported $16 billion financing gap. Cohen's responses were notably vague. He pointed to GameStop's $9.4 billion cash position and cited a financing letter from TD Securities for up to $20 billion, but the numbers still didn't reconcile a deal of this magnitude.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

When Sorkin and co-hosts Becky Quick and Michael Santoli pushed deeper on dilution concerns and strategic rationale, Cohen leaned on repetition rather than substance—insisting the offer was "half cash, half stock" without addressing the fundamental math or why GameStop should pursue an acquisition larger than itself. What the mainstream financial press has downplayed is the visceral quality of Cohen's discomfort on air. Videos circulated showing his pauses, evasive body language, and apparent struggle to articulate basic deal mechanics—red flags that typically precede investor flight. Rather than presenting a CEO commanding his brief, the interview showcased someone unable to defend a transformative corporate action under basic questioning. This wasn't a matter of rhetorical style; it was an inability to explain how a company operates when its own leadership cannot answer direct questions about financing and shareholder dilution. The broader implication extends beyond GameStop's stock price.

What Else We Know

Burry's exit demonstrates that even investors with deep conviction in a company's fundamentals will flee when management pursues strategies that defy financial logic. For ordinary investors who entered GameStop positions after hearing Burry's name or following the retail trading narrative, this moment represents a critical test: whether to trust management's vision or acknowledge that the original thesis—that GameStop represented genuine value—has been abandoned in favor of increasingly speculative bets. The fact that the very person who sparked the bull case is now exiting should serve as a sobering reminder that even celebrated contrarian investors can be wrong, and that a CEO's inability to explain a deal during a live national broadcast may be the most honest signal a market gets.
