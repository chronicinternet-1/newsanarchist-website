Grusch Accuses Spy Agencies of Hiding Billions in Secret UAP Slush Funds

UAP whistleblower David Grusch charged the Defense Intelligence Agency with obstructing Congress and harboring billions in concealed funding for classified aerospace programs, escalating demands for unredacted disclosure just weeks after the Trump administration's partial file release.

Casey North AI-ASSISTED June 10, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Two years after his explosive congressional testimony, former intelligence officer David Grusch returned to Capitol Hill on June 9 with a sharper accusation: the government's own spy agencies are systematically hiding billions of dollars in classified defense spending from lawmakers who authorize the nation's budget.

Grusch, who served on the Pentagon's Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force and later as the National Reconnaissance Office's UAP representative, told a bipartisan coalition of representatives that the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) has stonewalled congressional oversight efforts despite lawful document requests. The charge came during a press conference at the Capitol where lawmakers and disclosure advocates demanded Congress pass stronger whistleblower protections and compel full release of restricted UAP materials.

The accusation cuts to the heart of a broader transparency crisis: the Trump administration released two batches of declassified UAP files beginning May 8, 2026—first through the Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters (PURSUE) portal, then a second batch on May 22 containing 222 documents including 51 audio recordings and over 40 videos. But career intelligence officials have continued to resist, redact, and withhold additional materials that could illuminate decades of unexplained aerial incidents.

"We would ask questions, and they would then push back. We would ask more questions, and the pushback became more significant," said Representative Jared Moskowitz, D-Florida, describing the agency resistance. "We would try to put language in an amendment, in a bill, and they would oppose it."

Grusch's "slush fund" allegation targets a structural vulnerability in U.S. defense budgeting. The Trump administration's fiscal year 2027 budget includes nearly $132 billion in classified military and intelligence spending accessible only to cleared officials and select lawmakers. Within that opaque universe, special access programs (SAPs) operate with minimal external oversight—the ideal cover for sustained, undisclosed funding of classified aerospace projects.

Representative Eric Burlison, R-Missouri, framed the issue in terms of legal accountability: "There must be real penalties for officials who knowingly withhold information from Congress."

Moskowitz expanded the critique beyond UAPs to Pentagon-wide financial dysfunction: "There's trillions of dollars missing at the Pentagon."

What makes Grusch's timing significant is the narrow window of opportunity. The PURSUE releases have exposed the scale of government UAP documentation—videos of high-performance objects tracked by military sensors, pilot accounts describing aerial phenomena that exhibit unexplained maneuverability, and decade-old intelligence memos suggesting the government has long known something unexplained operates in restricted U.S. airspace. But the released files are heavily redacted. Metadata is missing. Context is stripped. And the most sensitive materials remain locked away.

NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman conceded as much in late May, noting that while the declassified files show "real unexplained phenomena," they reveal nothing of alien bodies or recovered spacecraft. But he also observed that "some of the most interesting data that NASA has provided as part of the UAP disclosure was taken on the surface of the moon from Apollo 12 and 17." That data—previously hidden—is now public. What else remains classified?

The second tranche of files included an intelligence officer's firsthand account of helicopter crew members witnessing "orange orbs" that tracked fighter jets and matched their speed before vanishing. The memo notes the witnesses "were virtually speechless after these observations." There is no official conclusion about what the objects were. No follow-up investigation is documented. The case simply ends.

That pattern—credible military witnesses, multi-sensor corroboration, zero explanation—repeats across the released files. And it suggests that whatever analytical frameworks exist within classified channels have either concluded the phenomena are terrestrial adversary technology (highly classified), concluded they are non-human, or produced no definitive assessment worth sharing with the public.

Grusch has previously testified under oath that he encountered evidence in classified channels related to UAPs and "non-human intelligence." His June 9 statement that the DIA is blocking document releases implies that the withheld materials would substantiate claims he can only hint at in unclassified forums.

The press conference also heard from Representatives Anna Paulina Luna, R-Florida, and Tim Burchett, R-Tennessee, both vocal disclosure advocates. They pushed for passage of the UAP Disclosure Act, which would strengthen whistleblower protections and require federal agencies to declassify and publish UAP records on a forced timeline.

But here's the hard reality: Congress has no mechanism to compel declassification over military objections if national security is cited. The Pentagon's official position—repeated by Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth and confirmed by multiple intelligence analysts—is that the declassified files show no verified evidence of extraterrestrial technology or recovered alien craft. That position shields the classified remainder from forced release.

Yet the existence of a multi-billion-dollar "slush fund" dedicated to aerospace programs of unknown purpose contradicts the claim of transparency. If there is nothing anomalous to hide, why the obstruction? Why the redactions? Why the classified budgets?

Grusch's accusation transforms the disclosure debate from "do aliens exist?" into a narrower, more consequential question: "Are U.S. intelligence agencies preventing elected representatives from understanding how defense dollars are spent and what objects are operating in American airspace?"

That is a question Congress can answer—if it has the political will. So far, Grusch's June 9 press conference suggests it does not. The bipartisan attendance was symbolic. The legislative follow-up remains to be seen.
