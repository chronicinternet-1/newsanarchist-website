FBI Admits Warrantless Location Purchases as AI Supercharges the Dragnet

FBI Director Kash Patel confirmed under oath in March 2026 that the bureau is buying Americans' location histories from commercial data brokers without a warrant — while the Pentagon simultaneously tried to blacklist an AI company for refusing to enable the same surveillance at scale.

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 3, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

On March 18, 2026, something extraordinary happened in a Senate Intelligence Committee hearing: a sitting FBI director openly confirmed, on the record, that his agency buys Americans' location data from commercial data brokers and uses it in federal investigations — no warrant required, no judge consulted, no probable cause established.

FBI Director Kash Patel, appearing alongside Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard and Defense Intelligence Agency chief Lt. Gen. James Adams III, told Sen. Ron Wyden of Oregon that the FBI 'uses all tools to do our mission.' When pressed on whether that included purchasing Americans' location data, Patel said: 'We do purchase commercially available information that's consistent with the Constitution and the laws under the Electronic Communications Privacy Act.' Wyden's follow-up was blunt: 'So you're saying that the agency will buy Americans' location data?' Patel's non-denial was the confirmation.

This was not a bureaucratic abstraction. The FBI has a contract worth up to $27 million with Babel Street for 5,000 licenses to its Locate X product — a tool that aggregates location data harvested primarily from consumer smartphone apps. The Department of Homeland Security went further: in February 2026, DHS signed a $1 billion contract with Palantir to deploy AI-powered data analytics across all DHS components, including Customs and Border Protection and ICE. ICE separately holds a $30 million contract with Palantir for 'ImmigrationOS,' a platform designed to track people for deportation with 'near real-time visibility.' DHS has been purchasing cell phone location data since at least 2017.

The legal architecture enabling all of this is a gap that privacy advocates have been raising for years. In 2018, the Supreme Court ruled in Carpenter v. United States that the government needs a warrant to compel a carrier to hand over cell tower location data. But purchasing the same data from a commercial broker is a different question — one no court has yet resolved. The government's legal theory holds that the third-party doctrine applies: data you share with an app is data you've forfeited. The Electronic Communications Privacy Act, written in 1986 when data brokers did not exist, provides no meaningful restriction. That legal theory has not been tested in court and, notably, Patel's March 18 admission reverses what his predecessor Christopher Wray told Congress in 2023, when Wray stated the bureau was not actively purchasing location data.

The surveillance apparatus that has assembled itself around this loophole is not hypothetical. It spans every major federal law enforcement and intelligence agency. What is new — and what makes this moment qualitatively different from the NSA revelations of 2013 — is the AI layer now sitting on top of that raw data. Where previous surveillance programs were constrained by human capacity to sort through information, AI systems can now synthesize thousands of disparate data points — fitness trackers, smart thermostats, grocery loyalty cards, geolocation pings from mobile ad networks — into detailed behavioral profiles within seconds. That automation effectively removes meaningful human oversight from domestic intelligence gathering in a way that was not technically possible even five years ago.

This is precisely what made the Pentagon's confrontation with Anthropic so revealing. The maker of the Claude AI model refused the Defense Department's demand to remove contractual safeguards preventing use of Claude for mass domestic surveillance. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth designated Anthropic a 'supply chain risk to national security' — a label previously reserved for Chinese firms like Huawei — and the Pentagon moved to sever a contract valued at up to $200 million. Trump ordered every federal agency to cease use of Anthropic's technology. On March 9, four days after the designation, Anthropic filed suit in federal court in California, arguing the move violated its First and Fifth Amendment rights.

The significance of the Anthropic dispute is not the corporate drama. It is what the Pentagon's demands reveal about intent. The government did not merely want to use Claude for counterterrorism or foreign intelligence. It demanded that Anthropic grant 'full access for all lawful uses' and drop explicit prohibitions on domestic mass surveillance. The Pentagon's position was that it already has internal policies against mass surveillance, so a written contractual bar was unnecessary. Anthropic declined. In its public statement, the company noted that under current law the government can already purchase detailed records of Americans' movements, web browsing, and associations without a warrant — and that powerful AI makes it possible to assemble that scattered data into a comprehensive picture of any person's life automatically and at massive scale.

The Trump administration's national AI policy framework, released on March 20, 2026 — two days after Patel's Senate testimony — compounded the concern. The framework urges Congress to use grants and tax incentives to fund wider deployment of AI tools across American industry and to allow industry and academia to use federal datasets to train AI systems. Federal datasets contain biographical, employment, and tax information accumulated across a lifetime. Opening that data to AI training pipelines, without corresponding privacy protections, creates a durable infrastructure for surveillance that no future administration would easily dismantle.

Against that backdrop, a bipartisan group of lawmakers introduced the Government Surveillance Reform Act of 2026 on March 12, sponsored by Senators Ron Wyden (D-OR) and Mike Lee (R-UT), along with Representatives Zoe Lofgren (D-CA) and Warren Davidson (R-OH). Cosponsors include both Sen. Elizabeth Warren (D-MA) and Sen. Cynthia Lummis (R-WY) — a pairing that illustrates the unusual coalition this issue has assembled. The bill would ban federal agencies from purchasing Americans' data from data brokers without a warrant, require court-authorized warrants for FBI backdoor searches of Section 702 communications, close the 'about collection' loophole allowing the NSA to capture communications that merely mention a surveillance target, and repeal a controversial 2024 RISAA expansion that can compel landlords, data center operators, and other service providers to secretly hand over communications access.

The FBI currently conducts roughly 200,000 warrantless searches of Americans' communications each year using Section 702 data. That authority was set to expire on April 20, 2026. The White House and House Speaker Mike Johnson both pushed for a clean reauthorization with no changes. Rep. Jim Jordan, once an ally of the warrant-requirement coalition, flipped and backed the administration's clean extension. Some 130 civil society organizations signed a letter urging Congress to close the data broker loophole before any Section 702 renewal, citing the 'unprecedented expansion of warrantless mass surveillance' and the potential for the loophole to 'supercharge AI-powered surveillance.' The legislation stalled.

The data broker loophole is not a technical edge case. It is the operating system of modern domestic intelligence. A purchase receipt at a hardware store, a geolocation ping from a navigation app, a click through a health information website — individually innocuous, collectively a surveillance profile that Hoover-era investigators would have required wiretap orders and informants to compile over months. The government does not build this collection apparatus. It subscribes to it.

I spent years inside NSA contractor systems where the legal architecture around collection was taken seriously — not perfectly, but seriously. What we are watching now is the wholesale abandonment of that architecture, replaced by a credit card and a data broker's API. Kash Patel's March 18 testimony was not a slip. It was a declaration of doctrine: the Fourth Amendment does not apply when the data is commercially available. The Anthropic confrontation makes the ambition explicit — the government wanted an AI model capable of mass domestic surveillance and was prepared to invoke the Korean War-era Defense Production Act to get it. The Government Surveillance Reform Act would close the loophole. Congress let the April 20 deadline pass. If nothing changes, the winners are the surveillance contractors — Palantir, Babel Street, and their successors — the agencies that subscribe to their feeds, and every future administration inheriting this infrastructure. The losers are the 330 million people whose location histories, browsing records, and financial data are already in the pipeline, awaiting the next query.
