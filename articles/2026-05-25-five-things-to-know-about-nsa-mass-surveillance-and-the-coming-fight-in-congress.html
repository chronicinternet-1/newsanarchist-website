    Five Things to Know About NSA Mass Surveillance and the Coming Fight in Congress

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 24, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: THE NSA'S WARRANTLESS BULK COLLECTION PERSISTS THROUGH LEGAL ARCHITECTURE DESIGNED TO PREVENT EXACTLY THAT The National Security Agency continues operating mass surveillance programs that collect Americans' communications without individualized warrants, sustained by statutory language in the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act that the agency interprets as authorization for bulk data acquisition rather than targeted surveillance. The architecture enabling this collection operates through specific FISA court procedures that require minimal threshold evidentiary standards. Under Section 702 of FISA, reauthorized in 2018 and set to expire in 2025, the NSA may target "non-United States persons" reasonably believed to be outside the United States.

    What the Documents Show

    The statutory language contains no explicit prohibition on collecting communications of American citizens incidentally connected to those targets. The FISA Amendments Act, enacted in 2008, formalized this structure. What the statutory text does not explicitly forbid, NSA lawyers have consistently interpreted as permissible. Documents obtained by the American Civil Liberties Union establish that NSA collection under Section 702 routinely sweeps in domestic communications. The agency does not know—and does not systematically attempt to determine—the volume of Americans' data captured in these operations.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Internal NSA compliance reports cited by the ACLU show the agency has repeatedly discovered it retained Americans' communications longer than permitted by internal guidelines, yet continued operations without substantial operational change. The FISA court itself operates under procedural constraints that limit oversight effectiveness. The court hears only arguments from the Department of Justice. No adverse party represents privacy interests or challenges government assertions about collection scope. The government supplies the court with its own legal interpretations of what FISA permits. When the court grants surveillance applications, those approvals become the bureaucratic foundation for sustained collection.

    What Else We Know

    The government need not demonstrate that proposed surveillance targets pose specific national security threats—only that the targets are "non-United States persons" and that collection methods are "reasonably designed" to target them. The next statutory reauthorization fight—expected in Congress in 2025—will determine whether Section 702 continues unchanged, receives modification, or expires entirely. The ACLU and allied civil liberties organizations have submitted detailed reform proposals. These include explicit prohibitions on bulk collection, requirements that NSA demonstrate individualized probable cause for American citizens' communications, and restoration of adversarial proceedings in FISA court where privacy advocates can present counterarguments to government surveillance applications. Congressional defenders of current NSA authorities argue that Section 702 contains sufficient safeguards and that modification would impair national security operations. The statutory text itself—not NSA overreach beyond legal authority—is the operational problem.

    The Marcus Webb Take
    Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

    The pattern here is institutional: we have created legal structures that require no wrongdoing to enable mass surveillance. The NSA isn't violating FISA—the agency is executing it.

    This matters because it reveals what often escapes mainstream coverage: the real power isn't in secret programs later exposed. It's in statutes written permissively enough that lawful behavior becomes indistinguishable from the abuse we theoretically prevent through oversight. The FISA court, Congress, and the executive branch benefit from this ambiguity. Ambiguity protects all of them. The government gets surveillance authority. Congress gets plausible deniability—they didn't authorize bulk collection explicitly, they merely didn't forbid it. The courts perform their checking function while approving what they review.

    What I find striking is the 2025 reauthorization moment nobody in mainstream coverage is treating with urgency it deserves. This is a hard legislative deadline. Either Congress modifies Section 702's statutory language with specific prohibitions, or collection continues unchanged for another several years. The burden falls on those demanding reform to propose language narrow enough to survive legal challenge but effective enough to actually constrain NSA behavior.

    Watch what language Congress uses in the reauthorization bill. Watch whether it adds explicit prohibition on domestic bulk collection or merely reaffirms existing authority. The words matter more than the debate.
