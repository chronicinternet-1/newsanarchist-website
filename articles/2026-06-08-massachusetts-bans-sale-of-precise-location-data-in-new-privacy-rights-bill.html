    Massachusetts bans sale of precise location data in new privacy rights bill

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    June 8, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: Massachusetts Bans Precise Location Data Sales—While Federal Law Enforcement Continues Warrantless Purchases Massachusetts has passed legislation explicitly prohibiting the commercial sale of precise location data, but the law contains a structural gap that leaves the state's residents vulnerable to the same data-acquisition methods federal law enforcement has relied on for over a decade: purchasing location intelligence directly from commercial brokers without judicial oversight. The Massachusetts Consumer Data Privacy Act passed the state House unanimously on June 8, 2026—146-0—after the Senate approved an identical framework in September 2025. The bill prohibits companies handling data on more than 100,000 residents from selling precise geolocation information, biometrics, genetic data, and religious or sexual orientation markers without explicit user consent.

    What the Documents Show

    The law applies to both residents and visitors. Governor approval is expected, though no signing date has been announced. The legislation represents the latest state-level attempt to regulate what federal law has left largely uncontrolled: the location data pipeline. What the reporting misses is the institutional mechanism that renders this ban incomplete. Federal agencies—including the FBI, DEA, and ICE—have operated under the legal interpretation that data purchased on the open market bypasses Fourth Amendment protections requiring a warrant.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    This doctrine emerged after the 2018 Supreme Court decision Carpenter v. United States, which ruled that cell-site location information held by carriers requires a warrant. Rather than comply with that ruling uniformly, federal law enforcement pivoted to purchasing location data from commercial brokers—specifically from aggregators that compile location signals from apps, advertising networks, and mobile SDKs. No warrant is required for these purchases under current legal interpretation. Massachusetts's ban on sales addresses the supply side. It does not address federal acquisition of location data collected before the law takes effect, nor does it establish penalties for federal agencies that circumvent the ban by purchasing data through out-of-state brokers.

    What Else We Know

    The Trump administration's decision in 2026 to reverse the Biden-era FTC effort to ban federal purchase of sensitive commercial data means there is no counterforce at the national level restricting what the FBI, DEA, or other agencies can buy. The practical effect: Massachusetts residents' location data will continue flowing to federal law enforcement through channels that predate the law and remain outside its jurisdiction. Data brokers operating in states without location data restrictions—Texas, Florida, and most of the Midwest—can continue selling location intelligence to federal agencies without consent restrictions. The FBI's Location Intelligence Program, documented through FOIA requests as operating through contracts with multiple commercial data providers, faces no statutory limitation from Massachusetts law because those purchases occur outside the state's regulatory reach. The ban is genuine policy progress. But it reflects the core institutional failure: states regulate commercial data markets while federal agencies operate in a separate legal architecture where the same data is acquisition-legal.
