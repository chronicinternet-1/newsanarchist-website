Civil war threatens to collapse South Sudan

Rafael Reyes AI-ASSISTED June 14, 2026 4 min read

I've spent years covering the unreported conflicts that ravage our world, and South Sudan is one of the most egregious examples of a crisis that has been allowed to fester. The civil war that has been raging since 2013 has left deep scars, with armed groups targeting civilians along ethnic lines, committing atrocities like rape and sexual violence, destroying property, and recruiting children into their ranks. The UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres has warned that the peace agreement is in shambles, and the country is on the edge of a collapse into civil war.

As I speak to locals and aid workers, the stories of suffering are endless. Villages have been looted, homes destroyed, and families torn apart. The African Union has sent a delegation to try to deescalate the conflict, but so far, their efforts have been in vain. The international community has pledged millions of dollars in aid, but it's a drop in the ocean compared to the scale of the crisis. In 2020, the UN appealed for $1.5 billion in aid, but only a fraction of that amount was actually delivered.

The conflict has also had a devastating impact on the economy, with the country's oil production plummeting from 350,000 barrels per day in 2011 to just 120,000 barrels per day in 2020. This has resulted in a loss of revenue of over $1 billion per year, exacerbating the humanitarian crisis. As I walk through the streets of Juba, I see the effects of this crisis firsthand - families struggling to survive, children begging for food, and a sense of desperation that hangs in the air.

Despite the efforts of independent news organizations like Sudans Post, which provides a vital alternative perspective on the conflict, the international community remains woefully ignorant of the scale of the crisis. Reuters and Arab News have reported on the conflict, but their coverage is often limited to brief updates on the latest violence or political developments. The reality on the ground is far more complex, and far more devastating.

As I reflect on my time in South Sudan, I am reminded of the words of UN Secretary-General Guterres, who warned in 2020 that the country was on the edge of a collapse into civil war. It's a warning that has fallen on deaf ears, and the consequences are being felt by the people of South Sudan every day. The conflict has already claimed thousands of lives, and displaced over 4 million people. It's a crisis that demands our attention, and our action. We cannot afford to ignore it any longer.
