EU Shifts to Renewables

Europe's transition to carbon-free energy accelerates as solar and battery systems become cost-competitive with fossil fuels

Elena Vasquez
Former foreign correspondent, 15 years covering conflict zones
July 8, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

The European Union is undergoing a significant shift towards renewable energy sources, driven by the increasing cost-competitiveness of carbon-free energy and concerns over climate change. As reported by bloomberg.com, combined solar and battery systems can now supply electricity more affordably than gas in many parts of the world. This trend is expected to continue, with many EU countries investing heavily in renewable energy infrastructure, such as the new 400-kV electricity interconnector commissioned by Portugal and Spain, which will increase cross-border power exchange capacity by about 1,000 MW, as noted on Renewables Now.

The shift towards renewables is also being driven by geopolitical considerations, with many countries seeking to reduce their reliance on foreign energy sources. According to the Ipsos Energy Transition Barometer, concern that countries rely too much on foreign energy sources climbed 7 points to 70% between February and May 2026, a shift driven by the Iran conflict making energy vulnerability feel concrete and immediate. This has led to increased investment in domestic renewable energy production, such as the plan by German tyre manufacturer Continental AG to erect three wind turbines near its tyre factory in Korbach, Germany, to secure direct access to around 55 GWh of clean electricity annually.

The EU's shift towards renewables is also having an impact on global energy markets, with reuters.com reporting that global oil prices fell on Monday after U.S.-Iran talks concluded in Switzerland with Tehran saying it had secured waivers for oil and petrochemical exports. This development is likely to have significant implications for the global balance of power, particularly in the context of US-China Ties Shift and the ongoing tensions in the Middle East, where the US has recently US Retaliates in Hormuz. Meanwhile, the US is also facing pushback on its energy policies, with Trump Faces Pushback from environmental groups and other stakeholders.

As the EU continues to transition towards renewable energy, it is likely to have a significant impact on the global energy landscape, with potential implications for the balance of power and the emergence of new potential superpower nations. With the cost of renewable energy continuing to fall, it is likely that the EU's shift towards renewables will accelerate in the coming years, driven by both economic and geopolitical considerations. The latest news on the energy transition can be found on reuters.com, which provides up-to-date coverage of the global energy market.

As I reflect on the EU's shift to renewables, I firmly believe that this transition is not only necessary but also long overdue. My thesis is that the EU's commitment to renewable energy will have a profound impact on the environment and the economy. If nothing changes, the big winners will be the fossil fuel corporations that have dominated the energy landscape for decades, continuing to reap profits at the expense of our planet. However, with the EU's renewed focus on sustainability, I am hopeful that we can create a more equitable and environmentally conscious future, one that prioritizes the well-being of people and the planet over corporate interests.
