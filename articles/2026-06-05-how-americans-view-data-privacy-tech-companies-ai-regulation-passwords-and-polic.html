    How Americans View Data Privacy: Tech Companies, AI, Regulation, Passwords and Policies

    Jordan Calloway
    Investigative journalist
    June 5, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: The Privacy Consent Theater: Why Americans Say "No" and Tech Companies Win Anyway Americans are screaming they don't want their data harvested by artificial intelligence, yet the regulatory machinery designed to protect them remains dead in the water—and the people who built that broken system have never been held accountable. According to Pew Research Center data released this week, 60 percent of Americans say they are not comfortable with companies using their personal information to train artificial intelligence systems. The number is damning: six in ten citizens actively oppose what has become the default operating procedure of the digital economy.

    What the Documents Show

    Yet between that polling number and actual protection stands a chasm that Congress refuses to bridge, and the Federal Trade Commission under Lina Khan has failed to cross despite years of public warnings. The Pew survey reveals the gap between stated public preference and regulatory reality. Seventy-one percent of Americans believe the government should regulate artificial intelligence, and 52 percent say tech companies have too much power over personal data. These aren't fringe positions—they represent a genuine consensus. But here is what the mainstream coverage of this data misses: the regulatory architecture already exists.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Section 5 of the FTC Act grants the agency authority to police unfair and deceptive practices. What's missing is enforcement with teeth. The FTC has filed complaints against Amazon Web Services, Meta Platforms, and others over data practices, but these actions treat symptoms while the underlying model remains intact. Tech companies have discovered they can survive FTC scrutiny by paying fines that amount to rounding errors in quarterly reports. Meta paid $5.1 billion to settle privacy violations in 2019—then continued collecting and monetizing the same data under marginally adjusted terms. Amazon Web Services faced FTC action over children's data practices; the company adjusted consent language and moved forward.

    What Else We Know

    No executives faced criminal liability. No systemic change emerged. What Pew's data exposes is that Americans understand the asymmetry: they want opt-in consent for AI training, meaning companies should ask permission and let users refuse. Instead, they get opt-out models buried in terms of service, where refusal requires legal expertise and persistence most users don't possess. Sixty percent of Americans don't want this. The FTC knows Americans don't want this.
