What they're not telling you: # Meta, TikTok Are Sent Personal Data From Health Exchanges — Alarming Privacy Experts Nearly all state-run health insurance exchanges are leaking sensitive personal data—including race, location, and immigration status—directly to Meta and TikTok through hidden tracking pixels embedded in their enrollment websites. A Bloomberg News investigation of thousands of webpages across the 20 state-run health exchanges plus Washington, DC discovered that advertising trackers have been quietly installed on these government sites without explicit user consent. The trackers fire when citizens navigate enrollment pages, capturing not just anonymous browsing patterns but intimate details about visitors' circumstances: their geographic location, in some cases their racial or ethnic background, and indicators of immigration status.

# THE TAKE: Government Health Exchanges Are Just Ad Networks Now Twenty state health exchanges installed Meta and TikTok pixels—tracking code that broadcasts your insurance shopping directly to surveillance platforms. Race, location, immigration status. The data flows without encryption, consent mechanisms, or audit trails. This isn't negligence. It's architectural choice. States outsourced "analytics" to companies with zero healthcare compliance obligation. The pixels fire on every page load—including sensitive enrollment screens disclosing income, citizenship, medical history. Privacy experts feigning surprise miss the obvious: these exchanges operated as voluntary data pipelines from day one. Blame compliance theater. HIPAA doesn't cover state administrators using third-party ad networks. FTC has no enforcement mechanism here. The real story? Government agencies learned to monetize citizen health data without statutory restriction. They're not contractors for Meta. They're partners.

What the Documents Show

This data flows in real-time to major social media platforms and their advertising partners, creating a direct pipeline from government health infrastructure to private tech companies. The scale is staggering. Nearly every state exchange reviewed—a critical infrastructure serving millions of Americans seeking insurance during open enrollment periods—has implemented these trackers. What makes this particularly troubling is the gap between what state officials believe their sites are doing and what's actually happening. Bloomberg's technical analysis revealed that state administrators often underestimated how much user data was being transmitted.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Some officials appeared unaware their sites were sending demographic information at all, believing the trackers only measured page traffic or basic analytics. The discrepancy suggests neither proper technical audits nor adequate vendor oversight. The mainstream tech press typically frames such stories as minor privacy lapses—inconvenient perhaps, but not catastrophic. This misses the gravity of the situation. Health insurance exchanges aren't casual websites; they're government portals where Americans make vulnerable disclosures about their medical conditions, family situations, and financial status. That someone visiting to research pregnancy care, mental health coverage, or immigration-related benefits now has that behavior tracked and broadcast to Meta's advertising network represents a fundamental breach of the reasonable privacy expectations citizens should have when accessing government services.

What Else We Know

The fact that race and immigration data are being transmitted adds another dimension: this information has historically been weaponized for discrimination, and placing it in the hands of profit-driven platforms creates obvious risks. The source of the problem points to how government procurement works in practice. State exchanges typically use third-party vendors to operate their websites, and these vendors routinely insert advertising and analytics trackers as a way to either offset costs or generate revenue. State officials, often lacking dedicated cybersecurity or privacy staff, may approve these arrangements with minimal technical review. No one asks hard questions about what gets collected or where it goes. The vendors have financial incentives to gather and share as much data as possible—that's how they monetize government contracts.
