Follow the Money

A forty-year gap between crime and identification suggests either an extraordinary evidentiary challenge or a failure of investigative priority. The Wenatchee Police Department's brief announcement also contains no acknowledgment of whether any suspects were previously identified and cleared, whether investigative missteps occurred, or whether any individual was wrongly implicated during the original investigation. These are standard questions that emerge in cold cases, particularly ones that span multiple generations of law enforcement personnel and changing departmental protocols. The statement references coordination with the Chelan County Prosecutor's Office, the institutional authority responsible for pursuing charges. However, no statement from that office—no position from the prosecutor assigned to the case, no explanation of prosecutorial strategy, and no comment on evidentiary sufficiency—appears in the available public record. This absence is notable because prosecutors, not police departments, determine whether sufficient evidence exists to pursue charges.

What Else We Know

The Wenatchee Police may have a suspect; the county prosecutor may have a very different assessment. Without access to the actual investigative file, the forensic reports, the suspect's identity, the victim's identity, or the specific evidence, the public cannot evaluate whether this "solve" represents genuine closure or merely a department claiming victory based on incomplete facts. The 40-year timeline itself raises a fundamental question: what changed in 2024 that couldn't be addressed in 2023 or 2004 or 1994? Until that question is answered transparently, the announcement amounts to institutional self-congratulation without public accountability.

The Sam Okafor Take
True Crime & Justice

Cold case announcements serve a political function before they serve a truth function, and Wenatchee's vague proclamation reveals the machinery perfectly.

What I find striking is how thoroughly law enforcement can declare a case "solved" while disclosing almost nothing. No victim name. No suspect name. No evidence summary. No explanation of the 40-year gap. This isn't transparency; it's a press release designed to generate a favorable headline without inviting scrutiny.

The pattern here is institutional: police departments benefit enormously from being seen as effective. A solved cold case—even one shrouded in ambiguity—improves department metrics, increases public trust, and justifies budget requests. The Chelan County Prosecutor's Office benefits similarly. Meanwhile, the public is asked to celebrate a "breakthrough" it cannot possibly evaluate.

What readers should understand is that "solved" is a status assigned by the institution that solved it, not a neutral fact. Until a conviction stands, until evidence is tested in court, and until defense counsel has full access to investigative materials, no case is truly solved. Demand the actual case file. Demand the victim's identity, the suspect's identity, and the specific evidence. If Wenatchee Police won't provide it, ask why an institution funded by taxpayers gets to declare victory in private.
