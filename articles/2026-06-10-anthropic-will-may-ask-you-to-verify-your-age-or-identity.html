    Anthropic will (may) ask you to verify your age or identity

    I know, I know, AI is very not-private. But I think this is the wrong direction. I've chosen to use Claude, because they frame them self as 'the good guy'. I don't know if Mistral is better, but I will use the subscription I have to replace Claude in my daily work.

    What they're not telling you: Anthropic's Age Verification Push Exposes the "Good Guy" AI Gambit Anthropic—the AI company founded by former OpenAI executives Dario and Daniela Amodei that built its brand on ethical AI principles—is now asking users to submit age and identity verification data, and the mainstream tech press has barely noticed because the narrative doesn't fit the redemption story they've been selling. The policy shift, buried in terms-of-service language, represents a fundamental contradiction that deserves scrutiny. Anthropic's founding pitch to users and investors was explicitly positioned against the move-fast-and-break-things culture that defined OpenAI under Sam Altman.

    What the Documents Show

    The company marketed Claude as the responsible alternative, the AI built with constitutional AI methods and safety-first engineering. Users chose Anthropic because they believed the company meant something different. That trust is now being tested by the same infrastructure demands that plague every other surveillance-dependent platform. What the mainstream framing misses is simple: this isn't a technical necessity. Age and identity verification systems are policy choices, not infrastructure requirements.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    They require users to submit government-issued documents or other identifying information to a company whose security track record remains untested at scale. Anthropic hasn't disclosed what data retention policies govern this information, who has access to it, or how it integrates with their existing data pipelines. The sourced Reddit discussion shows the immediate user response—defection. One user with an active subscription stated explicitly they would migrate to Mistral, a competitor, rather than comply. The pattern here reveals something crucial about how AI companies operate. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta—they all began with public commitments to ethical development.

    What Else We Know

    As user bases grew and regulatory pressure mounted, each has gradually implemented the same data collection and verification infrastructure as traditional tech giants. The difference is one of timing and framing. Anthropic was supposed to be different. That was the entire value proposition. Dario Amodei built Anthropic's reputation on transparency and constitutional principles. But constitutional AI methods don't prevent policy choices about data collection.

    The Rafael Reyes Take
    Conflict & Emerging Wars

    What I find striking is how predictable this pattern has become, and how thoroughly it invalidates the "good company" framing that venture capital uses to differentiate competing platforms. Anthropic's move reveals the actual hierarchy of pressures facing AI companies: regulatory compliance and investor returns outrank user trust, always.

    The companies and investors who benefit from this narrative are the ones selling the story itself. They need you to believe that one company is genuinely different, genuinely better, so that when it inevitably consolidates user data and implements verification systems, you'll see it as reluctant necessity rather than deliberate strategy.

    What you need to understand is this: there is no AI company exempt from these pressures. The question isn't which platform to trust. The question is whether you should submit identity verification to any of them, and what you're actually trading for access. Watch what Anthropic does with the verification data they collect. Demand transparency on retention, access, and integration. That's the only metric that matters.
