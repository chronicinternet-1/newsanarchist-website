What they're not telling you: # After Town Bans Flock, Councilmember Crashes Out, Proposes Internet, Phone Ban ## SECTION 1 Councilmember Jeff Flowers of Bandera, Texas is threatening to ban cell phones, the internet, and all outward-facing cameras from a town of 900 people because residents voted to stop government surveillance—and he's framing it as the logical endpoint of privacy advocacy. The Bandera city council voted 3-2 last week to terminate its contract with Flock Safety, the license plate reader surveillance company, after months of public outcry. Residents attended multiple council meetings demanding the removal of eight AI-powered cameras installed on poles throughout town—infrastructure funded by a state grant.

What the Documents Show

The technology proved so unpopular that vandals destroyed the poles repeatedly, forcing the town to absorb replacement costs. The vote was unambiguous: Bandera's majority wanted out. Flowers, who dissented from the 3-2 decision, responded with what amounts to a ransom note disguised as civic philosophy. In a letter published by the Bandera Bulletin, he announced plans to introduce something he's calling the "Bandera Declaration of Digital Independence"—a package of ordinances that would ban "all cellular and GPS-capable devices for all operations within city limits," "all outward facing cameras," and "all internet services and electronic record-keeping." He wants the town to operate on 1880s infrastructure: paper ledgers and cash transactions only. The move is transparently retaliatory.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Flowers claims he's merely taking privacy advocates to their "logical conclusion," but what he's actually doing is weaponizing an absurdist proposal to punish residents for rejecting his preferred surveillance system. His letter reveals the rhetorical strategy: he equates opposition to government license plate readers with an irrational demand for total technological elimination. "Comparing a neighbor's desire for a safe street to a dark chapter of history is a classic case of comparing apples to oranges," Flowers wrote, referencing residents who invoked Nazi surveillance rhetoric to oppose the Flock cameras. This is a false choice, and Flowers knows it. Nobody in Bandera voted to ban all technology. They voted to reject one specific corporate surveillance tool sold to them as essential for "community safety" and "accountability." Flock Safety positioned itself as the reasonable middle ground between lawlessness and totalitarianism—a framing that Flowers is now inverting to make residents appear fanatical.

What Else We Know

What the mainstream coverage of this story has missed is that Flowers is not an outlier. He represents a growing cohort of local officials who view rejection of surveillance technology as an existential threat to their power. When residents refuse a surveillance tool, officials often respond with escalation or absurdist performative legislation designed to make privacy concerns look ridiculous. Flowers' proposal isn't a genuine policy vision; it's a hissy fit with parliamentary procedure. The real story is that a councilmember is using his position to punish democratic dissent through political theater. The Bandera city council meeting where Flowers plans to introduce these proposals has not yet been scheduled.
