What they're not telling you: # EFF Launches New Offline Campaign for Saudi Wikipedian Osama Khalid Declassified Saudi judicial records reveal a pattern of arbitrary sentencing increases that suggest political pressure rather than legal consistency in cases involving online speech critics. Osama Khalid's case exposes a mechanism rarely discussed in Western coverage of Saudi Arabia: the weaponization of vague national security laws against people whose only documented "crime" is sharing factual information online. The 37-year-old began editing Wikipedia Arabic at twelve and grew into a respected open-source contributor, translator, and technology advocate who worked on projects like EFF's HTTPS Everywhere—encryption tools designed to protect ordinary internet users.

What the Documents Show

His blog documented observations about Saudi governance, women's rights, and surveillance infrastructure. Nothing in the available record indicates he engaged in organizing, hacking, or violence. Yet in July 2020, amid COVID-19 lockdowns when oversight was minimal, Saudi authorities detained him without explanation. The sentencing trajectory itself is the story mainstream outlets gloss over. Khalid initially received five years.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

On appeal, prosecutors requested and received 32 years—a 540 percent increase. In 2023, it dropped to 25 years. By September 2024, it fell again to 14 years. This pattern—dramatic increases followed by reductions—does not reflect judicial review discovering new evidence. According to ALQST, the Saudi human rights organization leading his release campaign and co-signers of an April statement with EFF, this volatility "underscores the arbitrary manner in which sentencing is carried out in the Saudi judicial system." Translation: his sentence appears determined by political temperature, not law. His Wikipedia contributions on Loujain al-Hathloul, a women's rights activist, and al-Ha'ir prison—a facility documented by human rights groups for torture—touched subjects the Saudi state treats as state secrets despite international scrutiny.

What Else We Know

What distinguishes Khalid's case is the specificity of his persecution. He wasn't imprisoned for organizing dissent or coordinating protests. He was imprisoned for making information accessible—for translating concepts like encryption, for documenting public human rights concerns, for the basic act of sharing. His blog post criticizing government surveillance of encrypted platforms particularly matters: it suggests authorities viewed his technical literacy itself as a threat to state control. EFF's decision to launch an offline campaign acknowledges that online advocacy has reached its limits when the target state controls the entire digital infrastructure and judicial system simultaneously. The broader implication is straightforward and urgent: Khalid's case demonstrates that in states lacking independent judiciaries, free speech protections exist only as long as they remain invisible.
