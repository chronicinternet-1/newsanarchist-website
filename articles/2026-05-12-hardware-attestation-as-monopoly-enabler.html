What they're not telling you: Hardware Attestation as Monopoly Enabler Major tech companies are quietly deploying hardware attestation technologies that lock users into their ecosystems while creating barriers competitors cannot overcome, according to ongoing discussions in developer communities. Hardware attestation—the practice of embedding verification mechanisms directly into computer chips—is presented by tech giants as a security feature protecting users from malware and unauthorized access. The mainstream narrative, amplified in corporate marketing and mainstream tech publications, focuses exclusively on these safety benefits.

The Take
Diana Reeves · Corporate Watchdog & Markets

# THE TAKE: Hardware Attestation's Monopoly Trap Intel and AMD aren't selling security. They're selling gatekeeping. Hardware attestation—cryptographic proof that code runs on "genuine" chips—sounds neutral. It's not. By embedding verification authority into silicon, these duopolists convert technical standards into legal moats. Microsoft, Apple, and Google stack these attestations into their platforms, creating a three-layer lock: the CPU manufacturer certifies the chip, the OS vendor certifies the software, and the cloud provider certifies the environment. Independent developers can't compete. You can't run Spotify's algorithm on your own server and attest it runs uncompromised. You *can't* build a competing cloud without Intel's blessing. This isn't about protecting users from malware. It's about protecting market share from disruption. The security theater obscures naked consolidation.

What the Documents Show

What gets systematically downplayed is the monopoly mechanics baked into these systems. Once a company controls the attestation hardware, they control which software runs, which updates install, and ultimately which competitors can operate on that device. The user doesn't own their device in any meaningful sense; they rent access to it under terms the manufacturer can unilaterally change. The technical implementation reveals the trap. When hardware attestation chains verification authority to a specific corporation, that company becomes the sole gatekeeper for what the device considers "legitimate." Third-party repairs become impossible because replacement parts lack the manufacturer's cryptographic signatures.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Independent software developers cannot distribute applications without manufacturer approval. Security researchers cannot audit how the system actually works—doing so voids warranties or disables core functionality. This is fundamentally different from traditional software locks, which can theoretically be bypassed through legitimate means. Hardware-level control is, by design, user-proof. The business model is straightforward: lock in users, eliminate competitor access, extract maximum value. A user with an older phone manufactured by Company A cannot simply switch to Company B's operating system on the same hardware—the attestation layer prevents it.

What Else We Know

The device becomes worthless, forcing replacement purchases. Repair shops cannot compete because they cannot obtain legitimate attestation credentials. App developers must negotiate with the gatekeeper on the gatekeeper's terms. This extends the monopoly not just to devices, but to entire ecosystems of services and accessories. Developers discussing these issues on technical forums consistently note that this infrastructure is being normalized without substantial public debate. The framing—security, safety, protection—is genuinely difficult to argue against in the abstract.
