Trump Posts Article Laying Out: "Here's How To Crush Tehran In Three Moves"

What they're not telling you: # Trump Amplifies Military Strike Proposal Against Iran Without Strategic Resolution Mechanism President Trump on Thursday posted to Truth Social a New York Post article authored by Richard Goldberg of the Foundation for Defense of Democracies, outlining a three-point military strategy against Iran, despite the absence of any documented diplomatic framework or escalation protocol governing what such strikes would accomplish. The article, originally published May 1st, resurfaces as Trump administration officials continue rhetorical threats of "massive military strikes" while maintaining no publicly disclosed mechanism for translating military action into negotiated settlement. Goldberg, whose institutional affiliation—the Foundation for Defense of Democracies—operates both as a think tank and maintains FDD Action as a registered lobbying entity, frames military escalation as a prerequisite to negotiating leverage.

What the Documents Show

The same organization placed Nick Stewart, identified as a former senior official from FDD Action's lobbying arm, directly into Trump's Iran negotiating team. This structural overlap between policy advocacy, military strategy authorship, and personnel placement within official negotiating positions establishes a direct institutional pipeline from advocacy to executive decision-making. The timing reveals operational inconsistency. Trump's post arrives after Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei issued explicit directives constraining uranium enrichment stockpiles, yet Trump's amplification of strike proposals contains no demonstrated response to this constraint—no documented assessment of whether Iran's action satisfied specified conditions, no public criteria for evaluating compliance, and no articulated off-ramp from military threats. The source material documents what amounts to day 83 of threatened military action without materialization.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

The White House maintains publicly stated objectives—reopening the Strait of Hormuz, forcing Iranian compliance with unspecified terms—while repeatedly threatening escalation that does not occur. This pattern, according to the source, repeats weekly. No declassified intelligence assessment appears in the source material supporting either the military necessity of the three-point strategy or the feasibility of its execution. No National Security Council memo establishing decision criteria exists in the provided documentation. The military institution itself—the Joint Chiefs of Staff, Central Command—generates no visible public position on the strategic logic of the proposed strikes. What the mainstream framing misses entirely is this: the primary institutional actor shaping Iran military strategy is not the Department of Defense or the intelligence community, but a private nonprofit policy organization that derives institutional legitimacy from simultaneous advocacy and strategic authorship.

What Else We Know

Goldberg does not appear in the source material as a government employee subject to classification restrictions or institutional oversight. He operates as a private author whose work directly influences presidential communication strategy, and his organization simultaneously places personnel into official negotiating positions. This represents not policy coordination but institutional capture—the machinery of executive decision-making accepting strategy documents from external advocacy entities without visible competitive analysis or adversarial vetting. --- THE TAKE --- The pattern here is institutional decay masquerading as policy continuity. I find striking not Trump's rhetoric but the absence of any visible mechanism preventing a private policy organization from simultaneously authoring military strategy, lobbying government, and placing personnel into official negotiating positions. This reveals a larger institutional failure: the executive branch's incapacity to maintain separation between external advocacy and internal decision-making.
