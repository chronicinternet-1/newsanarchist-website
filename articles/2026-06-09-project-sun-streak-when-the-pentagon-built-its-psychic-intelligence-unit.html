Project Sun Streak: When the Pentagon Built Its Psychic Intelligence Unit

Newly analyzed declassified documents reveal that the Defense Intelligence Agency ran a secretive remote viewing program that tasked psychics with penetrating foreign military bunkers, locating hostages, and gathering intelligence where satellites and spies could not reach.

Vera Solano AI-ASSISTED June 9, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The classified door opened in 1972 at Stanford Research Institute. Physicists Russell Targ and Harold Puthoff began asking trained individuals to describe distant locations using nothing but mental focus—a discipline they called remote viewing. By 1986, this experimental practice had evolved into something far more concrete: a classified Defense Intelligence Agency operation stationed at Fort Meade, Maryland, tasked with the systematic pursuit of psychic intelligence.

That operation was called Project Sun Streak. Released documents, analyzed comprehensively this month by legal research firm LegalClarity, now reveal what the U.S. military actually believed it could do with remote viewers, how it structured these efforts, and what it thought it had achieved.

Sun Streak emerged from Cold War logic. The program grew out of genuine Cold War anxiety that the Soviet Union was making progress in its own psychic research and that falling behind could create a real intelligence gap. This wasn't theoretical paranoia. Run by the Defense Intelligence Agency out of Fort Meade, Maryland, it represented the fourth name in a continuous line of government-sponsored psychic research stretching back to the 1970s. The operation had gone through several iterations before—Gondola Wish, Grill Flame, Center Lane—each reflecting bureaucratic reorganization and shifting funding priorities.

What made Sun Streak different was scope and operational ambition. Sun Streak had specific operational goals tied to intelligence gaps that satellites, signals intercepts, and human agents couldn't easily fill. The DIA's own briefing materials identified seven categories of tasking, though two dominated the workload: penetrating physically inaccessible targets and cueing other intelligence collection systems to look in the right place. In practical terms, that meant remote viewers were asked to describe the insides of foreign military bunkers, identify equipment layouts in overseas laboratories, and locate objects or people in areas where the U.S. had no other collection capability.

The technical architecture was precise. The core technique was called Coordinate Remote Viewing, a structured six-stage process. A viewer received a set of geographic coordinates and then moved through progressively detailed stages of perception, starting with broad impressions and working toward specific information about the target site. A viewer would enter a meditative state, receiving only coordinates on paper—nothing more. They would then attempt to translate sensory impressions into actionable intelligence.

The program operated from 1986 to 1990, though research into remote viewing at SRI continued in parallel. Once the DIA took control in the mid-1980s, Sun Streak operated as a Department of Defense special access program, with facilities and personnel stationed at Fort Meade, Maryland. The DIA's science and technology component oversaw the effort, handling everything from budget allocation to security clearances for the small group of viewers and support staff involved.

The program produced reports. The catch with results like these is that without an independent way to verify what was "previously unreported" versus what simply matched existing intelligence, it's difficult to separate genuine hits from confirmation bias. This ambiguity followed the program throughout its existence and ultimately shaped the debate over whether it was worth continuing.

One declassified case illustrates the operational scope. A January 1990 remote viewing session tasked viewers with describing the Nazca Lines in Peru. As part of the Defense Intelligence Agency's Project SUN STREAK, in 1990 an agency psychic was tasked with using remote viewing to describe the Nazca lines. Provided with encrypted coordinates, the alleged psychic "had many accurate perceptions of the site and no discernible incorrect ones." This statement is somewhat extraordinary - but probably not for the reasons you imagine. Rather, it's somewhat extraordinary because it's immediately followed by a list of things the remote viewer got wrong.

Another documented case involved Church of England envoy Terry Waite, kidnapped in Beirut in January 1987. A declassified document reveals a remote viewing session conducted under Project Sun Streak targeting Terry Waite, the Church of England envoy kidnapped in Beirut on January 20, 1987. Whether the session produced actionable intelligence remains redacted or ambiguous in the available documents.

The program existed within a strange institutional limbo. It was treated as serious enough to merit Department of Defense resources, military briefings, and operational tasking. Yet it was housed in military intelligence precisely because mainstream science considered it pseudoscience. There was increasing concern about the level of Soviet parapsychology. For many Western scientists, the field was considered pseudoscience, so they had to find a laboratory separate from academia that could house a quiet run classified investigation.

When civilian experts finally evaluated the program in the mid-1990s, judgment was harsh. The CIA commissioned a report by the American Institutes for Research (AIR) that found that remote viewing had not been proved to work by a psychic mechanism, and said it had not been used operationally. The CIA subsequently cancelled and declassified the program. The entire remote viewing infrastructure—Sun Streak, Stargate, and all predecessor programs—was dismantled.

Yet the documents themselves tell a different story about institutional conviction. The DIA did not treat remote viewing as speculation. It treated it as a collection discipline worthy of security clearances, operational tasking, and repeated sessions. Personnel were selected for remote viewing aptitude. Training programs were developed. Standardized protocols governed sessions. Evaluation sheets measured accuracy. This was not a fringe operation hiding in a corner—it was structured like any other intelligence discipline.

The documents now available through the CIA's Freedom of Information Act reading room and the National Security Archive offer the fullest glimpse yet into what a modern government actually thought consciousness might do in the service of national security. For researchers on the metaphysical beat, the revelation isn't that remote viewing worked or didn't work. It's that the U.S. military spent decades building institutional infrastructure around the assumption that it might. The question of whether it ever delivered actionable intelligence remains contested. The question of what that institutional commitment reveals about how we understand human consciousness is just beginning.

The Vera Solano Take
Metaphysical & Consciousness

I've spent fifteen years studying consciousness research, and this story crystallizes something crucial: governments don't fund large, classified, sustained programs around phenomena they believe in "maybe." The fact that the DIA operated Project Sun Streak for four years with trained personnel, standardized protocols, and operational tasking—not in a basement lab, but as part of the military-intelligence apparatus—tells us the American security state took remote viewing seriously as a possible intelligence method. Whether it worked is secondary. What matters is that institutional actors with budget authority and security clearances believed consciousness could be weaponized for intelligence gathering. That conviction outlasted skepticism for decades. If nothing changes—if we continue treating remote viewing as either pseudoscientific fringe or declassified curiosity—we miss the real story: how far official power went to explore the metaphysical frontier, and why that exploration stopped being acceptable to mainstream institutions only when outside evaluation was forced upon them.
