The Gum Ruse: How a Fake Flavor Survey Closed Two Cold-Case Murders

Forty years after a Washington state killer raped and strangled two women in separate attacks, detectives finally broke the case by posing as gum company reps — and waiting for a convicted sexual sadist to spit into a ramekin.

Sam Okafor AI-ASSISTED June 3, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

On May 13, 2026, a Snohomish County Superior Court judge sentenced Mitchell Gaff, 68, to a minimum of 50 years in prison and a maximum life sentence — guaranteeing he will die behind bars — for the murders of Susan Vesey in 1980 and Judith "Judy" Weaver in 1984. The sentence closed two cold cases that had tormented Everett, Washington, families for more than four decades, cases in which law enforcement had once pointed fingers at innocent men while the actual killer walked free, legally changed his name, and registered as a sex offender under the radar.

The story of how Gaff was finally caught is one of the most ingenious and chilling pieces of investigative work I have seen in my twelve years at the Department of Justice. When CODIS — the national Combined DNA Index System — returned Gaff as a potential match to DNA evidence from Weaver's 1984 murder, Everett Police Department Detective Susan Logothetti knew she needed a second, confirmatory sample. Gaff was a level 3 sex offender who had legally changed his name to Sam Price. He barely left his house except to visit a nearby grocery store. Surveillance wasn't yielding an opportunity to collect a discarded cigarette butt or beverage cup — the usual covert methods.

So in January 2024, three undercover detectives knocked on Gaff's door, posing as representatives of a gum company conducting research on flavor preferences. Gaff welcomed them in. He was enthusiastic, sampling different sticks of gum with apparent enjoyment. When the time came to try a new flavor, one detective held out a small dish — a ramekin — for Gaff to discard the used piece. He obliged without suspicion. Logothetti, who was present, later told CNN she had "never been a part of anything this elaborate" and that the gum ruse was something she "thought was kind of crazy at the time." But it worked. DNA extracted from that discarded chewing gum was consistent with DNA found on Weaver's body, on three pieces of her clothing, and on the murder weapon itself, according to charging papers.

Gaff was arrested in May 2024 and charged with Weaver's murder. What happened next reveals just how cold cases cascade when investigators follow the science without blinking. Weaver's 1984 murder and the 1980 murder of 21-year-old Susan Vesey had originally been treated as unrelated cases. Vesey was a married mother of two children, both under the age of two, when Gaff found her apartment door unlocked one night in July 1980. He hid in a bedroom closet, then emerged with a knife from her kitchen. Her husband Ken, just 23 at the time, found her body on the bedroom floor the next morning — their 15-week-old baby lying unharmed on the bed beside her.

For years, Vesey's brother-in-law Gerry had lived under a cloud of suspicion in that murder. It was only after Gerry died and Ken Vesey called Logothetti in January 2025 to share that news — effectively clearing his own brother — that Logothetti, who by her own admission wasn't familiar with the Vesey case, submitted that evidence to the Washington State Patrol Crime Lab. Detectives noticed "startling similarities" between the two killings. By March 2026, the DNA from a piece of white electrical cord used to bind Vesey came back as a confirmed match to Gaff.

Gaff had already been in the CODIS database for an unrelated reason: in 1984, just three months after he killed Weaver, he broke into a home and raped two teenage sisters. He served 21 years in prison for those crimes and was diagnosed as a sexual sadist. He had also been previously charged with burglary and assault for an attack in 1979. His history of sexual violence was extensive and well-documented. Yet none of it had connected him to Vesey or Weaver — because in 1980 and 1984, as court papers bluntly state, "DNA was not yet a forensic tool available to law enforcement."

On April 16, 2026, Gaff pleaded guilty to two counts of first-degree murder with premeditated intent in Snohomish County Superior Court. He described the murders in detail in open court. At sentencing, he addressed the families of his victims. "No one did anything to deserve me coming into their lives," he said. It was the kind of statement that sounds like contrition but lands like a confession of monstrous indifference — the bare minimum acknowledgment of carnage delivered after a lifetime of evading accountability.

Vesey's daughter, Debra Newton, was two years old when her mother was murdered. She is now a middle-aged woman. "For over 45 years, my family has grappled with not knowing who had taken her from us," she said in her victim impact statement. Weaver's niece, Dawnyel Wilder Harris, wrote that her one wish was that Gaff "never sees daylight again." Snohomish County Prosecutor Craig Mattheson put it plainly to the judge: "It could've been anyone" — any door left unlocked, any window left open.

And that is the part of this story that should keep investigators awake. Gaff had previously admitted to committing multiple uncharged rapes during earlier trials. The true scope of what he did may never be fully known. Logothetti said that for closure to happen, the Weaver and Vesey cases "just needed science to catch up." That's true. But science only catches up when someone like Logothetti refuses to let a case go cold for good — when she still picks up the phone after a victim's husband calls years later, when she authorizes a crazy-sounding gum ruse on a hunch, when she watches a killer spit into a ramekin and manages not to give herself away.

I spent twelve years at DOJ watching cold cases stall not because evidence didn't exist, but because no one was willing to invest the resources, creativity, or institutional patience to crack them. The Mitchell Gaff case is a template — and a rebuke. It shows what's possible when a detective like Susan Logothetti refuses to accept that a 40-year-old case is just a dead file. The gum ruse wasn't magic; it was methodical, funded, and legally executed. The DNA science was already in place. What was missing, for decades, was the sustained attention. But here's the uncomfortable arithmetic: for every Mitchell Gaff who gets caught through a CODIS hit and a brilliant covert operation, there are dozens of cold case murders — disproportionately affecting women, disproportionately affecting Black and Indigenous victims — where no detective is making that phone call, no grant is funding that DNA test, no undercover team is knocking on a suspect's door. Gaff is now serving 50-to-life. That is justice. But the families who will never get that phone call, because their loved one's case wasn't assigned to a Susan Logothetti? They are the cost of a system that treats cold cases as optional. If nothing changes, the winners are the killers who never got caught.
