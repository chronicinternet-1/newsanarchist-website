Privacy focused alternative for Google Flights?

Google flights is pretty convenient but of course they are doing something nefarious every time you use it. Is there an alternative?

Flights collects and retains passenger search patterns, route preferences, and price sensitivity data through mechanisms that users cannot fully audit or disable without abandoning the service entirely. The question surfacing in privacy communities—whether alternatives exist to Google Flights—reflects a gap between what Google discloses in its privacy policies and what its infrastructure actually captures. Google's public documentation states the company collects "search queries and related information" through Flights, but does not specify the granularity of behavioral tracking, the duration of retention, or the internal integration of this data with Google's broader advertising and location services.

What the Documents Show

Google Flights operates as a meta-search aggregator, pulling real-time pricing from airline reservation systems while simultaneously logging which users searched for which routes, at what times, with what flexibility parameters, and crucially—which price points triggered booking behavior. This transactional metadata becomes addressable targeting intelligence. A user searching for economy flights from New York to Los Angeles on specific dates creates a behavioral record: travel patterns, scheduling flexibility, budget constraints, and destination frequency. Google retains this data according to its standard retention policies, which vary by product but typically operate on multi-year cycles for analytics purposes. The technical infrastructure enabling this collection is largely invisible.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Google Flights uses tracking pixels, session identifiers, and cross-domain cookies that persist across Google's ecosystem—Search, Maps, Gmail, YouTube. When a user searches flights while logged into a Google account, that search attaches to a persistent identifier linked to location history, email patterns, and search behavior across all Google services. The privacy policy discloses aggregation at the policy level but not the specificity at the technical level. Alternatives exist but operate under different transparency constraints. Kayak, Momondo, Skyscanner, and Expedia perform similar aggregation functions but are owned by different parent companies—Booking Holdings, Expedia Group respectively—each with their own data retention and sharing practices. Open-source and privacy-focused projects like Flightradar24 operate different business models: Flightradar24 is aviation tracking, not search aggregation.

What Else We Know

True privacy alternatives would require either using airline websites directly (reducing comparison efficiency) or using services that explicitly delete search data (a shrinking category). The mainstream framing of this issue treats Google's data collection as a trade-off: convenience in exchange for targeted advertising. What this framing obscures is the baseline question—whether users can access equivalent search functionality without the collection mechanism at all. The answer, based on available tools, is that no mainstream alternative provides both search functionality and credible data minimization. Kayak and Skyscanner offer marginally better privacy policies than Google, but still retain booking and search data for extended periods. What users cannot verify is whether alternative aggregators share data with Google through backend relationships, whether they sell to data brokers, or what their actual retention timelines are in practice versus policy.

The Marcus Webb Take
Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

What strikes me about this question is how it exposes the false choice embedded in consumer tech. Users ask for alternatives not because privacy-respecting flight search is technically impossible—it isn't—but because every functional alternative operates within the same data-extraction infrastructure. This isn't an accident of competition; it's the business model.

The pattern here is institutional: Google, Booking Holdings, and Expedia benefit from the assumption that data collection is the price of service. They benefit from privacy policies that technically disclose practices while remaining functionally unauditable. They benefit from users believing alternatives don't exist or don't work.

What actually happened is the advertising-surveillance business model colonized an entire category of commercial software. Booking platforms, search, maps, calendar—each integrated into the same surveillance infrastructure because surveillance became more profitable than the service itself.

Watch what happens when someone builds a flight search engine and publicly commits to zero data retention. Watch whether it can acquire users. Watch whether Google or the carriers actively obstruct it. That absence or obstruction will tell you everything about whether this is actually a technical problem or a policy choice.
