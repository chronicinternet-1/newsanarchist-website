    The NSA Wants Carte Blanche for Warrantless Surveillance

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 24, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: THE NSA WANTS CARTE BLANCHE FOR WARRANTLESS SURVEILLANCE The National Security Agency has formally requested statutory authority to conduct bulk surveillance of Americans without judicial warrants, according to classified position papers obtained by civil liberties organizations and reviewed by multiple intelligence oversight bodies. The request, filed through the Office of the Director of National Intelligence and channeled to the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, seeks to formalize what NSA officials argue is already implicit operational authority under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act. The agency's legal division, led by officials whose names remain classified in public filings, has argued that existing interpretations of FISA's "incidental collection" provisions create ambiguity about which communications can be retained and searched without subsequent judicial approval.

    What the Documents Show

    Rather than clarify the existing framework through the FISA Court—the secret tribunal established to review surveillance requests—NSA leadership is requesting that Congress grant explicit blanket authorization. The technical mechanism matters here because it defines infrastructure scale. Under current FISA Section 702 operations, the NSA collects communications that transit through U.S. telecommunications infrastructure when one party is reasonably believed to be a foreign national outside U.S. That collection happens without individual warrants.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    What the agency now seeks is statutory language permitting warrantless searches of that already-collected database using U.S. person identifiers—names, email addresses, phone numbers, IP addresses—without triggering the minimization procedures that theoretically protect Americans from surveillance. Documents reviewed by the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board indicate the NSA's request specifically targets what it calls "query authority expansion." Current rules require NSA analysts to document queries of Americans' communications within collected foreign intelligence data. The proposed change would eliminate that documentation requirement for queries deemed to serve "national security" purposes, a classification determination made by NSA personnel themselves with no external review mechanism. Three points deserve emphasis because they do not appear in the mainstream coverage of this request. First, the proposal bypasses the FISA Court entirely—it does not ask for a new warrant standard or a modified threshold for judicial approval.

    What Else We Know

    It asks Congress to authorize surveillance that requires no court approval at all. Second, the request includes no technological limitations. NSA could theoretically query bulk surveillance databases for every communication mentioning a political organization, religious group, or labor union, provided an analyst asserts national security justification. Third, the mechanism already exists in draft form within NSA's data architecture; the agency is not requesting new technical capability but rather seeking legal cover for systems already operational in test environments at Fort Meade, Maryland. The official rationale—preventing terrorism and counterintelligence threats—obscures what the documents actually authorize: a surveillance infrastructure with no external constraint, no warrant requirement, and no court review. The distinction between asking permission to build a surveillance capability and asking permission to use one already built is not semantic.

    The Marcus Webb Take
    Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

    The pattern here is institutional fait accompli disguised as a request. The NSA doesn't want permission to surveil Americans—it already does that at scale. What it wants is legal retroactive cover for systems deployed without explicit statutory authorization, which is far more revealing about how the surveillance state actually operates than any debate about warrant standards.

    I find striking the absence of any technological constraint in the proposal. Why? Because constraints require ongoing oversight. Blanket authorization requires only one legislative vote, then bureaucratic silence. The agencies that benefit from this arrangement are obvious: NSA, CIA, FBI. What's less obvious is that this proposal also protects telecommunications carriers who would otherwise face liability for furnishing customer data without warrants. Section 702 shielded them; expanded warrantless query authority extends that shield indefinitely.

    The larger institutional failure is this: Congress created the FISA Court in 1978 to review classified surveillance. Instead of using that mechanism to authorize new capability, NSA is asking Congress to legislate away the court's jurisdiction entirely. Readers should watch whether any FISA Court judge publicly objects to being rendered irrelevant—and whether Congress votes to make that irrelevance official.
