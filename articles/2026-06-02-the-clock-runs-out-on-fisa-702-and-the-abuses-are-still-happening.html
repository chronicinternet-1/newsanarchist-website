The Clock Runs Out on FISA 702 — and the Abuses Are Still Happening

A classified FISA Court opinion from March 17, 2026 reportedly reveals that the FBI, NSA, and CIA are still running unauthorized filter tools to conduct warrantless backdoor searches on Americans' communications — the very abuse Congress was told had been fixed — and with a June 12 statutory deadline now ten days away, the Trump administration has refused to declassify the opinion before Congress votes on whether to hand these agencies another multi-year blank check.

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 2, 2026 7 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

WASHINGTON — Ten days from now, on June 12, 2026, the United States government's most powerful domestic surveillance authority will either expire or be renewed. What Congress decides will affect the private communications of every American with a phone, an email address, or a social media account. What makes that decision harder — and more urgent — is what Congress still does not officially know.

A Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court (FISC) opinion dated March 17, 2026 remains classified. Its contents, reported by The New York Times and The Washington Post based on unclassified talking points the Trump administration circulated to House Republicans, describe a systemic problem that intelligence officials had publicly claimed was resolved: the FBI, NSA, and CIA have been using unauthorized "filter tools" to conduct queries of Americans' communications swept up under Section 702 of FISA — without the attorney approvals, supervisory sign-offs, audit trails, or written justifications required by law.

That is not a historical footnote. That is the present state of affairs, according to the FISA Court's own March 17 ruling.

Section 702 authorizes the NSA to collect the communications of foreign intelligence targets located outside the United States. The statute is nominally aimed at foreigners. But because Americans communicate with foreigners, their calls, texts, and emails are swept up in bulk — without any individualized warrant. The FBI, CIA, NSA, and National Counterterrorism Center then conduct what the government euphemistically calls "U.S. person queries," searching through that collected data for Americans' communications. Civil liberties advocates and constitutional scholars have long described this as a backdoor around the Fourth Amendment.

Congress last reauthorized Section 702 on April 20, 2024, via the Reforming Intelligence and Securing America Act, known as RISAA. That law included modest reforms: FBI agents would need attorney or supervisory approval before querying Americans' data, would be required to record reasons for each query, and those queries would be subject to internal audits.

Within months, those reforms were being systematically circumvented.

In August 2024, Department of Justice overseers discovered that the FBI had been quietly using a querying tool that allowed agents to access Americans' communications without adhering to any of RISAA's procedural requirements. U.S. person queries run through this tool were not counted, tracked, or audited as the law demanded. The FBI did not record reasons for these queries. It did not obtain required attorney or supervisory approvals. According to the Brennan Center for Justice, which has been tracking this litigation closely, it took months for the DOJ to shut the tool down — and even then, the March 2026 FISA Court opinion reportedly found that "the problem the DOJ claimed to have fixed in early 2025 is in fact ongoing — and extends beyond the FBI."

The scope of that statement deserves to sit on the page for a moment. It is not just the FBI this time. The NSA and CIA are reportedly using similar filter tools to search through Section 702 data for Americans' communications. The systemic violations of RISAA are ongoing. And because queries run through these tools are not being counted, the reported statistics on U.S. person queries submitted to Congress — including a 35 percent increase in FBI queries from 5,518 in 2024 to 7,413 in 2025, per a letter from acting FBI assistant director Ted Groves to the Senate Judiciary Committee — represent an incomplete picture of actual surveillance activity.

This is the classified opinion that bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee leaders want released before the June 12 vote.

On April 30, when Congress passed its latest 45-day emergency extension of Section 702 by a House vote of 261-111, Democratic Sen. Ron Wyden of Oregon announced that a letter co-signed by Intelligence Committee Chairman Tom Cotton (R-AR) and Vice Chairman Mark Warner (D-VA) had been sent to Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard and acting Attorney General Todd Blanche. The letter demanded that the FISC opinion be publicly released within 15 days — in time to inform Senate deliberations before the June 12 deadline. That 15-day window has now expired.

As of this writing, the opinion has not been declassified. Wyden's office has stated that the DNI neither met the deadline nor acknowledged it. "Congress cannot reauthorize Section 702 without an open, public debate about how this surveillance law affects the constitutional rights of Americans," Wyden said. He added that failing to declassify the opinion "shows this administration's utter contempt for Congress and its dedication to hiding the facts about surveillance from the American people."

Gabbard, for her part, was reported by Breitbart News on May 21 to be "working diligently" on the declassification. ODNI issued a statement saying the process must be "meticulously conducted and coordinated with all relevant Intelligence Community agencies, due to the amount of sensitive intelligence information." Cotton told Breitbart he was "confident ODNI is moving forward as fast as possible while still protecting sources and methods."

The program's reauthorization debate has been a legislative trainwreck. Congress knew Section 702 expired April 20. It passed a 10-day emergency patch on April 17. It then failed to pass a three-year extension because House Republicans attached an unrelated provision banning the Federal Reserve from issuing a central bank digital currency — a poison pill Senate Majority Leader John Thune declared dead on arrival. The House then passed the 45-day extension on April 30 while the Senate passed it unanimously, pushing the new deadline to June 12. The Trump administration has throughout pushed for a clean reauthorization — no warrant requirement, no new oversight mechanisms, no structural reforms.

The reform debate has a documented record beneath it. Between 2020 and early 2022, FBI personnel conducted more than 278,000 searches of Section 702 databases that did not meet legal standards. Those queries targeted, among others, Black Lives Matter protesters, January 6 Capitol riot participants, congressional campaign donors, journalists, and members of Congress. The FISA Court characterized those as "persistent and widespread violations." A warrant requirement amendment failed by a single vote in the House in 2024.

Now, in the 2026 cycle, the oversight apparatus that exposed those violations has itself been gutted. In May 2025, FBI Director Kash Patel disbanded the Office of Internal Auditing, the internal watchdog created by then-Attorney General William Barr in 2020 specifically in response to prior FISA abuse findings. That office had been the primary mechanism for detecting noncompliant query patterns at an aggregate level. It no longer exists. Meanwhile, Inspector General offices government-wide collectively lost 16.6 percent of their workforce between January 2025 and early 2026.

The reform camp in Congress — a bipartisan coalition that has sought a warrant requirement for nearly two decades — has put forward proposals that include written judicial authorization before federal law enforcement can access the content of Americans' communications collected under 702, along with criminal penalties of up to five years for intentional misuse. Intelligence officials have argued that any warrant requirement would impede counterterrorism operations. Former NSA General Counsel Stewart Baker testified to that effect before Congress in January.

What is not in dispute: the June 12 deadline is real, Congress has no consensus, a secret court opinion detailing ongoing surveillance abuses has been withheld from the public, the internal watchdog is gone, and the administration wants renewal without conditions. That is the transaction being contemplated.

I spent years inside the apparatus. I know what these filter tools do — they are not administrative conveniences. They are mechanisms for evading oversight while maintaining the operational ability to search Americans' communications at will. The FISA Court apparently said so in March. Congress has not been allowed to read it. Here is the structure of the problem: the compliance machinery — the FBI's Office of Internal Auditing, the Inspector General infrastructure, the query audit trails — has been systematically dismantled over the past 18 months. The reported query numbers submitted to Congress are now unverifiable because the tool generating the undercounted queries was operated outside the auditing framework. We are being asked to accept compliance assurances from an agency that disbanded its own compliance office. If Congress renews Section 702 cleanly on June 12, without a warrant requirement and without the declassified court opinion, the winner is not national security. The winner is unaccountable executive power. The NSA, FBI, and CIA will hold, for at least another year, warrantless access to the communications of every American who has ever exchanged a message with anyone abroad — with no independent mechanism to detect when that access is abused. The people who win when nothing changes are the people running the tools.
