    California moves to exempt Linux from its upcoming age-verification law after backlash over forcing operating systems to collect users’ ages

    &;
    Jordan Calloway
    Investigative journalist
    May 26, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: California's Age-Verification Law Proves the State Can't Write Tech Regulation Without Breaking the Internet California quietly moved to exempt Linux from its upcoming age-verification mandate after the operating system's developers pointed out the state had written a law so technically illiterate it would require Linux distributions to spy on millions of users' birthdates—a requirement that would either shatter the open-source ecosystem or turn California into a testing ground for mass surveillance infrastructure. The exemption itself is the story. It doesn't exist yet in final form, which means California legislators drafted an age-verification law without understanding what they were regulating.

    What the Documents Show

    The bill's language would have technically applied verification requirements to any software that provides access to age-restricted content online. Under that definition, Linux—which runs servers, browsers, and distribution channels worldwide—would have been classified as a covered entity required to collect, store, and verify user age data. The Linux Foundation's technical team flagged this in public comment: the law would force an operating system to become a data collection mechanism, which is impossible to implement without either breaking the system's architecture or creating a centralized surveillance layer that violates Linux's distributed-by-design philosophy. California legislators are now moving toward an exemption, according to reporting, but the damage reveals a systemic failure in how state governments regulate technology. The officials responsible—the bill's drafters and the committees that advanced it without technical vetting—treated "the internet" as a monolith rather than a stack of interdependent systems.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    There is no public record yet of which state legislators requested technical review before advancing the bill, or whether anyone in the legislative counsel's office flagged the architectural impossibility of the requirement. What makes this actionable is that it's not unique. California has passed similarly broad tech regulations—from data privacy frameworks to algorithmic accountability bills—that later required amendment or clarification when their language collided with technical reality. The state treats technology policy like consumer protection law, applying liability frameworks designed for corporations selling products to distributed systems, open-source projects, and infrastructure layers that don't fit the liability model. The Linux exemption also exposes why industry lobbying works in tech regulation. Linux's developers got heard because they have institutional credibility and organized response capacity.

    What Else We Know

    Smaller open-source projects, privacy tools, and infrastructure maintained by volunteers won't have that access. They'll either comply with impossible requirements, geofence away from California, or disappear. The exemption protects the ecosystem—but only the visible parts of it. Most striking: there's no evidence California required independent technical review before drafting this bill. No architecture assessment. Just policy written by people reasoning about technology from first principles, which is how you accidentally criminalize an operating system.

    The Jordan Calloway Take
    Government Secrets & FOIA

    The pattern here is that state legislatures are writing rules for systems they don't understand, then treating the inevitable technical failures as edge cases rather than proof they need different processes. What this exemption reveals is that California's regulatory structure has no built-in mechanism for technical review before bills advance. Committees vote on language they can't evaluate.

    The officials drafting tech policy benefit from this opacity—it lets them claim they're "protecting consumers" without the accountability that comes with understanding what they're actually mandating. The industry benefits differently: large companies hire compliance teams to navigate broken rules, while open-source and smaller projects just leave the state.

    What you need to watch: whether California requires independent technical architecture review before any bill affecting software infrastructure advances to committee. Demand it. That's the actual fix. An exemption for Linux just means the next bill will break something else.
