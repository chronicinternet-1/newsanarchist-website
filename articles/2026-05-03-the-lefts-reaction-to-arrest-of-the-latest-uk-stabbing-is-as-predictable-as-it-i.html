What they're not telling you: # The Left's Reaction To Arrest Of The Latest UK Stabbing Is As Predictable As It Is Disgraceful Progressive activists rushed to condemn police officers for using force against a stabbing suspect before the victims' families had finished seeking medical care. Following an alleged knife attack on two Jewish men in Golders Green, north London, by a 45-year-old British national of Somali origin, video footage emerged showing arresting officers using force to restrain the suspect. Within hours, high-profile critics took to social media.

What the Documents Show

Shola Mos-Shogbamimu, a prominent activist with significant social media reach, posted that officers had committed "contemptible abuse of police power" by kicking the suspect in the head multiple times while he was already incapacitated by Taser. She questioned why the suspect wasn't kept alive for court proceedings, framing the arrest tactics as "disgusting." Green Party leader Zack Polanski amplified similar criticism, describing the incident as officers "violently kicking a mentally ill man in the head when he was already incapacitated." What distinguishes this case is the broader context these critics omitted from their analysis. The suspect had previously been referred to Prevent, the government's counter-radicalization program—a detail that reframes the incident as part of a pattern of security failures rather than a simple policing dispute. The source material notes this detail "depressingly, inevitably," suggesting a recurring problem with how referrals to such programs result in real-world violence. The article's central argument targets a credibility gap: activists conducting real-time judgment of police conduct from their keyboards have never experienced the chaos of an active threat situation.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

The source emphasizes that force cannot be evaluated through a "six-second clip" but must be assessed by necessity in the moment. Officers responding to a marauding knife attack face immediate decisions with limited information and potentially lethal consequences. Their training prioritizes public safety first, followed by suspect safety—a hierarchy that keyboard critics seldom acknowledge when analyzing edited video footage hours or days after the incident. The mainstream framing, as presented in the source material, treats police response as the primary moral question rather than the attack itself or the systemic failures that preceded it. By centering the arrest tactics, progressive commentators shifted focus away from why a Prevent-referred individual allegedly conducted a targeted attack on a specific community. This pattern has broader implications for ordinary Britons.

What Else We Know

When high-profile activists routinely condemn police for using force to stop active threats, they create institutional hesitation that may embolden future attacks. Officers facing social media condemnation for split-second decisions made under extreme duress may second-guess protective action. The real casualty of this dynamic isn't the suspect's comfort during arrest—it's public confidence in police willingness to act decisively when lives hang in the balance.
