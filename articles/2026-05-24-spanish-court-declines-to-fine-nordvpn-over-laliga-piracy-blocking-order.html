    Spanish court declines to fine NordVPN over LaLiga piracy blocking order

    Diana Reeves
    Former SEC examiner
    May 22, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: # blocking-order.html" title="2026-05-23-spanish-court-declines-to-fine-nordvpn-over-laliga-piracy-blocking-order" class="na-ilink">Spanish Court Denies LaLiga's Push to Fine VPN Provider, But the Real Victory Belongs to the Content Cartel A Spanish judge has declined to impose coercive fines against NordVPN for allegedly failing to block pirated LaLiga streams, but this technical reprieve obscures a more troubling question: who benefits when courts impose filtering obligations on foreign companies without proper hearing, and what happens when those companies call the bluff? In February 2024, Commercial Court No. 1 of Córdoba issued what it called a "dynamic injunction"—a real-time blocking order—targeting NordVPN and ProtonVPN specifically.

    What the Documents Show

    The order demanded active IP address blocking of servers hosting unauthorized LaLiga content. The critical procedural detail that vanished from the mainstream account: neither company was permitted to be heard before the order issued, and no immediate right of appeal existed. This is not the rule of law as typically understood in jurisdictions that pretend to care about due process. NordVPN and ProtonVPN—both incorporated outside EU territory—immediately questioned the court's jurisdiction. NordVPN went further, warning publicly that blanket IP-level blocking would create "overblocking," rendering thousands of lawful websites inaccessible across Spain and potentially beyond.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    This is where market structure matters. VPNs operate by routing traffic through intermediary servers. Blocking entire IP ranges is crude enforcement—a sledgehammer approach that inevitably hits innocent bystanders. LaLiga, the Spanish professional soccer league whose broadcasting rights are worth roughly €4.95 billion across a four-year cycle (2022-2026), was unsatisfied with NordVPN's compliance efforts. The league petitioned the court to impose financial penalties—"coercive fines," the mechanism by which courts squeeze compliance from defiant parties. The technical record here is instructive: NordVPN presented evidence that the flagged IP addresses changed rapidly, often within hours, making any static blocking list obsolete before implementation.

    What Else We Know

    Second, they demonstrated that IP-level blocking would disable access to legitimate services. The judge declined LaLiga's request for fines. The court's reasoning, as NordVPN describes it, accepted the technical evidence as "relevant to compliance" but stopped short of declaring either side definitively correct. Instead, the judge found both expert reports deserving of "the same consideration" while reaching "opposite conclusions." Translation: the court found the technical objections plausible enough to deny financial punishment. This matters because LaLiga wanted escalation—it wanted to establish precedent that VPN providers who claim technical impossibility should still face penalties. The league's economic interest is clear: it collects fees from authorized streaming platforms like DAZN (which paid approximately €1.06 billion annually for Spanish rights in the 2022-2026 cycle).

    The Diana Reeves Take
    Corporate Watchdog & Money & Markets

    What strikes me most about this ruling is how it exposes the actual economy of platform enforcement: LaLiga didn't need to win on the merits—it only needed the threat mechanism to function. The real story isn't that NordVPN prevailed. The real story is that a court was willing to issue ex parte blocking orders without hearing the defendants, and it took a company with resources and outside incorporation status to fight back.

    I find the procedural violence here more consequential than the technical dispute. When courts in major markets begin issuing filtering injunctions without adversarial process, they're building infrastructure for regulatory capture. Today it's piracy. Tomorrow it's content moderation, tax compliance reporting, or surveillance cooperation. The pattern is clear: intermediaries get ordered to police networks, penalties escalate until compliance becomes cheaper than resistance, and nobody measures the collateral damage of overblocking.

    LaLiga benefits from the mere existence of this threat. Authorized streaming platforms benefit. What the ruling reveals is institutional failure at the appellate level—no immediate right of appeal in a case affecting foreign companies' fundamental obligations is a red flag about Spanish procedural fairness.

    Watch whether this becomes a model. If other European courts adopt ex parte filtering injunctions without adversarial hearing, we're watching the normalization of platform regulation by judicial decree rather than democratic process.
