How The American System Reshaped The World

Jordan Calloway
Investigative journalist
May 10, 2026 · 3 min read

What they're not telling you: # How The American System Reshaped The World America's founding principle—that freedom exists in the absence of government control—has become almost unrecognizable in modern practice, revealing a fundamental inversion of the system the nation's architects designed. The Founding Fathers constructed a deliberate framework premised on a radically different view of liberty than what dominates contemporary discourse. Their expansive conception of personal freedom wasn't libertarian chaos; it was freedom contingent on moral self-restraint.

The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

# THE TAKE: The "American System" Myth Is Colonialism With Better PR "Freedom in the absence of government control"—spare me the libertarian greeting card. The American System didn't reshape the world through *absence*. It reshaped it through overwhelming state violence: CIA coups (Guatemala '54, Iran '53, Chile '73), IMF structural adjustment programs that gutted national sovereignty, and military occupation zones masquerading as democracies. Worship? Speech? Try telling that to whistleblowers in Guantanamo or journalists prosecuted under the Espionage Act. The U.S. government literally funds domestic surveillance programs (COINTELPRO documented, NSA revealed by Snowden—receipts exist). "Make a living" means working for wages set by corporations the state actively subsidizes while crushing labor organizing. This framework exported American corporate capitalism globally—not universal freedom. That's imperialism wearing a Constitution as costume. The world didn't get reshaped. It got colonized.

What the Documents Show

Citizens possessed the inherent right to worship, speak, write, earn a living, and protect their families without government interference—but only insofar as they refrained from infringing on others' identical rights. This was the architecture: personal liberty coupled with personal responsibility. Self-governance at the individual level was meant to preclude the need for heavy-handed state governance. What's been systematically downplayed in mainstream narratives is how thoroughly this moral framework depended on Judeo-Christian worldview embedded in common law. The legal system wasn't designed as value-neutral machinery; it was rooted explicitly in biblical morality concerning harm, fairness, and justice.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

An American earnestly practicing Christian principles would naturally operate within legal boundaries because the boundaries themselves reflected those principles. The system assumed a population capable of self-restraint would need minimal external constraint. The machinery that governed this balance operated in three interlocking parts: personal freedom, moral conscience, and state-implemented punishment. When individuals successfully governed themselves through moral reasoning, state punishment became unnecessary. Only when members of society abandoned self-control and pursued liberty recklessly—threatening others' freedom—did the state intervene with legal consequences. This wasn't authoritarian overreach; it was a backstop against genuine harm.

What Else We Know

Modern governance has inverted this entirely. Today's system assumes widespread moral failure and pre-emptively constrains behavior through regulation, surveillance, and punishment rather than cultivating the self-restraint the founders considered essential. The mainstream framing treats this expansion as necessary protection or progressive oversight. What's underplayed is the foundational assumption embedded in this shift: contemporary institutions operate as though the population is incapable of self-governance and requires constant external management. The American system exported worldwide—through international institutions, trade agreements, and military presence—carries this same inversion: freedom rhetoric masking expansive control mechanisms. For ordinary people, the implication is stark.
