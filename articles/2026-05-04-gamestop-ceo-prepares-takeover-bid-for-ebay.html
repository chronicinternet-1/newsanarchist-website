What they're not telling you: # GameStop CEO Prepares Takeover Bid For eBay Ryan Cohen, the CEO of GameStop, is preparing an unsolicited $56 billion offer to acquire eBay—a company nearly four times larger than his own—backed by a $20 billion debt commitment from TD Bank. According to Wall Street Journal sources, Cohen has already accumulated a 5% stake in eBay worth approximately $12 billion and is offering $125 per share, representing roughly a 20% premium over Friday's closing price. This aggressive move comes just three months after Cohen publicly signaled his intention to pursue major acquisitions in the consumer and retail space, explicitly stating he wanted to expand GameStop's portfolio far beyond video games and collectibles.

The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

# GameStop's eBay Bid Is Distraction Theater From a Dying Company Let's be clear: This isn't a takeover bid. It's a Hail Mary from a CEO desperately manufacturing relevance. GameStop's Q3 2024 earnings showed collapsing revenue and a cash burn that makes their $56B "offer" laughable—they're operating on fumes while the meme stock crowd cheers. eBay's actual fundamentals? Stable marketplace, real cash flow. GameStop? A physical retailer getting atomized by digital distribution. This reeks of the classic distressed-company playbook: announce something *huge* to trigger retail FOMO, distract from balance sheet hemorrhaging, maybe pump the stock 15% before reality reasserts itself. The SEC filing will reveal what we already know—no financing, no serious board consideration. Just noise. eBay shareholders should laugh this off. GameStop investors should ask harder questions about why their CEO is chasing fantasies instead of fixing the actual business.

What the Documents Show

The leak to the same publication that reported his initial acquisition appetite suggests a calculated strategy—building public narrative momentum before a formal announcement expected as early as this month. What the mainstream financial press has largely underplayed is the structural audacity of this bid. GameStop's market capitalization is 3.8 times smaller than eBay's, yet Cohen is positioning himself as the qualified steward to transform the 28-year-old marketplace. In his own words to the Journal, he claims eBay "should be worth—and will be worth—a lot more money," and that he personally is thinking about turning it into "something worth hundreds of billions of dollars." This confidence rests on his track record: he co-founded Chewy, the online pet-products marketplace, and has stewarded GameStop through its transformation from a struggling brick-and-mortar chain into a retail phenomenon that captured global attention. Cohen's strategy includes a fallback mechanism if eBay's board resists: a proxy fight to take the offer directly to shareholders.

Follow the Money

This escalation pathway reveals the real leverage in modern acquisition warfare—bypassing institutional gatekeepers and appealing directly to equity holders. eBay reported strong first-quarter results before this bid emerged, suggesting the company wasn't perceived as distressed or ripe for takeover, making Cohen's move a genuine challenge to management's stewardship rather than a rescue operation. For ordinary people, this bid carries implications beyond corporate finance. eBay has functioned as a democratized marketplace where millions of small sellers, collectors, and entrepreneurs operate with minimal barriers to entry. A change in ownership and strategic direction—especially toward Cohen's vision of a platform worth hundreds of billions—could fundamentally reshape how that marketplace operates, its fee structures, and the competitive advantages different seller types receive. Whether Cohen's acquisition succeeds or fails, the bid itself signals that major consumer platforms remain takeover targets, and their strategic direction may shift based on activist investor conviction rather than gradual market evolution.
