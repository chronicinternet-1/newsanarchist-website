US-Iran Deal Near

US and Iran close to arrangement to reopen Strait of Hormuz

Elena Vasquez AI-ASSISTED June 13, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

As a seasoned foreign correspondent, I have been closely following the developments in the US-Iran negotiations, and it appears that a deal is nearing completion. According to reports from Iranian officials, the next phase of talks will focus on the lifting of American sanctions, including those on Iran's oil sales and international banking transactions, in exchange for concessions on the Iranian nuclear program. This is a significant step forward, considering the long-standing tensions between the two nations.

The European Union's plans to launch its 21st sanctions package against Russia, targeting Russian banks and crypto, may seem unrelated to the US-Iran deal, but it highlights the complex geopolitical landscape we are operating in. The EU's move is aimed at delivering a sweeping blow to Moscow's financial infrastructure and wartime supply chains, and it will be interesting to see how this affects the global economy. Meanwhile, the US has recently downed Iranian attack drones, even as deal momentum builds, indicating that there is still much work to be done to establish trust between the two nations.

Pakistani journalist Baqir Sajjad has reported that the draft Iran-US agreement is being considered for approval at the leadership level in both countries. According to Sajjad, the 14-point draft includes immediate de-escalation, an end to hostilities across all fronts, including Lebanon, a US non-interference pledge, phased lifting of the maritime blockade, sanctions relief on oil and petrochem, and gradual restoration of Iran's financial access. This is a significant breakthrough, and if approved, it could have far-reaching implications for the region.

It's worth noting that the US has a history of negotiating with Iran, including the nuclear deal brokered by the Obama Administration in 2016. As part of that deal, the US released or dropped charges against seven Iranians jailed in the United States, and granted Iran access to $50 billion of its own assets, which had been blocked in banks by international sanctions. This deal also led to the release of several Americans, including Washington Post correspondent Jason Rezaian and Matt Trevithick, who was captured while enrolled in an intensive Farsi language program at the University of Tehran.

However, not everyone is convinced that the current deal is a good idea. Former US President Donald Trump has denied Iran's account of the deal terms, decrying a new drone attack and calling the Iranians "dishonorable people." Trump's comments come after Iran's Mehr News Agency reported that a draft deal includes a commitment from the US to lift oil sanctions, and that final negotiations will not begin until half of Iran's frozen funds are released and a US naval blockade ends. As the negotiations continue, it's clear that there are still many obstacles to overcome before a deal can be reached.

As I consider the looming US-Iran deal, I firmly believe that a successful agreement is crucial for global stability. In my opinion, if nothing changes and a deal falls through, hardline Iranian factions will be the ultimate winners. They will use the failure as evidence that the US is an untrustworthy negotiating partner, bolstering their own influence and power. This would be a devastating outcome, allowing Iran's most extreme elements to dictate the country's foreign policy and potentially sparking further conflict in the region. It is imperative that diplomats find a way to salvage the negotiations and reach a mutually beneficial agreement.
