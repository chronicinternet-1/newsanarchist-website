HHS Watchdog: CMS Handed MA Insurers $462M on Fake Stroke Codes

The federal health care watchdog has found that Medicare Advantage insurers collected $462 million from taxpayers in a single year by submitting acute stroke diagnosis codes that had zero supporting hospital records — and CMS won't even say whether it agrees the money should be clawed back.

Jordan Calloway AI-ASSISTED June 3, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The Department of Health and Human Services Office of Inspector General dropped a bombshell audit on June 1, 2026, estimating that the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services made $462 million in net overpayments to Medicare Advantage organizations in 2021 alone — all of it traced to a single, well-documented coding scam: acute stroke diagnoses appearing only on physician billing records, with no corresponding inpatient or outpatient hospital record to prove any stroke ever happened.

The report, numbered A-02-23-01020 and published on the HHS-OIG website, is the latest and most sweeping entry in a years-long pattern of audits that have repeatedly found the privatized Medicare program hemorrhaging taxpayer dollars through diagnosis inflation. But the scale of this finding is qualitatively different from its predecessors. This isn't a single insurer getting caught padding a contract in one state. This is a program-wide estimate: $462 million gone in one year, from one diagnosis category, on one technical coding trick.

Here's how it works. Under the Medicare Advantage program, CMS makes monthly payments to MA organizations — the private insurers running these plans — based on a risk-adjustment system that pays more for sicker enrollees. The theory is that an insurer covering a population with more strokes, cancers, and heart disease deserves higher payments to cover those costs. Insurers are required to submit diagnosis codes from their providers, and CMS relies almost entirely on those self-reported codes to calculate what it owes.

The problem, which the OIG has flagged in report after report, is that some diagnosis codes are structurally easy to abuse. Acute stroke is one of the highest-risk categories. A genuine acute stroke generates a hospital record — an emergency admission, an inpatient stay, imaging studies, clinical notes. But the OIG found that MA organizations submitted acute stroke codes on physician data records for enrollees who had no corresponding hospital claim in the same service year. In plain English: someone billed Medicare as if the patient just had a stroke, with no hospital visit to back it up.

The OIG's methodology is straightforward and devastating. It looked at the 2021 payment year, identified enrollees where MA organizations submitted an acute stroke diagnosis solely through a physician record, checked whether any inpatient or outpatient hospital record confirmed the stroke, and found the codes were unsupported at a scale that extrapolates to $462 million in net overpayments across the entire MA program.

This is not a new problem. Previous OIG audits of specific MA organizations have identified this exact pattern — acute stroke codes on physician records without hospital confirmation — and the watchdog has called it out in audit after audit targeting individual insurers including Humana, CVS's Coventry subsidiary, Gateway Health Plan, Priority Health, and Blue Cross Blue Shield of Alabama. In early 2026, OIG published a series of compliance audits on individual MA contracts; error rates in those samples ran from 81 to 91 percent. The biggest drivers were high-paying conditions including acute strokes and acute heart attacks. The most common failure: history-of conditions coded as active in the audit year, with no chart documentation to justify the higher payment.

CMS itself estimates that 9.5 percent of all payments to MA organizations are improper, mainly due to unsupported diagnoses submitted by MA organizations. With Medicare Advantage spending now topping $500 billion a year, that 9.5 percent figure represents tens of billions of dollars in annual waste. A January 2026 analysis by the Medicare Payment Advisory Commission found that MA payments are currently $76 billion above what spending would have been in traditional Medicare, with a significant portion attributed to coding intensity — the industry's preferred euphemism for what auditors call miscoding and what lawyers call fraud.

The OIG made one recommendation in the June 1 report: that CMS implement a procedure to prevent overpayments when acute stroke codes appear only on a physician data record without a hospital record in the same service year. The fix is technically simple. CMS already has the data. A basic automated cross-check — does this enrollee have a corresponding hospital claim? — could stop $462 million a year from walking out the door.

CMS's response? The agency did not specify concurrence or nonconcurrence with the recommendation.

Read that again. The government's own health insurance regulator, presented with a documented $462 million annual leak from a well-understood coding vulnerability and offered a single, actionable fix, refused to say yes or no. Not a timeline for implementation. Not a technical objection. Not a rebuttal. Silence dressed up in bureaucratic language.

This is not a minor procedural omission. Under the Inspector General Act and OMB Circular A-50, federal agencies are required to respond substantively to OIG audit recommendations. Failing to take a position is itself a form of noncompliance — one that lets the clock run while the money keeps flowing.

The broader context makes CMS's non-answer even harder to stomach. On February 3, 2026, OIG issued the first Medicare Advantage compliance program guidance the agency had published in more than 25 years, flagging chart reviews and in-home health risk assessments used to inflate risk scores as suspect activity. The guidance made clear that plans must certify the accuracy of their risk adjustment data. Submitting or failing to delete inaccurate diagnosis codes is not a paperwork technicality — it is a potential False Claims Act violation.

And yet CMS, under the leadership of Administrator Mehmet Oz, who pledged during his confirmation hearings to heavily scrutinize MA plans, cannot manage to say yes to a recommendation that would prevent nearly half a billion dollars in annual overpayments from a single diagnosis code.

The MA industry spent years and hundreds of millions of dollars in lobbying to protect the program's risk-adjustment architecture. They have litigated OIG audit methodology in federal court, disputed coding determinations at every stage, and fought CMS's attempt to recover past overpayments. Analysts at the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget have estimated that upcoding, projected forward, could lead to Medicare overpaying MA plans by $600 billion over the next decade.

Every dollar an insurer collects for a diagnosis the medical record doesn't support is a dollar moved from public funds to private margins. The OIG has now quantified $462 million of that transfer — from just one code, in just one year — and handed CMS a one-sentence solution. The question now is whether CMS will act, or whether this report joins the stack of ignored recommendations that stretches back years.

I've been covering inspector general reports long enough to know that the most revealing sentence in any federal audit is often the agency's response to the recommendation — and when CMS refuses to specify concurrence or nonconcurrence with a single-sentence fix to a $462 million annual problem, that non-answer tells you everything. CMS is not confused about what the OIG found. The agency has its own data. It could cross-reference physician stroke codes against hospital records tomorrow morning. The technical barrier is zero. The political barrier is the Medicare Advantage industry, which has spent years and enormous resources fighting every audit, every recoupment, and every methodology that might interrupt the revenue stream from diagnosis inflation. If nothing changes, the winners are obvious: the private insurers running MA plans who pocket the spread between what they're paid and what care actually costs. The losers are the Medicare trust fund, which is projected to face insolvency within a decade, and the seniors who are enrolled in plans whose financial model depends not on delivering care efficiently, but on coding aggressively. Every $462 million that flows out on unsupported stroke codes is money that isn't extending the program's solvency. CMS has the fix. It needs to use it.
