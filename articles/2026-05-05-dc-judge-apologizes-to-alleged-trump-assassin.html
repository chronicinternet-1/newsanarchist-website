What they're not telling you: # DC Judge 'Apologizes' To Alleged Trump Assassin A federal magistrate judge in Washington, D.C., apologized in open court to Cole Tomas Allen, the 31-year-old accused of attempting to assassinate President Trump at the White House Correspondents' Association Dinner on April 25, sparking accusations of a two-tier justice system that extends constitutional protections to alleged violent attackers while ordinary Americans face routine rights erosion. Magistrate Judge Zia Faruqui expressed deep concern over Allen's custody conditions during an emergency hearing, with his remarks captured and widely circulated. According to Fox News reporting on the proceeding, the judge voiced serious worries about the defendant's placement in restrictive custody following the shooting incident.

What the Documents Show

Faruqui stated he was "very concerned about his constitutional rights," noting that Allen had requested meetings with his legal team that were not permitted. The defendant had been placed in restrictive 24-hour lockup without windows in a padded room, with no opportunity for recreation. The judge's approach to Allen's treatment becomes more striking considering the allegations. Allen, a California resident with no prior criminal record, faces charges including attempted assassination of the president after authorities say he rushed a security checkpoint at the White House event. The incident allegedly involved gunfire at a location where the sitting president and numerous civilians were present.

Fox News reporting noted that Allen had been placed on suicide watch by the Department of Corrections—a decision the judge specifically questioned. The judge's apologetic stance contradicts the framing typically applied to other defendants facing serious federal charges. Fox News host Larry Kudlow articulated the apparent contradiction live on air: "The judge apologised to this guy, who would've sprayed the whole audience?! And killed God knows how many people? Then would've taken a shot at the president? We're apologizing to this guy?!

Kudlow's exasperation reflected a broader frustration among observers who questioned why apparent standards of judicial demeanor shifted dramatically when the alleged perpetrator faced the most serious charges. The incident illustrates the dynamics operating within the federal justice system when high-profile violent cases reach the courts.
