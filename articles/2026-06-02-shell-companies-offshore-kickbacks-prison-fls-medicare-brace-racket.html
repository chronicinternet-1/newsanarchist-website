Shell Companies, Offshore Kickbacks, Prison: FL's Medicare Brace Racket

Three South Florida men were sentenced June 1 to a combined 17-plus years in federal prison for funneling $6.9 million in fraudulent Medicare orthotic-brace claims through a web of shell companies and an offshore kickback operation — the latest conviction in a nationwide DME fraud epidemic that just claimed a $1 billion scalp.

Jordan Ames AI-ASSISTED June 2, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Marco Scamarone, 34, of Tamarac. Jose Mendez, 34, of Coral Springs. Renee Vazquez, 33, also of Tamarac. Three men in their early thirties, operating out of two durable medical equipment companies they built specifically to steal from Medicare. On June 1, 2026, a federal judge handed them the bill.

Scamarone drew 70 months. Mendez drew 78 months. Vazquez drew 60 months. And that was just the prison time. Scamarone and Mendez were ordered to pay $2,217,840.35 in forfeiture and $3,016,324.20 in restitution. Vazquez was ordered to pay $1,723,773.18 in forfeiture and $2,249,392.09 in restitution. The court didn't leave much on the table.

The scheme ran from January 2022 to February 2023 — just 13 months. In that window, the defendants owned and operated two fraudulent durable medical equipment companies: Braces and Orthotics LLC, registered in the Eastern District of Virginia, and Stone Oak Durable Medical Equipment LLC, operating out of the Southern District of Florida. The geographic split across two federal districts isn't accidental. Experienced fraud operators know to spread their footprint.

The mechanism was textbook DME fraud. The companies submitted approximately $6.9 million in fraudulent claims to Medicare for orthotic braces that were medically unnecessary and, by DOJ's accounting, ineligible for Medicare reimbursement under any standard. To generate the referrals and fraudulent doctors' orders required to make those claims stick, the defendants paid illegal kickbacks and bribes to an offshore marketing company. The offshore component is significant — it adds a layer of jurisdictional complexity and makes evidence harder to secure, a feature, not a bug, for the conspirators.

Once the Medicare payments cleared, the money didn't sit in a bank account. The three men laundered more than $2.2 million in proceeds through a series of shell companies under their control, or controlled by associates. The laundering was the tell. In nine years tracking benefits fraud, I never once saw a simple DME theft operation that didn't have a shell company sitting underneath it. The shell company is the fingerprint of intent — it tells you the operators knew exactly what they were doing and planned for it in advance.

All three pleaded guilty in December 2025 to conspiracy to commit money laundering. The sentencing this week closed the loop on an investigation run by the Department of Health and Human Services Office of Inspector General, the FBI Miami Field Office, and the U.S. Postal Inspection Service.

"Today's sentences send a clear message: if you steal from federal health care programs in South Florida, you will face prison time, forfeiture, restitution, and federal accountability," said U.S. Attorney Jason A. Reding Quiñones for the Southern District of Florida.

This case doesn't exist in a vacuum. It lands three weeks after a federal jury in the same Southern District of Florida convicted Brett Blackman, 42, founder and CEO of healthcare software company HealthSplash, for orchestrating what prosecutors called one of the largest Medicare fraud conspiracies in recent memory — a scheme that billed Medicare and other federal health care programs more than $1 billion. Blackman's sentencing is scheduled for August 26, 2026, and he faces a maximum of 20 years in prison.

The HealthSplash case is where the architecture of this fraud becomes undeniable. Blackman acquired Power Mobility Doctor Rx, LLC — known as DMERx — in September 2017. DMERx was an internet-based platform built to generate doctors' orders for durable medical equipment, including orthotic braces. Under Blackman's control, it became an order-generation machine. Foreign call centers cold-called Medicare beneficiaries and pressured seniors into accepting medically unnecessary braces. Those requests were funneled through the DMERx platform to telemedicine doctors who signed the orders — in some cases without any interaction with the patient at all. Suppliers and pharmacies then billed Medicare for the equipment. The claims totaled over $1 billion. Medicare and other federal programs paid out more than $450 million before investigators caught up.

The fraud was concealed through sham contracts and deliberate manipulation of the doctors' orders — co-conspirators reportedly removed what one insider called "dangerous words" that might trigger Medicare audits. An undercover federal agent who posed as a Medicare beneficiary documented the scheme firsthand: contacted by a foreign call center, pressured to accept multiple braces, and assigned a doctor who signed orders claiming in-person tests had been performed — even though the doctor never spoke with the agent at all.

Blackman's co-defendant Gary Cox, former CEO of DMERx, was convicted in a prior trial and sentenced to 15 years in prison.

The DOJ's Health Care Fraud Strike Force Program now comprises nine strike forces operating across federal districts nationwide. Since 2007, the program has charged more than 6,200 defendants who collectively billed federal health care programs and private insurers more than $45 billion. The Scamarone-Mendez-Vazquez case is one thread in that fabric — but it is a thread that illustrates exactly how the machine runs at the local level: a pair of shell companies, an offshore kickback pipeline, fraudulent doctors' orders, and Medicare claims for braces nobody needed.

The DOJ also recently launched a new West Coast Health Care Fraud Strike Force on April 30, 2026, targeting Arizona, Nevada, and Northern California — a geographic expansion that signals the government is following the fraud wherever it migrates. The new strike force operates under the recently created National Fraud Enforcement Division, led by Assistant Attorney General Colin McDonald.

The pattern is consistent, from South Florida to Southern California: phony DME companies, offshore marketers, rubber-stamp telemedicine orders, Medicare billing in the millions, and shell companies to scrub the money. The names change. The playbook does not.

The Jordan Ames Take
Government Benefits Fraud & Financial Crime

I spent nine years watching this exact fraud operate in cycles. The Scamarone case is not remarkable because of its scale — $6.9 million in fraudulent claims is barely a rounding error in a program that processes hundreds of billions annually. What makes it instructive is the architecture: two shell companies in different districts, an offshore marketing arm that's nearly untouchable, and doctors' orders generated without ever seeing a patient. This is not opportunistic fraud. This is a franchise model. And the HealthSplash conviction, where $1 billion flowed through a software platform designed explicitly to launder the kickback chain, tells you the same operation just scales. The DME brace fraud pipeline has been documented, prosecuted, and re-prosecuted for fifteen years. It persists because Medicare's enrollment controls still allow newly incorporated shell companies to bill within months of registration, and because offshore kickback networks sit largely outside U.S. enforcement reach. Who wins if nothing changes? The next group of operators who've read these indictments and know exactly where the seams are.
