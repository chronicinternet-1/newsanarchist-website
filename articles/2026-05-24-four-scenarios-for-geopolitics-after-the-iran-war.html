Four scenarios for geopolitics after the Iran war

Elena Vasquez
Former foreign correspondent
April 16, 2026
3 min read

What they're not telling you: THE ATLANTIC COUNCIL'. S FOUR FUTURES: WHO BENEFITS. FROM EACH IRAN SCENARIO The Atlantic Council's scenario modeling reveals less about what happens next in Iran than it does about which US alliance structures survive the coming decade intact. The Council—Washington's preferred think tank for NATO coordination and great-power strategy—has mapped four post-conflict futures: a weakened Iran that fragments into regional proxies; a chastened Iran that negotiates within an expanded JCPOA framework; a radicalized Iran that accelerates proxy networks across Syria, Iraq, and the Gulf; and a broader regional conflagration that drags in Israel, Saudi Arabia, and potentially Turkish forces. Each scenario carries a hidden constituency with institutional interests at stake.

What the Documents Show

Start with the first scenario—Iranian fragmentation. This benefits the US military-industrial complex and Gulf Cooperation Council monarchies most directly. A weakened Tehran means sustained American military presence across Qatar, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia. It means continued contracts for Lockheed Martin and Raytheon. It means the petrodollar system remains unchallenged, with Saudi Arabia and the UAE as middlemen between OPEC supply and Western finance.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

The Council's framing here obscures what's actually being defended: not democracy or regional stability, but the post-1971 architecture where US currency dominates energy markets. The second scenario—negotiated restraint—threatens everyone invested in the current containment apparatus. It would require the Biden or successor administration to walk back sanctions architecture that the Treasury Department has spent two decades constructing. It would mean European banks (already chafing under secondary sanctions pressure) could rejoin Iranian trade. Germany's industrial exporters, locked out since 2018, could reenter markets. This threatens the dollar's dominance in non-Western trade.

What Else We Know

Notice how establishment analysis treats this scenario as least probable—not because the evidence suggests it, but because the institutions producing the analysis benefit from ongoing confrontation. The third scenario—radicalized Iran doubling down on Hezbollah, Islamic Jihad, and Houthis—is the Atlantic Council's baseline assumption dressed in reassurance language. It justifies continued Israeli military aid packages, which Congress passes bipartisanly. It justifies the permanent US military footprint across the Levant. Most importantly, it justifies keeping Iran outside the dollar-based financial system. Sanctioned nations cannot challenge dollar hegemony; they must trade in yuan, rubles, or barter.
