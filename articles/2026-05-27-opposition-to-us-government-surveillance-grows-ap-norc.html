    Opposition to U.S. government surveillance grows - AP-NORC

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 26, 2026
    4 min read
    Share

    What they're not telling you: Public Opposition to Mass Surveillance Reaches Highest Levels Since NSA Programs Exposed, But Institutional Architecture Remains Unchanged A majority of Americans now oppose the federal government's collection of telephone and internet data, according to new AP-NORC polling data, yet the surveillance infrastructure that enabled two decades of warrantless monitoring persists without meaningful legal constraint. The polling findings mark a significant shift in public sentiment since the Edward Snowden disclosures revealed the scale of NSA mass surveillance operations in 2013. What the topline numbers obscure, however, is that opposition has grown despite—not because of—any structural dismantling of the collection apparatus itself.

    What the Documents Show

    The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court continues to issue bulk collection authorities. The Defense Intelligence Agency maintains signals intelligence programs. The NSA's metadata collection under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act renewal remains authorized through 2026. The AP-NORC data quantifies erosion of institutional trust, but documents show the actual collection mechanisms have only expanded in sophistication. The NSA's bulk telephone records program, nominally ended in 2015, was replaced by the Call Detail Records program, which maintains access to the same data through different contractual arrangements with telecommunications carriers.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    AT&T, Verizon, and CenturyLink continue providing call detail records to federal agencies under standing orders. The corporate angle here matters: these companies face no statutory penalty for compliance, only potential litigation from privacy advocates—a cost structure that guarantees continued cooperation. What distinguishes this polling moment from previous sentiment shifts is the bipartisan nature of the opposition. Majorities across demographic and political groups now express skepticism of government surveillance authorities. Yet the legislative response has been marginal. The USA Freedom Act of 2015, heralded as surveillance reform, merely transferred metadata collection from direct NSA possession to telecommunications company servers—a procedural relocation that changed access patterns but not scope.

    What Else We Know

    The Five Eyes alliance—the signals intelligence partnership between the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand—continues operating under bilateral agreements that circumvent domestic legal restrictions. Documents from prior disclosures show that NSA shares raw intelligence feeds with GCHQ (UK), ASD (Australia), and CSEC (Canada) without the minimization procedures required for domestic collection. This means foreign intelligence agencies access American communications data under less restrictive legal frameworks than the NSA officially claims to follow domestically. The infrastructure persists because the institutional incentives protecting it remain intact. The Defense Department's budget for signals intelligence collection and processing exceeds $20 billion annually across classified accounts. Personnel invested in these programs—contractors, military intelligence analysts, NSA divisions—constitute a constituency with direct financial interest in program continuation.

    The Marcus Webb Take
    Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

    The gap between public opposition and institutional continuity reveals something fundamental about how power operates in classified governance: polling doesn't constrain bureaucracy. What constrains bureaucracy is statutory language, budget authority, and court orders—none of which have meaningfully changed despite a decade of public skepticism.

    I find striking that the corporate infrastructure partners—AT&T, Verizon, the contractors managing NSA backend systems—face zero electoral pressure. They have no political constituency demanding accountability. Citizens can withdraw support from elected officials; they cannot withdraw support from telecommunications companies providing the physical backbone of surveillance infrastructure.

    The pattern here is straightforward: when institutions operate partially outside democratic oversight, public opinion becomes a metric for internal communications strategy rather than a constraint on operations. The NSA monitors its own popularity in polls. This monitoring serves feedback purposes—adjust messaging, manage congressional relations—but carries no operational consequence.

    What readers should understand: surveillance infrastructure built during emergency operates indefinitely after emergency ends. Watch whether the next FISA reauthorization fight in 2026 produces statutory change or merely reauthorizes existing authorities under updated language. That outcome will tell you whether opposition has translated into actual institutional constraint, or merely shifted the narrative burden.
