What they're not telling you: # The $900 Billion Question: Why Anthropic's Valuation Skyrocketed While OpenAI's CFO Insists Demand Remains Unsatisfied Anthropic is now entertaining investor offers at a $900 billion valuation—more than double its $350 billion price tag just weeks earlier—even as OpenAI's chief financial officer claims there exists a "vertical wall of demand" the market cannot yet fulfill. The timing is worth scrutinizing. Just two months ago, the Pentagon had blacklisted Anthropic as a "supply chain risk," severing government ties with the AI company.

What the Documents Show

Yet the National Security Agency, operating under the Department of Defense, soon reversed course and demanded access to Anthropic's most powerful model, codenamed "Mythos." According to internal warnings cited in available reports, this model could allegedly "hack every major system." The Treasury Department similarly sought access. The reversal occurred with little public explanation—a dramatic about-face that preceded the valuation surge. The comparison to OpenAI deepens the puzzle. Google committed $10 billion to Anthropic at the $350 billion valuation, while Amazon pledged $5 billion at the same price. Within roughly one week, Anthropic began entertaining offers at $900 billion—a 157 percent increase.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Anthropic had previously rejected investor proposals at $800 billion or higher, according to Bloomberg reporting, suggesting the company suddenly became willing to accept dramatically higher valuations. The shift coincides with Anthropic's stated need to "ramp up fundraising amid the breakout success of its AI software" and infrastructure demands. What the mainstream narrative downplays: the distinction between Anthropic's public-facing Claude model and its government-accessible Mythos variant. The former reportedly outperforms OpenAI's GPT for workflow tasks and analytical work. The latter carries security implications serious enough to trigger Pentagon interest despite official blacklisting. Yet mainstream coverage treats these as secondary details rather than exploring why government agencies would scramble to access a tool they'd just restricted—or what capabilities justified the reversal.

What Else We Know

OpenAI's CFO assertion about unfulfilled demand adds another layer. If demand truly faces a "vertical wall," why would Anthropic suddenly find investors willing to pay triple the previous valuation? Either demand has genuinely exploded in days—suggesting a genuine market inflection point—or the valuation reflects something other than demand metrics. The Pentagon's policy reversal suggests government interest may be driving valuations independent of commercial demand. For ordinary people, the implications are substantial. If Anthropic's valuation reflects genuine AI capability advantages—particularly ones government agencies deem strategically critical—then the competitive landscape between AI providers is shifting rapidly.
