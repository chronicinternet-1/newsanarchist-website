The Saturday observances in Moscow's Red Square marked a stark departure from pre-2022 pageantry, with scaled-down military displays and reduced attendee numbers. Speaking before thousands of military personnel and a handful of foreign leaders, Putin doubled down on framing the ongoing conflict as a "just war" against what he characterized as an "aggressive force armed and supported by the entire NATO bloc." His rhetoric deliberately inverted conventional Western narratives about the conflict's origins, casting Russia as the party responding to external aggression rather than initiating it.

What the Documents Show

"They are confronting an aggressive force armed and supported by the entire NATO bloc. And despite this, our heroes move forward," Putin stated, explicitly linking current military operations to the patriotic legacy celebrated by Victory Day commemorations. The ceasefire arrangement reveals diplomatic maneuvering largely absent from mainstream coverage. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky issued a military decree prohibiting attacks on the Moscow parade—a striking constraint on Ukrainian military operations during an active conflict. More significantly, Zelensky framed the restraint in humanitarian terms, stating that "Red Square is less important to us than the lives of Ukrainian prisoners who can be returned home," signaling his willingness to prioritize a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange.

What Else We Know

Moscow warned foreign diplomats to evacuate Kyiv, explicitly threatening massive bombing campaigns if the parade faced disruption. Rather than dismiss these as propaganda, the apparent Ukrainian compliance and ceasefire adherence suggest the threats carried sufficient credibility to influence decision-making in Kyiv.
