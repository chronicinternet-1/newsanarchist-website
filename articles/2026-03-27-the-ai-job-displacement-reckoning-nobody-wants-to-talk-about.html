The AI Job Displacement Reckoning Nobody Wants to Talk About

As AI reshapes white-collar work at unprecedented speed, the uncomfortable question is no longer whether jobs are disappearing — it's what comes next.

· 6 min read

There's a conversation happening in boardrooms, economics departments, and policy circles that hasn't fully spilled into public view. It goes something like this: artificial intelligence isn't just augmenting human work — it's replacing it. And unlike previous waves of automation that gutted manufacturing, this one is aiming squarely at the professional class.

The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

The "reckoning

The data is sobering. Since early 2025, white-collar job postings in sectors like legal research, financial analysis, software quality assurance, and customer success have dropped between 15 and 40 percent, depending on the segment. Layoffs at major corporations have clustered disproportionately in departments where AI tools have matured fastest. Meanwhile, productivity at those same companies has hit record highs.

That last part is the tell. When productivity rises as headcount falls, it's not a cyclical downturn — it's structural change. The work is still getting done. Fewer humans are doing it.

The Comfortable Lie

The dominant narrative from tech executives and optimistic economists is familiar: every technological revolution destroys some jobs and creates others. The spinning jenny put hand weavers out of work, but the textile mills hired thousands. The automobile killed the carriage trade but spawned an entire industrial ecosystem. AI will be no different, the argument goes — new categories of work will emerge that we can't yet imagine.

Maybe. But there are reasons to question whether this time is actually different, even if that's the most clichéd phrase in economic commentary. Previous automation waves targeted physical, repetitive tasks. This one targets cognitive, analytical, and even creative work. The skills that were supposed to be automation-proof — complex reasoning, writing, coding, research synthesis — are precisely the skills that large language models are absorbing fastest.

What the Numbers Actually Show

Goldman Sachs estimated in 2023 that AI could automate tasks accounting for roughly 300 million full-time jobs globally. The IMF has warned that nearly 40 percent of jobs worldwide are exposed to AI disruption, with the figure rising to 60 percent in advanced economies. These aren't fringe forecasts. They're mainstream institutional projections that have been largely buried beneath cheerful narratives about "augmentation" and "productivity partnerships."

What's emerging on the ground tells a more complicated story. Entry-level positions — the traditional pipeline for building skills and career capital — are evaporating first. Junior analysts, associate lawyers, entry-level coders, and research assistants are finding fewer openings as firms discover that senior employees augmented by AI can handle what used to require a full team of juniors.

This creates a peculiar paradox: the workers who most need experience to advance their careers are finding fewer opportunities to gain it. The ladder is being pulled up, not because the people at the top are malicious, but because the economic incentives have shifted dramatically.

The Policy Vacuum

What makes this moment genuinely alarming isn't the technology itself — it's the absence of any coherent policy response. In Washington, the AI debate has centered almost entirely on safety concerns, national security, and competition with China. The labor dimension is treated as a future problem, something to worry about later.

But "later" is arriving faster than anyone planned. Retraining programs — the perennial political answer to automation anxiety — face a fundamental challenge: what do you retrain people for when the target keeps moving? The skills that were valuable two years ago are being commoditized faster than curricula can be updated.

Universal Basic Income remains politically toxic in the United States, dismissed as either socialism or surrender. Profit-sharing from AI gains, which would at least distribute some of the productivity windfall to displaced workers, has no serious legislative vehicle. The policy apparatus is essentially frozen while the economy restructures around it.

The Anarchist's Lens

There's a bleaker read on all this that doesn't get sufficient airtime: what if the jobs don't come back, not because the technology is uniquely powerful, but because the economic system has no incentive to create them? Capital captures the productivity gains from AI. Labor absorbs the disruption. This isn't a bug — it's the predictable output of a system optimized for shareholder returns over social stability.

The question isn't whether AI creates new industries. It will. The question is whether those industries generate the volume and variety of employment that the displaced workforce needs. History suggests that new industries tend to create fewer jobs than the ones they displace, at least initially, and often require entirely different skill sets concentrated in different geographies.

Silicon Valley gets richer. Former knowledge workers in mid-sized American cities retrain for jobs that don't yet exist, or settle for service sector work at lower wages. The income distribution widens. Social cohesion frays. Political instability follows.

This is the scenario that serious economists are quietly modeling while public discourse focuses on AI-generated art and chatbot hallucinations. It deserves a more honest conversation than it's getting — one that doesn't assume the market will sort it out, because the market is precisely what's creating the problem.
