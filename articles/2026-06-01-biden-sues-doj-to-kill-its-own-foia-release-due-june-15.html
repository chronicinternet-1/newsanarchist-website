Biden Sues DOJ to Kill Its Own FOIA Release — Due June 15

A former president is in federal court right now trying to stop his own Justice Department from releasing classified-documents interview audio obtained through a FOIA lawsuit — and the clock runs out in 14 days.

Jordan Calloway AI-ASSISTED June 1, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Joe Biden filed suit against the United States Department of Justice last week in U.S. District Court in Washington, D.C., in a last-ditch attempt to permanently block the release of audio recordings and transcripts from his 2016 and 2017 conversations with ghostwriter Mark Zwonitzer — recordings that form the evidentiary core of Special Counsel Robert Hur's classified documents investigation. The release deadline is June 15, 2026. Unless a federal judge intervenes, the tapes go public.

This is not a hypothetical. This is a countdown.

The recordings at the center of this legal firestorm stem from interviews Biden conducted with Zwonitzer for his 2017 memoir, "Promise Me, Dad." The materials were swept up by Hur's investigators and became central to his February 2024 report, which concluded that Biden had "willfully retained and disclosed" classified materials to his ghostwriter — but recommended no criminal charges, citing, among other factors, the challenges of prosecuting an elderly former president.

The Heritage Foundation, the conservative think tank, had filed a FOIA lawsuit against the DOJ seeking those exact records. That lawsuit has been grinding through the courts for two years. Now it is on the verge of producing documents. The DOJ, under the Trump administration, reversed the position held by Biden's own Justice Department — which had resisted releasing the recordings — and informed Biden's legal team in February 2026, without any formal written explanation, that it intended to release the materials to the FOIA plaintiffs. Then, on May 5, the Office of the Deputy Attorney General told Biden's counsel that the department had made a final decision: the recordings would go out, with limited redactions, to both the Heritage Foundation and House Judiciary Committee Chairman Jim Jordan on June 15.

Biden's attorney, Amy Jeffress, argues in the lawsuit that the Trump DOJ "reversed" its predecessor's position "without any formal explanation," a phrase that appears almost verbatim in multiple court filings. That reversal, she argues, is legally impermissible. Biden's team maintains the recordings are exempt from disclosure under FOIA's privacy protections, and that the material was provided to Hur's investigators under an expectation that it would never reach the public. Biden's spokesperson TJ Ducklo was blunt in a statement to press: the DOJ itself previously said the tapes serve no public interest.

But here is what Biden's lawyers don't want you to think about too hard: the DOJ isn't releasing these records out of the goodness of its institutional heart. It is releasing them because a FOIA lawsuit forced the question, because a court-sanctioned process determined the public has a right to see what the government collected about one of its own sitting presidents. That is the system working, even if the politics around it are messy.

The procedural history is complex. Biden first moved to intervene in the Heritage Foundation's existing FOIA case. On May 21, 2026, a federal judge allowed him to join that litigation but barred him from pursuing claims directly tied to the House Judiciary Committee's separate records request. Blocked on that front, Biden's legal team filed an entirely new, standalone lawsuit — asking the court to permanently enjoin the DOJ from releasing the recordings to anyone.

The DOJ's response landed like a hammer. A department spokesperson told CBS News: "Joe Biden's Justice Department tried to hide audio recordings that clearly demonstrate a significant decline in his cognitive abilities as far back as 2016." The department said it would fight to ensure the American people can hear the recordings and draw their own conclusions about the former president's mental acuity during the period before he sought re-election to the presidency.

The political dimensions here are impossible to ignore, but they should not obscure the legal stakes. At the center of this case is a foundational FOIA question: does a former president retain the power to suppress records that his own government collected through a criminal investigation, simply because those records are personally embarrassing? Biden's team says yes — the recordings are private and protected. The DOJ and the Heritage Foundation say no — FOIA's public interest overrides the former president's comfort.

There is also a profound double standard simmering just below the surface. Biden's camp has pointedly noted that if the Trump administration were genuinely committed to transparency, it would simultaneously release Volume 2 of Special Counsel Jack Smith's report — the section addressing Trump's own alleged mishandling of sensitive government documents. That portion of the Smith report was blocked from release in February 2026 by a federal judge. The same administration now racing to release Biden's tapes has been aggressively suppressing the parallel record on Trump.

What happens June 15 depends on whether a federal judge issues a temporary restraining order before that date. The court battle is in its most acute phase now. Biden's lawsuit seeks a permanent bar on the recordings ever being released. The DOJ wants them out the door in two weeks. The Heritage Foundation, which spent two years in litigation to get here, is watching its FOIA win potentially get yanked away by a last-minute injunction brought by the very subject of the investigation.

Also worth tracking on the FOIA docket: the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2026, signed into law in December 2025 by President Trump, contains a separate and significant provision requiring the Director of National Intelligence to conduct a formal declassification review of COVID-19 origins intelligence and release those products publicly. That mandate — ordering the intelligence community to go back to the raw record rather than produce another summary — is working its way through agencies. No release has been made yet. The clock is ticking on that one too.

For now, all eyes are on June 15.

The Jordan Calloway Take
Government Secrets & FOIA

Let me be clear about what is actually happening here: a former president of the United States is suing his own country's Justice Department to prevent the release of records that a court-supervised FOIA process determined the public has a right to see. That is extraordinary — and the fact that it is wrapped in political warfare between Biden and Trump does not make it less corrosive to the transparency framework. FOIA is not a partisan instrument. It does not care whether the subject of the documents is a Democrat or a Republican. The Heritage Foundation sued. The DOJ agreed — reluctantly, under political pressure, but agreed nonetheless — to release. Biden's response is to run to court and ask a judge to make those records permanently disappear. If he wins, it sets a precedent that former presidents can veto FOIA productions simply by asserting personal privacy over records their own government collected. If nothing changes, the winners are the permanent secrecy establishment, the lawyers who profit from endless privilege litigation, and every future official who will cite Biden v. DOJ to suppress inconvenient records. The losers are the public and the statute itself.
