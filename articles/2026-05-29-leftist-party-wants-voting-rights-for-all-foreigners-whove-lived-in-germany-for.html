    Leftist Party Wants Voting Rights For All Foreigners Who've Lived In Germany For 5 Years

    Diana Reeves
    Former SEC examiner
    May 28, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: The German Left's Electoral Gambit: 14 Million Non-Citizens as Untapped Voting Base Germany's Left Party faction in the Bundestag has submitted a formal application to grant voting rights to all foreign residents who have lived legally in the country for at least five years, irrespective of nationality. This is a straightforward power play disguised as democratic expansion—and the numbers reveal exactly who stands to gain. The pool of potential new voters is staggering.

    What the Documents Show

    According to Federal Statistical Office data cited in the Left Party's motion, over 14 million people living in Germany currently lack German citizenship. That figure includes roughly 5 million EU citizens, but the remaining 9 million represent a largely untapped electoral resource. These foreigners have resided in Germany for an average of 15 years, meaning the vast majority already meet the proposed five-year threshold. The Left Party's framing of this as a remedy for "democratic deficit" obscures the central fact: the party is pursuing a structural change that would dramatically expand its electoral base at the precise moment when traditional German voting patterns are fracturing. The Left Party argues that the current system violates democratic principles outlined in Germany's Basic Law and contradicts the nation's self-identification as an "immigration society." The motion urges the federal government to collaborate with individual states to implement identical changes for state and municipal elections, which would have cascading effects on local power distribution.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Legal scholars cited in the party's motion point to a 1990 Federal Constitutional Court decision that blocked voting rights for foreigners, but argue this ruling deserves reconsideration given "shifting global dynamics" and the fact that EU citizens have since gained local voting rights. What the mainstream coverage of this proposal consistently misses: the strategic timing and the structural asymmetry. Germany's foreign-born population skews younger and more urban than the native population. When given voting access in other contexts, foreign residents have overwhelmingly voted for left-wing parties—a pattern documented across European democracies. The Left Party isn't framing this as an electoral advantage; it's framing it as principle. But principles don't drive political strategy.

    What Else We Know

    Electoral mathematics do. A 9 million-person expansion of the potential left-wing electorate isn't a minor constitutional adjustment. It's a fundamental remapping of German political power. The Federal Constitutional Court's 1990 decision remains binding law. The Left Party's motion cannot unilaterally override it. But the motion's submission signals intent to push this issue toward constitutional amendment or judicial reconsideration—a long game that acknowledges the proposal's radical departure from existing electoral law.
