Florida Man Sues After AI Facial Recognition Wrongly Sent Him to Jail

An ACLU lawsuit filed June 10 exposes how police deployed facial recognition with a 93 percent confidence score against a man 300 miles away, allowing the algorithm to stand in for investigation.

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 12, 2026 6 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Robert Dillon should never have been arrested. On August 2024, deputies from the Lee County Sheriff's Office arrived at his Fort Myers home to arrest him for attempted child luring at a Jacksonville Beach McDonald's—over 300 miles away, a five-hour drive through Florida. Dillon told them immediately: he had not left Fort Myers in two years. His wife confirmed it. The deputies already knew it, because the arrest warrant affidavit contained exculpatory evidence they would later conceal from the court. But the algorithm had spoken, and that was enough.

On June 10, 2026, the ACLU and ACLU of Florida, working with the law firm Hoguet Newman Regal & Kenney, filed a federal wrongful arrest suit in the U.S. District Court for the Middle District of Florida. The case—Dillon v. City of Jacksonville Beach—names four defendants: the city of Jacksonville Beach, the Jacksonville Beach Police Department, the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office, and the Pinellas County Sheriff's Office. Dillon seeks monetary damages and mandatory policy changes to restrict how police deploy facial recognition technology.

The technical crime itself was mundane. Around 11 p.m. on November 2, 2023, a girl under age 12 was approached by a man inside a McDonald's in Jacksonville Beach who repeatedly asked if she wanted to leave with him. She declined. He asked again: "Are you sure?" She called her mother, and he left. The victim's parents were in a hotel next door. Police arrived and collected video surveillance of the suspect.

What followed is a clinical demonstration of how institutional deference to algorithmic output can obliterate due process. Officer David Cohill photographed the suspect from the computer screen showing the surveillance footage—meaning the images Dillon alleges were "partially shadowed and off-axis," exactly the kind of poor-quality input that a 2015 FACESNXT training presentation warned could produce unreliable results. The Jacksonville Sheriff's Office ran those grainy cellphone photos through its system, called either FACES or FACESNXT in various documents. It returned a match: 93 percent confidence that the suspect was Robert Dillon.

Here is where the record diverges from what courts would later see. The investigating officer, Scott O'Connell, conducted a photo lineup. He showed the victim a set of photographs, one of which was Dillon's. The victim picked it out. What the arrest warrant affidavit failed to disclose—what constitutes Brady material, information favorable to the defendant—is that this lineup was tainted by the prior facial recognition result. The officer had already told the victim, implicitly or explicitly, who the algorithm suggested it was. The identification was not independent. It was confirmation bias engineered into law enforcement procedure.

On that basis—the algorithmic match plus the contaminated photo identification—Jacksonville Beach Police obtained an arrest warrant from a magistrate. The warrant application omitted entirely the fact that Dillon was documented as being 300 miles away at the time of the crime. The ACLU complaint alleges that Officer O'Connell possessed exculpatory evidence and did not disclose it.

When deputies arrested Dillon on August 2024, the body camera recorded his immediate and consistent protest: he had not been to Jacksonville Beach. He had been home. The deputies appear to have acknowledged the absurdity themselves. According to Dillon, one officer told him: "If what you're telling me is true, you got one hell of a lawsuit."

The state attorney's office charged Dillon with attempted child abduction. He was detained. The prosecution continued for over two months before the state attorney finally dropped all charges. No conviction. No settlement. Simply: we were wrong, go home.

For Dillon, the impact was permanent. In a statement released with the lawsuit, he wrote: "The night I spent in jail after they arrested me for a crime I did not commit still haunts me to this day. I will never get over how terrified and worried I was, wondering if I'd ever go home to my wife and daughter again. Over a year later, I'm still picking up the pieces of my life, all because the police relied on this dangerous technology instead of doing their jobs and actually investigating."

The Pinellas County Sheriff's Office, which oversees FACES across multiple Florida jurisdictions, quickly issued a defensive statement. "The assertion in the lawsuit that PCSO failed to train [officers in the facial recognition technology] is patently false," the statement read. The training, it claimed, makes "unequivocally clear" that the technology is "an investigative tool only." The sheriff's office added: "Facial recognition results are never 'matches.' They are leads."

That parsing is important and, under closer examination, damning. If facial recognition is a lead, not a match, then its confidence score should mean nothing without independent corroboration. In Dillon's case, the only corroboration was a photo lineup already poisoned by knowledge of the algorithm's output. If the technology produces mere leads, then why was it treated as primary evidence in the warrant affidavit? Why was the exculpatory information—his documented location 300 miles away—omitted? The answer is institutional momentum: the algorithm suggested it, the system was designed to trust the algorithm, and the human decision-makers moved in lockstep with the machine.

Dillon's case is not isolated. The ACLU notes in its complaint that facial recognition wrongful arrests have occurred in multiple Florida jurisdictions. In 2025, Beau Burgess of New Smyrna Beach was wrongfully arrested after a facial recognition match from the same systems. Earlier this year, Jalil Richardson of Jacksonville sat in jail for over 50 days after being misidentified as a vehicle thief through facial recognition. These are not anomalies. They are predictable failures of a system deployed without adequate safeguards, training oversight, or accountability mechanisms.

The federal government, meanwhile, continues expanding facial recognition at precisely the moment when judicial and legislative scrutiny is mounting. In May 2026, the Congressional Research Service released a report documenting how federal agencies—CBP, ICE, and others—are deploying the technology faster than any regulatory framework can accommodate. The Trump administration is simultaneously pulling back internal oversight mechanisms.

The lawsuit seeks both monetary damages and injunctive relief. More significantly, it demands structural changes to how police can use facial recognition: restrictions on deploying matches as sole basis for probable cause, mandatory independent corroboration, transparent training standards, and regular accuracy audits disaggregated by race and ethnicity—data that might reveal racial bias in the algorithms and the officers using them.

Jacksonville Beach Police, Jacksonville Sheriff's Office, and Pinellas County Sheriff's Office have all declined comment, citing pending litigation. The federal court system will now determine what happens when an algorithm is wrong and the criminal legal system simply accepts the error without sufficient investigation. It will determine whether Robert Dillon's trauma becomes a precedent that constrains police use of the technology, or merely another cautionary tale lost in the institutional machinery.

This case is the watershed moment we've been waiting for. For years, civil liberties advocates have warned that facial recognition would become a tool for investigative shortcuts rather than genuine inquiry. The Dillon suit proves it empirically: a 93 percent confidence match, derived from poor-quality images, was deemed sufficient to arrest a man documented as being 300 miles away. The exculpatory evidence was simply omitted. If the lawsuit succeeds, we get guardrails. If it fails—if courts defer to law enforcement's characterization of facial recognition as a "lead" rather than primary evidence—then we have formally ceded the power of arrest to machines that err systematically against people of color. The Pinellas County Sheriff's Office wins if nothing changes. The system wins. The algorithm wins. Dillon loses. We all do.
