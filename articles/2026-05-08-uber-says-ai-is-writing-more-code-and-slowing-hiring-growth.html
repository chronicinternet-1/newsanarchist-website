Uber Says AI Is Writing More Code and Slowing Hiring Growth

Marcus Webb
Former intelligence contractor
May 7, 2026 · 4 min read

What they're not telling you: # Uber Admits AI Is Replacing Engineering Growth—And It's Happening Faster Than Anyone Expected Uber's leadership has quietly revealed that artificial intelligence is now writing roughly 10% of the company's code, and the company is explicitly slowing hiring to capitalize on this productivity surge—a development that exposes how rapidly AI-driven workforce displacement is accelerating across tech's largest employers. During Uber's first quarter earnings call, CEO Dara Khosrowshahi disclosed that autonomous coding agents now account for approximately one in ten code updates at the ride-sharing giant. While human engineers still review these AI-generated outputs before deployment, the admission marks a significant escalation in how major tech firms are integrating AI into core operations.

The Take
Jordan Calloway · Government Secrets & FOIA

# THE TAKE: Uber's AI Smokescreen Uber's "efficiency gains" claim is corporate theater masking labor arbitrage. The company isn't slowing hiring *because* AI writes better code—it's slowing hiring *to justify* already-planned headcount cuts without admitting demand is softening. Their own filing admits what they won't say publicly: productivity gains mean fewer engineers per dollar of output. That's not innovation; that's substitution. What Uber calls "competitive advantage" is really "we need fewer people." The receipts matter here. Uber laid off 6,700 workers in 2023 after explosive spending. Now they're rehiring selectively while pivoting the narrative to AI-as-destiny instead of "we over-hired." This is deliberate framing. They're not saying "we miscalculated payroll." They're saying "technology made your roles obsolete." Different message, same outcome: engineering jobs disappearing while executives preserve their own ranks.

What the Documents Show

What makes this notable is the candor: Khosrowshahi framed the shift positively, describing it as giving "employees with superpowers," yet the underlying business logic is unmistakable—fewer humans are needed to accomplish the same work. The hiring slowdown stems directly from this capability expansion. CFO Balaji Krishnamurthy stated on the earnings call that Uber's executive team fundamentally miscalculated AI's trajectory when budgeting for 2026. In November 2024, leadership drafted hiring plans based on their assumptions about AI productivity gains. But when a fresh wave of advanced models arrived in December—a reference to tools like OpenAI's o1 and competing systems—those assumptions became obsolete almost immediately.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Uber responded by maintaining aggressive AI investment spending while explicitly reducing "incremental headcount growth." The company has already exhausted its entire 2026 budget allocation for Anthropic's Claude Code, underscoring velocity of internal AI tool adoption. Khosrowshahi's public framing deserves scrutiny. He suggested the math works if "every person at this company can increase their throughput by 20%, 30%, 50%, 100%." The mainstream tech press typically reports such statements as innovation wins. What gets underplayed is the implicit premise: if individual worker output doubles while headcount stays flat, the company gains the equivalent of doubling its workforce without doubling its payroll. This is not neutral progress—it represents a structural shift in how labor value flows through the organization. Uber isn't hiring fewer people because market demand declined; it's hiring fewer people because AI tools have made additional human workers economically redundant.

What Else We Know

The broader pattern matters more than Uber's specific choices. This company is a leading indicator. When a $70+ billion multinational with deep AI resources states it underestimated AI's impact on productivity—and then immediately rebalances capital away from human hiring toward AI systems—it signals the beginning of a sustained structural shift in tech employment. The fact that this admission came through a financial earnings call, not a press release or public statement, suggests management views this as operational optimization rather than newsworthy disruption. For workers outside the technology sector, this matters because Uber's experience will likely replicate across industries. If leading companies are already discovering that AI productivity gains exceed their projections by wide margins, labor demand in white-collar fields will contract faster than either official forecasts or public company statements suggest.
