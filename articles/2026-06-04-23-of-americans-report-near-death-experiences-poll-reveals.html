23% of Americans Report Near-Death Experiences, Poll Reveals

A landmark poll conducted by the International Association for Near-Death Studies suggests NDEs are vastly more prevalent than previously thought, with nearly one in four American adults claiming they've experienced a brush with the afterlife.

Vera Solano AI-ASSISTED June 4, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The numbers landed like a quiet bombshell. When the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS) and research firm Centiment released their new national poll last week, the headline finding stopped researchers and consciousness scholars in their tracks: 23 percent of American adults say they've had a near-death experience. That's roughly 60 million people.

For context, a 1982 Gallup study—the gold standard in NDE polling for decades—found only 15 percent of Americans reported ever coming close to death with unusual experiences. The IANDS/Centiment survey, conducted in March 2026 with 2,100 respondents, suggests the phenomenon is not the anomaly it was once treated as. It's approaching the prevalence of major mental health conditions.

"This remarkable data tells us three important things: NDEs may be more common than we realized; people who have NDEs or hear about them are positively impacted, and the majority of Americans believe evidence exists to support the phenomena," the researchers stated.

But the headline number only tells part of the story. The secondary findings paint an even more intriguing picture. Thirty-five percent of Americans say they know someone who's had an NDE. Among those who report experiencing one themselves, 51 percent said it gave them a deeper appreciation for life, while 36 percent reported feeling more connected to the spiritual realm, and 31 percent indicated the experience fundamentally shifted their worldview.

The timing of this data release matters. We're in the middle of Near-Death Experience Week—June 1st through June 7th—a new initiative IANDS launched this year to drive cultural conversation around death, dying, and what may lie beyond. And in August, the organization will host its largest conference ever in Bellevue, Washington, drawing researchers, experiencers, and curious observers from around the world.

What makes the IANDS poll particularly significant is not just the headline percentage, but what it reveals about American consciousness at this moment. We are, by the numbers, a nation genuinely open to—and increasingly experiencing—phenomena that mainstream medicine spent decades dismissing as hallucinations, oxygen deprivation, or psychological coping mechanisms.

The scientific community has been slowly pivoting. Earlier this year, University of Virginia researchers Bruce Greyson and Marieta Pehlivanova challenged a prominent neurophysiological model of NDEs, arguing it failed to account for the robust, multi-sensory, long-lasting nature of the experiences people report. Meanwhile, Belgian neuroscientist Charlotte Martial presented research at the Bial Foundation symposium suggesting NDEs might function as adaptive mental states—not hallucinations, but sophisticated survival mechanisms.

Yet the IANDS/Centiment poll reveals a crucial asymmetry: the public has moved further than institutions have. If 23 percent of Americans have genuinely experienced something they describe as a near-death experience, and 35 percent know someone who has, then the medical and psychiatric establishments are studying a phenomenon that is far more woven into the fabric of American experience than their cautious language typically acknowledges.

The poll methodology is worth examining. Unlike the Gallup study, which asked respondents if they'd been "on the verge of death or had a close call with unusual experiences," the IANDS/Centiment survey specifically framed NDEs as experiences where the subject "transitioned to another realm and then returned to human existence." This framing almost certainly influences response rates—people are more likely to say yes when you're being explicit about what you mean by "near-death experience."

Still, even accounting for methodological differences, the jump from 15 percent to 23 percent suggests something genuine is shifting. Either more people are having these experiences, or more people feel comfortable reporting them in an anonymous survey. Both interpretations carry weight.

What the poll doesn't fully capture is the qualitative dimension that makes NDEs so compelling: the consistency of the reported content across cultures and backgrounds. Experiencers describe tunnels, light, deceased relatives, overwhelming peace, and often, profound reluctance to return. These aren't random hallucinations—they have structure, narrative coherence, and lasting psychological impact.

The IANDS poll attempts to capture some of this. Beyond the headline prevalence figure, the survey reveals that among NDErs, the experiences tend toward the positive and transformative. Whether that reflects the nature of the experiences themselves or a reporting bias among survivors isn't yet clear—terrifying NDEs exist but may be less commonly discussed.

As we move into the latter half of 2026, with Near-Death Experience Week now an annual fixture and the IANDS Conference drawing unprecedented attendance, the cultural conversation is shifting. We're no longer asking whether NDEs are "real" in some binary sense. We're asking what they are, what they mean, and why so many Americans have had them. That shift in the question itself represents profound evolution in how we approach consciousness and mortality.

The Vera Solano Take
Metaphysical & Consciousness

The IANDS poll cracked something open. If one in four Americans has experienced what they call a near-death experience, then the scientific community's cautious neutrality isn't just understated—it's fundamentally out of step with lived experience. The institutions that study consciousness are chasing a phenomenon that has already infiltrated the mainstream. What wins if nothing changes? The same thing that always wins when institutions lag behind reality: confusion, self-medication, and spiritual seeking outside legitimate channels. The medical establishment had a chance to lean into this data and lead the conversation. Instead, they're still debating whether NDEs are hallucinations or not. Meanwhile, 60 million Americans know what they experienced. The poll just gave them permission to name it.
