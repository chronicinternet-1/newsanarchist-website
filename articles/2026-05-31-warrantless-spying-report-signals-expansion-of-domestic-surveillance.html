    Warrantless Spying Report Signals Expansion of Domestic Surveillance

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 30, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: WARRANTLESS SPYING REPORT SIGNALS EXPANSION OF DOMESTIC SURVEILLANCE The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court has authorized surveillance activities under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act that NSA officials acknowledge routinely capture Americans' communications without individualized warrants, according to newly examined court filings and Inspector General assessments. The American Prospect's reporting draws from FISA Court documents and government accountability materials indicating that the National Security Agency's upstream collection programs—which intercept internet traffic at telecommunications chokepoints operated by carriers including AT&T and Verizon—captured an estimated 200,000 to 300,000 emails belonging to U.S. persons annually between 2017 and 2020.

    What the Documents Show

    These figures emerged from classified NSA compliance reviews and were documented in FISC filings that remained largely inaccessible to public scrutiny until recent years. What distinguishes this expansion from prior surveillance disclosures is the explicit bureaucratic acknowledgment of the capture mechanism itself. NSA internal audits, disclosed through Freedom of Information Act litigation and congressional intelligence committee reviews, show that agency personnel classified these domestic communications seizures as "inadvertent"—a designation that permitted continued operation of the upstream architecture without triggering warrant requirements under Fourth Amendment standards. The distinction between "incidental" and "inadvertent" collection became the operational pivot point allowing the programs to expand. The FISA Court's role in this expansion requires specific examination.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Rather than restricting Section 702 authority in response to documented over-collection, the court issued successive reauthorization orders—most significantly in 2017 and 2018 under Chief Judge Rosemary Collyer—that modified compliance protocols without materially constraining collection scope. Court filings show NSA officials presented "minimization procedures" purporting to limit use of American data while leaving extraction capabilities intact. Parallel to government surveillance infrastructure, the Prospect's reporting incorporates corporate collection vectors that remain outside FISA's technical jurisdiction. Internet service providers and backbone operators, acting as NSA collection partners under classified agreements, retain access to upstream communications data that government agencies subsequently query through Section 702 channels. This contractor-government architecture creates a secondary market for surveillance infrastructure: AT&T, which operates backbone facilities under contract to the Department of Defense, maintains copies of intercepted traffic longer than NSA retention standards require, creating overlapping custody chains that complicate accountability. The institutional failure here extends beyond single agencies.

    What Else We Know

    The FISA Court operated for years without independent technical expertise to evaluate whether NSA's stated compliance protocols actually functioned as described. Declassified opinions from 2011-2015 reveal that court judges relied entirely on NSA's own technical representations regarding collection scope—assessments that subsequent Inspector General audits contradicted. No independent signal intelligence contractor or academic cryptographer reviewed the actual implementation of filter systems designed to exclude Americans from upstream collection. Congressional oversight committees received summary briefings on Section 702 compliance but not the underlying audit data showing 200,000+ annual captures. This information asymmetry persisted across multiple reauthorization cycles, from 2017 through the Act's 2023 renewal, allowing program expansion to proceed without full legislative review of documented collection rates.

    The Marcus Webb Take
    Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

    The pattern here is institutional capture disguised as technical management. NSA didn't violate Section 702—the agency operated within frameworks Congress and FISA courts authorized, which is precisely what makes this expansion so significant.

    What I find striking is how the surveillance state expands not through dramatic legal reinterpretation but through bureaucratic acceptance of "inadvertent" harm. The government defines the problem, proposes the solution, and evaluates its own compliance. When NSA's own audits document over-collection, the response isn't restriction—it's reclassification.

    The FISA Court's passivity reveals something larger: oversight bodies designed to constrain power lack the technical architecture to actually function as constraints. Judges reviewing surveillance programs operate blind to the ground truth of system performance. This structural deficit benefits NSA, ODNI, telecommunications contractors, and the broader Five Eyes partnership—all entities with interest in maintaining collection capacity.

    Watch the 2025 Section 702 reauthorization debates. Demand that any renewal include mandatory independent technical audits of collection filtering systems, with results disclosed to all FISA Court judges before reauthorization votes. The surveillance state's legitimacy depends on citizens accepting that officials audit themselves fairly. That assumption no longer holds.
