US Gov Enhances Privacy

The US government requires Privacy Impact Assessments for new information technology

Marcus Webb
Former NSA contractor turned privacy watchdog
July 10, 2026
2 min read
AI-ASSISTED

The US government has taken significant steps to enhance privacy protections for its citizens, as evident in the implementation of the E-Government Act of 2002, which recognized the importance of safeguarding personal information in government records and systems. According to the US Department of Commerce, the act requires Federal government agencies to conduct Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs) for all new information technology involving the collection, maintenance, or dissemination of information in identifiable form. This move is seen as a crucial measure to prevent unauthorized access to sensitive information, as highlighted in the recent controversy surrounding the tracking of queer musicians, which has sparked concerns about the potential misuse of personal data, as reported in the article MSG Tracks Queer Musicians.

The E-Government Act of 2002 has allocated substantial funds to support the development of privacy protection measures, with a budget of over $300 million dedicated to enhancing the security of government databases and networks. This investment has yielded significant results, with a notable reduction in data breaches and unauthorized access to personal information. The importance of robust privacy protections has been further underscored by the recent expiration of FISA 702, which has sparked renewed debates about the balance between national security and individual privacy. In a related development, the Japanese government has passed a bill to amend its personal information protection law, which has been hailed as a major victory for privacy advocates, as reported in the article 個人情報保護法改正案が参院で可決.

The US government's efforts to enhance privacy protections have been welcomed by civil liberties groups, who have long advocated for stronger safeguards against the misuse of personal data. The implementation of PIAs has been particularly praised, as it provides a robust framework for assessing the potential privacy risks associated with new information technology. As the government continues to invest in privacy protection measures, it is likely that the issue of data security will remain a top priority, with ongoing debates about the appropriate balance between national security and individual privacy.
