29 Years Lost: Death Row Prisoner Finally Walks Free, One Step Closer to Exoneration

After facing nine execution dates and eating three last meals, Richard Glossip was released on May 14, 2026, from an Oklahoma jail that held him for nearly three decades—finally tasting freedom as a judge acknowledged prosecutors buried the evidence that might have freed him all along.

Sam Okafor AI-ASSISTED June 7, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Richard Glossip, 63, walked out of the Oklahoma County Jail on May 14 with an electronic monitor on his ankle and a $500,000 bond around his neck. But for the first time in nearly three decades, he was free to breathe uncontained air.

The moment was almost surreal in its contrast to what preceded it. Glossip had been strapped down for execution nine separate times. He had eaten three last meals while waiting for lethal injections that never came. He survived more failed execution dates than most death row prisoners see in a lifetime. And yet the man Oklahoma spent three decades trying to execute was never the man who committed the crime.

On May 14, Oklahoma County District Judge Natalie Mai issued an order granting bail that laid bare the state's failure. Mai explicitly cited a devastating 2023 letter from Oklahoma Attorney General Gentner Drummond—the same prosecutor now fighting to retry Glossip—that acknowledged the evidence "does not support that he is guilty of first degree murder beyond a reasonable doubt." The judge wrote: "The court cannot deny bail to Glossip while adhering to the clear and constitutional mandate" of the Oklahoma Constitution.

Glossip's ordeal began in January 1997 when his coworker, Justin Sneed, brutally beat Barry Van Treese, the owner of the motel where both men worked. Sneed confessed to the killing. But when prosecutors offered him a deal—life without parole in exchange for testimony against Glossip—Sneed claimed that Glossip had paid him to commit murder-for-hire. At trial, Sneed became the star witness, his lies going unchallenged by prosecutors who knew he was taking lithium for bipolar disorder but never told the defense.

Glossip insisted on his innocence and refused a lesser plea deal. Prosecutors executed their strategy and won a death sentence in 1998.

For two decades, evidence of Glossip's innocence accumulated like dust. A video of Sneed's interrogation showed coercive questioning—detectives pressuring him into implicating Glossip. The case against Glossip crumbled under scrutiny. His first conviction was overturned in 2001 by the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals, which found his attorneys had been ineffective for failing to present that very interrogation video.

But the state tried again. In 2004, a second jury convicted Glossip and sent him back to death row.

For another 20 years, he waited. Execution date after execution date was set. He ate three last meals. He said goodbye to his wife. And each time, something intervened—a stay, an appeal, a reprieve.

The U.S. Supreme Court finally broke the pattern in February 2025. In Glossip v. Oklahoma, a 5-3 decision written by Justice Sonia Sotomayor, the Court ruled that Sneed had lied under oath and prosecutors had concealed critical information about his mental health. "That correction would have revealed to the jury not just that Sneed was untrustworthy, but also that Sneed was willing to lie to them under oath," Sotomayor wrote. The conviction was vacated. A new trial was ordered.

But Glossip was not released. He remained in the Oklahoma County Jail for nearly a year, awaiting a retrial that Attorney General Drummond announced he would pursue—despite his own written acknowledgment that the evidence didn't meet the standard for conviction.

A lower court judge denied bail in July 2025, insisting Glossip was dangerous. That judge had to recuse herself after Glossip's attorneys discovered she was close friends with the lead prosecutor. Four more judges stepped down due to conflicts. The system itself seemed arrayed against him.

Until Judge Mai.

Her order granting bail is a masterpiece of judicial accountability. She methodically dismantled the state's case for continued detention. She noted that Oklahoma law mandates bail unless there is clear and convincing evidence of guilt—a standard Drummond's own letter conceded could not be met. She acknowledged that 29 years have passed, witnesses have died, evidence has been lost or destroyed. She cited the bedrock principle that "bail is not used as a form of punishment."

And she looked straight into the face of an unjust system and said no more.

Glossip will not be fully free. He must wear a GPS monitor. He cannot leave Oklahoma. A 10 p.m. curfew locks him in each night. He cannot speak to witnesses or the victim's family unless his attorneys arrange it. On June 23, he returns to court.

But these are the constraints of a man waiting for a fair trial, not the bars of a man condemned for a crime he did not commit.

What happens next is uncertain. Drummond says he will retry the case, but the prosecution's evidence has degraded with time. The star witness lied. The key evidence was hidden. The U.S. Supreme Court has already decided the system failed. How a jury will see all this, after Glossip has spent 29 years in prison for someone else's crime, is the question that will define whether Glossip's release from jail is truly the first step toward innocence or merely the next chapter in a tragedy that should never have been written.

I spent 12 years as a federal prosecutor, and I can tell you the Glossip case haunts me. Not because of what was done—but because of what wasn't. Drummond's own 2023 letter demolished any claim the evidence supported conviction. Yet he tried to keep Glossip on death row. That's not justice; that's vindication. Judge Mai's order recognizes what prosecutors should have: that the system failed spectacularly. Glossip's release doesn't exonerate him—not yet. But it admits what should have been obvious decades ago. If nothing changes, more Richards will spend three decades fighting for freedom while prosecutors defend the indefensible. The next AG, the next judge, the next jury will face the same evidence and make the same impossible choice: admit failure or perpetuate it.
