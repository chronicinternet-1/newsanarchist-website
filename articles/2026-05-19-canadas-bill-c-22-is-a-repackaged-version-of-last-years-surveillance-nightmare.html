C-22 Is a Repackaged Version of Last Year's Surveillance Nightmare Bill C-22, the Lawful Access Act now circulating through Parliament, mandates that digital service providers—including telecoms, messaging applications, and internet platforms—retain metadata on all users for a minimum of twelve months and share that retained data with foreign governments including the United States. The Canadian government introduced Bill C-2 in the previous legislative session under the stated justification of "border security." That bill failed to advance past initial review stages due to documented opposition from privacy advocacy organizations. Rather than withdraw the proposal, the government has reintroduced substantially similar surveillance architecture under new statutory language.

The Take
Marcus Webb · Surveillance & Tech Privacy

# THE TAKE: Canada's Surveillance Kabuki Never Stopped Bill C-22 isn't repackaged—it's refactored. Same architecture, different API. Last year's C-27 collapsed under pressure from civil liberties groups and cross-party MPs who grasped what Ottawa was actually proposing: warrantless device searches at borders, metadata harvesting without judicial oversight, and CBSA authorities operating in a legal vacuum. The government learned nothing except optics. C-22 strips the most flammable language while preserving the substantive powers. Border agents still get expanded search authority. Data collection mechanisms remain. The surveillance infrastructure persists—just bureaucratically laundered. This is how policy capture works when oversight fails. Not repeal. Rebranding. Canadians should recognize the tell: if they're *relaxing* rhetoric while keeping the powers, they've already won the actual argument.

What the Documents Show

The Minister of Public Safety retains authority to issue demands to digital service companies requiring the creation of backdoors into encrypted communications systems. Critically, Bill C-22 explicitly prohibits companies from publicly disclosing the existence of these ministerial orders. The metadata retention requirement represents the bill's most expansive operational component. Metadata—records showing communication patterns, geographic location data, and temporal information about user activity—does not require content interception to reveal sensitive personal information. A metadata record showing when a user accessed a messaging application and to whom they connected, combined with location data and timestamp information, constructs a detailed behavioral profile without capturing message content.

Follow the Money

The one-year retention mandate creates centralized databases of this information across Canadian digital infrastructure, dramatically expanding the attack surface for unauthorized access and data exfiltration by criminal actors. The backdoor provision contains language designed to circumvent constitutional privacy protections through technical obfuscation. Bill C-22 permits ministerial orders demanding companies undermine encryption protections, provided these orders do not create a "systemic vulnerability." The bill does not define "systemic vulnerability" with sufficient technical precision to prevent administrative overreach. Canadian officials have publicly stated they believe surveillance mechanisms can be added to encrypted systems without introducing systemic vulnerabilities. This assertion is incompatible with the technical properties of end-to-end encryption. Any mechanism that permits authorized third-party access to encrypted communications necessarily compromises the mathematical integrity of the encryption system itself.

What Else We Know

The bill's definitions of both "encryption" and "systemic vulnerability" contain sufficient ambiguity to encompass not only messaging applications but also operating system-level security functions. A ministerial order issued under this language could theoretically demand that Apple, Google, or Microsoft modify operating system code to permit law enforcement interception of communications across their platforms. The mechanism for information sharing with foreign governments—specifically the United States—lacks meaningful oversight constraints. Data retained under the metadata mandate would flow to foreign law enforcement and intelligence agencies under bilateral information-sharing agreements rather than formal treaty obligations with parliamentary authorization. --- THE TAKE --- I find it striking that Canadian lawmakers are pursuing this surveillance architecture through statutory language obfuscation rather than explicit policy debate. The pattern here is institutional: when a surveillance mandate fails politically, governments don't abandon the infrastructure—they rename it and resubmit it.
