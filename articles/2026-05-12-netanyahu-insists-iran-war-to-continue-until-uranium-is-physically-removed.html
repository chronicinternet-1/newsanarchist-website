What they're not telling you: # Netanyahu Insists Iran War To Continue Until Uranium Is 'Physically' Removed Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has declared that military operations against Iran must continue indefinitely until enriched uranium is physically removed from Iranian territory—a demand that Trump administration officials acknowledge could only be accomplished through direct occupation or ongoing warfare. In a 60 Minutes interview that aired Sunday, Netanyahu told CBS host Major Garrett that despite achieving "a great deal" militarily, "additional operations remain necessary." He specified that enriched uranium stockpiles and enrichment sites "have to be taken out of Iran," along with destruction of ballistic missile production capabilities and Iranian proxy networks. Trump subsequently warned that the Iran ceasefire exists on "massive life support," indicating Washington shares Netanyahu's position on removing what Trump calls "nuclear dust" from Iranian territory.

What the Documents Show

When pressed on the logistics of this demand, Garrett directly questioned how such uranium removal could occur, asking whether it would require Israeli and American Special Forces working in tandem under international supervision. Netanyahu dodged the question entirely. Trump's own comments inadvertently revealed the operational difficulty: Iran told him "you will have to get it," acknowledging that only the United States and China possess the capacity to extract material from obliterated nuclear sites. This framing transforms the uranium demand from a diplomatic condition into a justification for continued military presence and operations on Iranian soil. The source material indicates this uranium removal demand represents the primary obstacle to peace negotiations.

🔎 Mainstream angle: The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Rather than describing this as an unrealistic precondition that could justify indefinite conflict, mainstream coverage has largely accepted the framing at face value. The narrative presented—that uranium removal is a legitimate, achievable objective—obscures that this condition functionally requires either Iranian surrender of territorial sovereignty for weapons inspections or perpetual military intervention. Netanyahu's refusal to explain implementation specifics, coupled with Trump's acknowledgment that the sites were "obliterated," suggests no clear mechanism exists for accomplishing this goal without sustained warfare. What the mainstream framing underplays is the asymmetry in negotiating positions. Netanyahu is articulating maximalist demands while simultaneously claiming victory, while Trump attempts to present conflict termination as success. Meanwhile, the American public appears increasingly skeptical of this arrangement, with growing criticism that Washington is executing Israeli military objectives rather than pursuing American national interests.

What Else We Know

For ordinary Americans, this signals potential for extended military commitment in a region where stated objectives remain deliberately vague and operationally undefined. The removal of uranium from Iranian territory—a demand that could theoretically never be fully verified or completed—provides indefinite justification for continued operations that, by Netanyahu's own admission, involve degrading but not eliminating Iranian military capabilities.
