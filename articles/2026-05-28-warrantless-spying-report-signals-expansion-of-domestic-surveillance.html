    Warrantless Spying Report Signals Expansion of Domestic Surveillance

    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 27, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: WARRANTLESS SPYING REPORT SIGNALS EXPANSION OF DOMESTIC SURVEILLANCE ## SECTION 1: THE STORY The NSA continues conducting warrantless surveillance on Americans at scale despite a 2013 inspector general report documenting the practice, according to newly available materials reviewed by The American Prospect. The key finding sits buried in declassified Office of the Inspector General (OIG) documentation: NSA personnel systematically collected phone records, email metadata, and internet activity logs without Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) court authorization between 2008 and 2013. What distinguishes this from the widely-known Edward Snowden disclosures is the institutional continuity—the OIG report indicates the mechanisms enabling these collections remained technically intact and operationally unchanged through subsequent administrations, with no evidence of systematic remediation across the technical infrastructure that performs the actual interception.

    What the Documents Show

    The surveillance infrastructure in question operates through what NSA documents term "upstream collection"—the Agency positions itself at major internet chokepoints operated by telecommunications carriers including AT&T, Verizon, and Level 3 Communications. At these physical intercept points, NSA equipment captures communications transiting domestic networks. The OIG found that personnel accessing this upstream data failed to apply required minimization procedures, meaning US person communications were retained and searched without warrant. The report documents specific instances where analysts queried the database using search terms unrelated to authorized foreign intelligence targets. NSA officials, including then-Director Keith Alexander, received the OIG findings.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    The Agency subsequently revised internal procedures but maintained the upstream collection apparatus itself. This distinction matters technically: revising how personnel access data differs fundamentally from dismantling the collection infrastructure. The physical taps at carrier networks remained operational. The databases remained populated. The technical capability for warrantless surveillance persisted. The mainstream coverage of this material has emphasized the 2013 reforms and treated the issue as historical.

    What Else We Know

    What The American Prospect's reporting reveals is that the underlying technical infrastructure enabling these collections has not been decommissioned. Recent FOIA requests and declassified documents indicate the same carrier partnerships, the same intercept methodology, and substantially the same query access protocols remain in place. The administrative restrictions imposed in 2013 function as procedural overlays on hardware and software systems designed to operate without such restrictions. What deserves scrutiny is the role of the FISA court itself. The court's declassified orders approving NSA surveillance programs contain language that technically permits the upstream collection while imposing minimization requirements. This creates a legal architecture where the collection is authorized in principle but implementation frequently violates the authorization's conditions.
