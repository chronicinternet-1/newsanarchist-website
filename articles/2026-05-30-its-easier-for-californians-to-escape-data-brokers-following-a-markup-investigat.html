    It’s easier for Californians to escape data brokers following a Markup investigation

    The Markup and CalMatters showed how website code could make it harder for Californians to exercise their right to remove personal data. Now much of that code has disappeared.
    Sam Okafor
    Former assistant district attorney
    May 22, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: THE INVISIBLE DELETION: How California's Data Brokers Built Legal Compliance Into a Maze California consumers have a legal right to delete their personal data from brokers—but dozens of companies deliberately obscured how to exercise it. The timeline begins in 2023, when The Markup and CalMatters launched a systematic investigation into data broker practices under California law. The reporters found something revealing: 35 companies had installed "no-index" code on their data deletion request pages.

    What the Documents Show

    This code—a simple instruction to Google and other search engines—prevented these pages from appearing in search results. On paper, the deletion right existed. In practice, it was hidden. The mechanism is worth understanding precisely. When a company places a no-index directive on a webpage, search engines don't catalog it.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    A California resident looking for "how to delete my data from [Company X]" would find nothing. The legal right remained untouched. The pathway to exercise it vanished. This wasn't accidental. Data brokers—companies like IQVIA Digital, Comscore, Telesign Corporation, and 6sense Insights—operate in an industry built on information asymmetry. They profit by knowing things about consumers that consumers don't know about themselves.

    What Else We Know

    The no-index code represented a rational business strategy: comply with the letter of California law while neutralizing its practical effect. As long as the deletion page technically existed, lawyers could argue the company followed regulations. As long as consumers couldn't find it, profits remained undisturbed. The actual intervention came from scrutiny, not enforcement. After The Markup and CalMatiers published their investigation in 2025, Senator Maggie Hassan (D-NH), the ranking member of the Senate Joint Economic Committee, sent letters to five major data brokers. This wasn't a subpoena or a fine.

    The Sam Okafor Take
    True Crime & Justice

    What strikes me most about this case is how it reveals that California's privacy law works only when someone is watching. The right exists. The mechanism exists. But without independent investigation and legislative attention, the right becomes theoretical.

    This isn't unique to data brokers. It's the pattern across regulatory capture: companies design systems that satisfy the letter of law while defeating its purpose. They bet that regulators lack resources or will to enforce. Often, they win that bet.

    The data here shows the exception—journalism and Senate pressure created compliance where regulation alone had not. But watch what happens next: Will those five remaining companies face consequences? Will the FTC use this as a model for systematic enforcement? Or will this be treated as a isolated success story rather than proof of systematic regulatory failure?

    The concrete thing readers need to understand: your legal rights mean nothing without visibility and enforcement. The California data deletion right existed before The Markup's investigation. It was always useless.
