US Spy Program Expires

US intelligence community's foreign spying powers have expired

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 14, 2026 4 min read

The US spy program, specifically FISA Section 702, has expired as of Friday night, marking the first time the intelligence agencies' authority to conduct warrantless surveillance of foreigners abroad has lapsed since its inception in 2008. This development has significant implications for the nation's surveillance capabilities and raises important questions about the balance between national security and civil liberties. As a former NSA contractor turned privacy watchdog, I have been closely following the developments surrounding this program, and it is clear that the expiration of FISA Section 702 has plunged the country into a state of legal uncertainty.

According to reports, lawmakers on the Hill were working on reforms to FISA Section 702, but a short-term extension of the program was rejected by House lawmakers, including 19 Republicans, just a day before the expiration. This rejection has resulted in the unprecedented expiration of the nation's warrantless spy powers, leaving the US intelligence community's foreign spying powers in limbo. The expiration of FISA Section 702 has sparked a heated debate, with some legal experts arguing that the warrantless foreign surveillance program can continue without congressional approval until March 2027.

As the US intelligence community navigates this uncertain landscape, it is essential to consider the potential consequences of unchecked surveillance powers. The American Civil Liberties Union and other advocacy groups have long argued that FISA Section 702 poses significant risks to civil liberties, particularly in the context of warrantless surveillance of foreigners abroad. With the program's expiration, there is a unique opportunity for lawmakers to reassess the nation's surveillance priorities and ensure that any future programs are subject to robust oversight and accountability mechanisms.

The expiration of FISA Section 702 also raises important questions about the role of Congress in overseeing the nation's intelligence agencies. As lawmakers consider potential reforms to the program, they must carefully weigh the competing interests of national security and civil liberties. The fact that 19 Republicans joined Democrats in rejecting the short-term extension of FISA Section 702 suggests that there is growing bipartisan concern about the program's implications for privacy and civil liberties. As the debate over FISA Section 702 continues, it is essential to prioritize transparency and accountability, ensuring that any future surveillance programs are subject to rigorous oversight and respect the fundamental rights of all individuals.
