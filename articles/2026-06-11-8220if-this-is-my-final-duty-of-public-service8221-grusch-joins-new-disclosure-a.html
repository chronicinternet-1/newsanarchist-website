    What they're not telling you: "If This Is My Final Duty of Public Service": Grusch Joins New Disclosure Appeal --- ## SECTION 1: THE STORY A coalition of lawmakers and former intelligence officers gathered at the Capitol on June 9 demanding the release of classified UAP records, arguing that recent government disclosures represent only a fraction of what remains hidden from public view. The event featured former Defense Intelligence Agency officer David Grusch, investigative journalist Leslie Kean, filmmaker James Fox, Representatives Anna Paulina Luna, Tim Burchett, and Eric Burlison, along with Air Force veteran Jeffrey Nuccetelli. Their stated objective was direct: expand transparency around unidentified anomalous phenomena programs, strengthen legal protections for whistleblowers who come forward, and assert greater congressional oversight of activities potentially operating outside traditional budget and authorization channels.

    What the Documents Show

    The most substantive claim made during the conference concerned the existence of advanced technology programs allegedly funded and operated without conventional congressional knowledge or direct appropriations oversight. Multiple speakers suggested that significant UAP-related activities may exist outside the visibility of many elected officials—a suggestion that directly challenges standard congressional budget authorization procedures. While unverified, this allegation carries institutional weight: if accurate, it would indicate a breakdown in the constitutional appropriations process, since Congress holds the power of the purse under Article I of the Constitution.     What Else We Know

    The June 9 event reframed disclosure as preliminary—a first chapter rather than a final accounting. One structural theme emerged consistently: the coalition presented the problem as fundamentally institutional rather than evidentiary. The issue, as they framed it, is not whether UAP phenomena exist—that remains contested—but whether the constitutional mechanisms for congressional oversight and public accountability have functioned as designed. Representatives Luna and Burchett have repeatedly emphasized that certain committees remain uninformed about programs under their nominal jurisdiction, a claim that speaks directly to governmental transparency rather than to the existence of extraterrestrial technology. --- ## THE TAKE What strikes me about this conference is that the principal argument being advanced is institutional rather than paranormal: these officials are not primarily claiming that aliens exist, but that Congress has lost control of its constitutional duty to authorize spending and oversee executive programs.
