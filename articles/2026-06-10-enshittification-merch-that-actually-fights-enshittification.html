    Enshittification Merch That Actually Fights Enshittification

    Enshittification isn&;t just a sweary word to describe the accelerating decay of the online platforms, apps, and services that we rely on.     Diana Reeves
    Former SEC examiner
    June 10, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: THE ENSHITTIFICATION RACKET: How Tech Giants Engineered User Decay While Regulators Watched The Electronic Frontier Foundation is selling t-shirts and enamel pins to fund the legal battles the federal government should be winning on its own dime. This is not a story about merchandise. It is a story about regulatory capture so complete that the only meaningful resistance to platform monopolization now comes from a 35-year-old nonprofit operating on a shoestring, while the Federal Trade Commission, the Securities and Exchange Commission, and the Department of Justice possess statutory authority and billion-dollar budgets they have largely failed to deploy against the structural incentives that have made tech companies systematically hostile to their own users.

    What the Documents Show

    The framework EFF has popularized under the term "enshittification"—the documented pattern by which platforms prioritize advertiser interests over user experience, eliminate privacy protections to maximize surveillance revenue, and leverage monopoly power to foreclose alternatives—describes the precise business model that US regulators have permitted to flourish unchecked for two decades. This is not market failure. This is policy failure with measurable beneficiaries and identifiable victims. Cory Doctorow, who has spent the last 25 years alongside EFF's technical and legal team, articulated the core insight in his recent book: the surveillance business model, the erosion of privacy, the monopoly power that eliminates alternatives, and the regulatory capture that prevents accountability are not bugs in the tech platform architecture. They are structural incentives hardwired into the economic model.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    Meta Platforms generates $114 billion in annual advertising revenue—97 percent of total revenue as of 2023—by monetizing user data harvesting. Google's ad business contributes approximately $175 billion in annual revenue through the same surveillance apparatus. Neither company has faced meaningful regulatory consequence for this architectural choice to treat users as the product rather than the customer. The FTC under Lina Khan has filed cases against Amazon and Meta, but these actions remain exceptional within decades of institutional passivity. The SEC has enforced almost no meaningful penalties against platform executives whose prospectuses and shareholder disclosures have downplayed or omitted the existential business model risk that regulatory action poses to their revenue streams. No C-suite executive responsible for architecting the surveillance infrastructure at major platforms has faced personal financial consequence—not through SEC disgorgement, not through criminal prosecution, not through civil liability.

    What Else We Know

    Meanwhile, EFF's annual budget of approximately $13 million must cover litigation across patent reform, privacy law, encryption standards, and antitrust intervention in platform markets. They fund this through individual donations and institutional grants. They are now, by necessity, generating revenue through branded merchandise to sustain the legal fights that federal regulatory agencies possess explicit statutory mandate to pursue. A nonprofit is distributing enamel pins with modified emoji imagery while Meta's executive compensation packages exceed $1 billion annually and Google's leadership realizes gains measured in the same scale. This is not metaphorical enshittification. This is the institutional enshittification of regulatory capacity itself—the decay of public accountability infrastructure while private monopolies compound their structural advantages.
