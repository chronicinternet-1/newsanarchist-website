28 Years for a Crime They Didn't Commit: Flawed Forensics Finally Free Three Philadelphia Men

Three men walked free from Pennsylvania prisons in May after serving 28 years for a 1997 murder they did not commit, exposing a systemic failure in medical examiner testimony that remains a leading cause of wrongful convictions nationwide.

Sam Okafor AI-ASSISTED June 9, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

Jermal Shuler, Marc Brittingham, and Rasheed Turner are home. After nearly three decades of incarceration for a North Philadelphia murder they did not commit, the three men were formally exonerated on May 26, 2026, when a Philadelphia judge vacated their convictions based on evidence that should have freed them years ago.

The case is a textbook example of how flawed forensic science—specifically bad pathology testimony—can send innocent people to prison for life. It's also a rare victory in Pennsylvania, a state that offers no compensation whatsoever to the wrongfully convicted, leaving exonerees to rebuild their lives from nothing.

On a weekend in 1997, a North Philadelphia woman was killed. Police focused on Shuler, Brittingham, and Turner based largely on the testimony of a single eyewitness who claimed she saw the three men at the crime scene on Saturday night. The case seemed straightforward until it wasn't.

The prosecution's entire timeline rested on expert testimony about the victim's time of death—testimony that was deeply unreasonable. A medical examiner concluded the woman died Saturday night, anchoring the eyewitness account. But here's the problem: the pathologist's methodology was flawed, and no one challenged it vigorously enough to stop three innocent people from being locked away for three decades.

Post-conviction work by defense attorneys and, notably, the Philadelphia District Attorney's Conviction Integrity Unit (CIU), brought in independent forensic pathologists who examined the evidence anew. In 2023, Illinois-based forensic pathologist Dr. James Filkins concluded the victim did not die Saturday night. She likely died early Monday morning—a full day later than the eyewitness placed Shuler, Brittingham, and Turner at the scene.

That conclusion was reinforced in 2025 when Dr. James Gill, chief medical examiner for Connecticut and former president of the National Association of Medical Examiners, reviewed the case for the CIU. Gill opined it was "extremely unlikely" the victim died Saturday night. The original prosecution theory collapsed under scrutiny.

What makes this case particularly instructive—and damning—is that flawed forensic science appears in 52 percent of Innocence Project exonerations. Pathology testimony, fingerprint analysis, ballistics, bite marks: these are supposed to be science. But science is only as good as the scientists behind it and the legal standards that govern its use. In this case, neither sufficed.

The exonerations came through a joint request from multiple organizations: Innocence Project attorneys, the Pennsylvania Innocence Project, the Exoneration Project, DLA Piper, and the CIU. This kind of collaboration—where the prosecution itself advocates for exoneration alongside defense counsel—is increasingly rare and increasingly necessary. It's a model that works. It freed these men.

But here's what happens next: nothing, financially. Shuler, Brittingham, and Turner were imprisoned for more than a combined 84 years. None of them will receive a dime in state compensation. Pennsylvania is one of only fourteen states with no wrongful conviction compensation law whatsoever.

They will rejoin society stripped of three decades—three decades of career building, relationships, accumulated wealth, health, stability. Exoneration is justice delayed. Compensation is justice attempted. Pennsylvania offers neither.

The National Registry of Exonerations documented 3,796 exonerations since 1989. Hundreds remain uncompensated. But what's more insidious is how many more remain in prison because the forensic testimony against them has never been seriously reexamined. Shuler, Brittingham, and Turner got out because someone finally looked harder. Thousands of others are still waiting for someone to care enough to do the same.

The release of these three men is justice, yes. But it is justice that came 28 years too late—and a justice that does not include the basic dignity of compensation for decades of stolen life. That's not a legal system correcting its mistakes. That's a legal system doubling down on them.

The Sam Okafor Take
True Crime & Justice

I spent twelve years as a federal prosecutor. I trained on the importance of reliable evidence, robust cross-examination, and the sacred obligation never to convict the innocent. What I learned from cases like Shuler, Brittingham, and Turner is that the system isn't designed to catch its own errors—it's designed to avoid admitting them. Flawed forensic science persists in 52 percent of exonerations because courts allow it to. Defense counsel challenge it, but underfunded defense teams rarely have the resources to hire independent experts. The prosecution has little incentive to reexamine its own case. What Pennsylvania offers these three men is freedom. What it refuses to offer is restitution for three lost decades of life. Fourteen states still have no wrongful conviction compensation law. That's not an oversight. It's a choice. These states have chosen that the cost of the system's failures should be borne entirely by the wrongfully convicted, not by the system itself. If nothing changes—if forensic standards don't improve, if wrongful conviction compensation laws don't expand, if conviction integrity units remain rare and underfunded—then more innocent people will spend the next 28 years in prison. And when they finally get out, they'll get nothing but our apologies.
