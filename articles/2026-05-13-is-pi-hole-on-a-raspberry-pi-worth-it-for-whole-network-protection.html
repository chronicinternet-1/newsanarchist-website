Is Pi-hole on a Raspberry Pi worth it for whole-network protection?

Hey there, does anyone here have experience with Pi-hole? So far I’ve set it up locally on my laptop through docker. But now I’m wondering if it’s worth investing in a Raspberry Pi and connecting it directly to my router to cover my entire network instead of just one machine. Did you notice any real advantages after moving to a dedicated Pi-hole setup?

This reality has largely escaped mainstream tech coverage, which frames Pi-hole as a modest ad-blocking tool rather than what privacy advocates recognize as essential infrastructure. According to discussions on privacy-focused Reddit communities, users moving from laptop-based Pi-hole deployments to dedicated Raspberry Pi setups connected directly to routers experience a network-wide transparency shift: every device in their home—phones, tablets, smart speakers, gaming consoles—suddenly routes. DNS queries through a single, filterable chokepoint.

The Take
Casey North · Unexplained & Emerging Tech

# THE TAKE: Pi-hole's Whole-Network Promise Is Oversold Pi-hole devotees peddle a seductive myth: one $35 device blocks ads network-wide. Reality? It's messier. Yes, DNS filtering works. No, it doesn't "protect" your network—it blocks ads and some trackers. But here's what gets buried: Pi-hole can't touch encrypted DNS requests. Modern browsers and apps increasingly ignore your DNS layer entirely. You're playing whack-a-mole. The Raspberry Pi itself adds complexity most users underestimate. Hardware failures, SD card corruption, maintenance headaches. Docker on your laptop already proves the concept; scaling to dedicated hardware rarely justifies the effort. Worth it? Only if you're genuinely tinkering for sport. For actual privacy? VPNs and browser settings do more heavy lifting. Don't let the elegance of the solution overshadow whether you actually need it.

What the Documents Show

The practical effect is dramatic. One user reported that "after moving to a dedicated Pi-hole setup," they gained visibility into which devices were attempting to connect to tracking domains, revealing patterns their individual machine-based setup never captured. Another noted the advantage of "covering my entire network instead of just one machine," emphasizing that piecemeal solutions leave household devices exposed to data harvesting that centralized filtering prevents. What mainstream tech media obscures is the asymmetry this creates: while ISPs nominally cannot see encrypted HTTPS traffic contents, they retain complete visibility of which domains every connected device queries—essentially a map of your family's browsing, shopping, and information-seeking behaviors. A dedicated Pi-hole intercepts this at the DNS level before queries leave your network, creating what users describe as reclaiming "network-level" visibility that corporate ISPs have quietly monetized for years.

Follow the Money

The Reddit discussions reveal users asking fundamental questions the tech press treats as settled: whether this investment is "worth it" for "whole-network protection." The hesitation itself is telling—most households have never considered that unfiltered DNS represents a privacy vulnerability requiring active mitigation. The technical barrier to entry—requiring either hardware investment or willingness to configure Linux systems—has effectively gatekept this knowledge. Mainstream coverage treats Pi-hole as a hobby project for tech enthusiasts rather than essential household infrastructure, similar to how router-level firewalls were once framed before becoming standard. Users in privacy communities treat it differently, discussing trade-offs between coverage and maintenance, suggesting they've already accepted that centralized DNS filtering is necessary rather than optional. The broader implication is structural: ordinary households remain transparent to their ISPs and the advertising ecosystem by default, with no mainstream pressure to change this arrangement. Those who deploy Pi-hole are not gaining some exotic advantage—they're reverting to a baseline level of network opacity that was standard before ISPs weaponized DNS visibility.

What Else We Know

The fact that this requires dedicated hardware and technical knowledge, rather than being default, reveals the real surveillance architecture: not one imposed through force, but through the simple friction of remaining ignorant that the alternative exists. The question Reddit users ask—whether a Raspberry Pi deployment is "worth it"—presupposes that most households have already decided transparency is acceptable. The evidence suggests otherwise, but only for those paying attention.
