    Five Things to Know About NSA Mass Surveillance and the Coming Fight in Congress

    What they're not telling you: THE LEGAL ARCHITECTURE OF DRAGNET SURVEILLANCE: NSA BULK COLLECTION PERSISTS DESPITE CONGRESSIONAL REAUTHORIZATION DEBATES The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court has authorized the National Security Agency to collect and retain Americans' phone records in bulk under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, a statutory framework scheduled for congressional reauthorization with minimal structural constraints documented in publicly available legal filings. The NSA's bulk surveillance apparatus operates through a deliberately layered authorization process that obscures its full scope. Section 702, set to expire without congressional action, permits the agency to target foreign nationals but has consistently captured Americans' communications in what NSA officials term "incidental collection."     What the Documents Show

    § 1881a—does not require a warrant for surveillance of non-U.S. persons outside American territory, meaning the agency can monitor international communications that transit U.S. fiber optic cables and data centers without individualized judicial approval. Documents released following Edward Snowden's disclosures revealed that the NSA maintains the ability to search these bulk databases using American citizens' identifiers without demonstrating probable cause or obtaining a warrant. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, which operates under classified procedures and rarely denies government requests, has upheld this architecture.

    Follow the Money

    In 2013, the court approved the NSA's upstream collection program—where the agency copies internet traffic flowing through telecommunications chokepoints—despite contemporaneous evidence that 90 percent of the data collected contained no connection to authorized foreign intelligence targets. The reauthorization debate occurring in Congress treats Section 702's renewal as procedural rather than structural. Proposed amendments focus on narrowing "query" procedures—how NSA analysts search existing databases—rather than restricting collection itself. This distinction matters fundamentally: an analyst can be prohibited from searching a database while the database continues expanding. The American Civil Liberties Union has documented that even modest query reforms proposed in recent legislative sessions would not require deletion of bulk communications already collected or prevent their use in future investigations if legal standards shift. Telecommunications carriers including Verizon and AT&T have cooperated with NSA collection under 18 U.S.C.

The companies face no legal risk for facilitating dragnet collection, while the government's disclosure requirements—mandating transparency reports about surveillance requests—remain limited to law enforcement demands under the Electronic Communications Privacy Act. Intelligence surveillance under FISA carries no equivalent transparency obligation. This creates an asymmetry where corporate complicity in mass surveillance involves minimal exposure and no contractual penalties for overreach. The coming congressional reauthorization will likely extend Section 702 with modifications to query procedures rather than collection authorities. Intelligence officials, including current NSA director General Paul Nakasone, have testified that any significant restriction on collection capability would damage counterterrorism operations.
