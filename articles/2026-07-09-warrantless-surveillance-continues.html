Warrantless Surveillance Continues

Despite expiration of FISA 702 surveillance authorities, warrantless data collection persists through various means

Marcus Webb
Former NSA contractor turned privacy watchdog
July 9, 2026
4 min read
AI-ASSISTED

Warrantless surveillance continues to be a major concern for civil liberties advocates, with the expiration of FISA 702 surveillance authorities on June 12th having little impact on the collection of data, which is set to continue until March 2027, as reported by The Conservative Treehouse. This has led to a growing number of Americans taking matters into their own hands, with some using angle grinders to remove AI surveillance cameras from their towns, prompting concerns over public safety. The Libertarian National Committee has also weighed in on the issue, passing a resolution on July 5, 2026, condemning the use of dragnet surveillance on personal electronic devices, a stance that is in lockstep with historical positions taken by the organization.

The use of warrantless surveillance has been a topic of controversy for many years, with whistleblowers such as Edward Snowden and Thomas Tamm bringing attention to the issue, as documented in the Snowden disclosures. More recently, the use of Flock cameras has raised concerns over the creation of a mass surveillance infrastructure, with the cameras capable of tracking vehicle movements and logging detailed location data on billions of American drivers. This has led to accusations of warrantless surveillance and misuse of data, with some arguing that the use of such cameras is a violation of the Fourth Amendment. As the issue continues to gain attention, it is likely that there will be further debate over the use of global data laws and the impact of facial recognition technology on civil liberties.

The use of automated traffic cameras has also become a topic of controversy, with some arguing that they constitute a form of warrantless surveillance. This has led to protests and calls for the removal of such cameras, with some arguing that they are an invasion of privacy and a violation of the Fourth Amendment. As the debate over warrantless surveillance continues, it is likely that there will be further discussion over the use of such technologies and the impact they have on civil liberties. The government's ability to access data collected by tech companies, such as Google, has also raised concerns, with some arguing that this constitutes a form of warrantless surveillance.

The Marcus Webb Take
Surveillance State & Tech Privacy

As I reflect on the current state of affairs, I firmly believe that warrantless surveillance is a blatant infringement on our civil liberties. My thesis is that the continuation of such practices undermines the very foundations of our democracy. If nothing changes, it's clear that the government and corporate interests will be the winners, as they will continue to exploit our personal data for their own gain. The average citizen, on the other hand, will be left vulnerable to privacy violations and potential abuse of power. It's imperative that we take a stand against warrantless surveillance and demand greater transparency and accountability from those in power.
