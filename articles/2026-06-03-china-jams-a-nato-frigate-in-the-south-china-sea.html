China Jams a NATO Frigate in the South China Sea

For the first time in recorded naval history in the South China Sea, the PLA openly deployed electronic warfare against a NATO member's warship — and then bragged about it.

Rafael Reyes AI-ASSISTED June 3, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

PARACEL ISLANDS / SINGAPORE — On May 27, 2026, the Royal Netherlands Navy frigate HNLMS De Ruyter (F804) was sailing through disputed waters near the Paracel Islands when the People's Liberation Army did something it has never publicly admitted doing before: it hit a Western warship with electronic jamming.

The PLA Southern Theater Command confirmed it in plain language. Its spokesman, Senior Captain Zhai Shichen, announced that Chinese naval and air forces had deployed "necessary measures including verbal warnings and warning electronic interference" to expel the Dutch frigate and its embarked helicopter from waters Beijing claims as sovereign territory. The statement was posted to the Command's official WeChat account and has since reverberated through every naval war college in the Western Pacific.

This is not a small thing. It is a threshold moment.

China has spent years harassing foreign warships in the South China Sea — ramming coast guard vessels, aiming lasers at Philippine sailors, brandishing deck guns. But jamming a NATO frigate's electronics is categorically different. Electronic warfare attacks can disrupt communications, degrade radar, blind navigation systems, and in worst-case scenarios, send aircraft into the sea and run ships aground. The PLA just told the world it is willing to do exactly that — and is proud of it.

According to the U.S. Naval Institute, the incident unfolded when De Ruyter, a De Zeven Provinciën-class air defense frigate and one of the Royal Netherlands Navy's most capable surface combatants, entered the Paracel Island chain — an archipelago seized from Vietnam by Beijing in 1974. The frigate's embarked helicopter repeatedly launched from the deck and, according to the PLA, "violated Chinese territorial airspace." The Southern Theater Command scrambled naval vessels, J-16 fighter jets, and electronic jamming systems in response.

The Netherlands confirmed the ship was on its five-month Pacific Archer deployment mission — a freedom-of-navigation patrol aimed at strengthening Indo-Pacific partnerships. De Ruyter had just completed a port call in Manila and exercised alongside the Philippine Navy. Her commander, Rogier de Wit, told Manila Bulletin the week before that an earlier encounter with a Chinese helicopter had been "very professionally" handled on both sides. What happened at the Paracels was not professional. It was a weapons deployment.

Critically, China has never before publicly acknowledged using electronic countermeasures against a foreign warship or aircraft in the South China Sea. South China Morning Post analyst Fu Qianshao, a former PLA Air Force pilot, confirmed this was "a very serious form of warning" — and one notch below the most serious measures available. That notch is kinetic. The PLA is deliberately signaling how many notches remain.

The timing, as always in this theater, was deliberate. The incident on May 27 occurred just two days before the 23rd Shangri-La Dialogue opened in Singapore, Asia's premier defense summit, attended by representatives from 44 countries, including dozens of defense ministers and military chiefs. Beijing sent no defense minister to the forum — for the second consecutive year, Chinese Defense Minister Dong Jun skipped the event, which Beijing regards as a U.S.-led platform. While the world's defense officials gathered to talk about Chinese aggression, the PLA was demonstrating it in real time.

At the same Singapore forum, total military spending across Asia was reported approaching US$630 billion, with China, Japan, South Korea, and the Philippines all expanding defense budgets. Japan's Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi, speaking at the Dialogue, pledged to keep strengthening Japan's military, prompting Beijing's Foreign Ministry to issue a furious condemnation of Tokyo's "neo-militarism" — rhetoric that has become near-daily since Japan's latest defense budget exceeded 9 trillion yen, hitting a record high for the 14th consecutive year since WWII.

De Ruyter was en route to RIMPAC 2026, the Rim of the Pacific exercise off Hawaii, where 31 nations including the Philippines are set to drill from June 24 to July 31. Beijing is watching that list closely. The South China Sea Strategic Situation Probing Initiative noted this week that since 2024, the number of freedom-of-navigation transits by non-U.S. extra-regional navies — European nations, Australia, New Zealand — has "risen sharply." China analyst Hu Bo warned at a press conference that these non-American navies "often lack a clear understanding of the red lines and bottom lines" in the Western Pacific, making their behavior "more aggressive and thus more likely to trigger miscalculations or even armed clashes."

That is the PLA's justification. It is also a threat.

The Netherlands confirmed the ship was exercising its lawful right to freedom of navigation under international law — a claim that directly contradicts Beijing's position that its 10-dash-line territorial claim gives it authority to deny, degrade, or destroy foreign military navigation in the South China Sea. That claim was invalidated by the 2016 UN arbitration ruling, which Beijing rejects entirely.

Meanwhile, a separate story broke this week that compounds the picture. On June 1, Bloomberg reported that at least seven Chinese universities supporting the PLA and the defense industry are seeking access to Nvidia's H200 chips — the most powerful AI processors Washington has ever permitted for sale in China. Two of those institutions, Beihang University and Northwestern Polytechnical University, are part of China's "Seven Sons of National Defense," an elite cluster dedicated to advancing PLA capabilities. Both are blacklisted by the U.S. Commerce Department. Beihang's School of Cyber Science and Technology — which describes itself as having "national defense characteristics and aerospace advantages" — is pursuing a lease to use the chips. This comes after Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang personally lobbied at the recent Xi-Trump summit for eased restrictions on chip exports to China, with his argument forming the basis for Trump's decision to allow H200 sales. The credibility of that argument has now taken a direct hit.

Taken together: the PLA used electronic warfare against a NATO ship near sovereign Chinese-claimed waters the same week China's military-linked universities were caught pursuing the West's most powerful AI chips. One story is about hardware. One story is about software. Both are about the same war being prepared.

The Rafael Reyes Take
Conflict & Emerging Wars

Let me be direct about what happened at the Paracels. The PLA just crossed a line it has never crossed before in public — deploying and then openly advertising electronic warfare against a NATO member's frigate. That's not a warning shot. That's a demonstration for every Navy in the world watching. The message is: we will blind your ships, and we will say so proudly. The Netherlands went quiet. No official rebuttal, no protest on record at the time of filing. That silence is being read in Beijing as validation — and in every Southeast Asian capital as a lesson in what happens to small navies that test China's patience. If nothing changes, here is who wins: Beijing. Every incident that passes without a coherent NATO or allied response narrows the space for freedom of navigation further. Europe is finally showing up to this theater — Dutch, British, German, French hulls in the Pacific — but showing up without a unified doctrine for what to do when the PLA starts jamming your electronics is worse than not showing up at all. China is running an experiment right now. It's measuring how much it can take before someone hits back. So far, the answer is: a lot.
