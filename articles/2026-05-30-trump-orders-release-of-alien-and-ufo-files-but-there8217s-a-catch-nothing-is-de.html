    Trump Orders Release of Alien and UFO Files, But There’s a Catch: Nothing Is Declassified, Yet

    Casey North
    Science journalist
    February 20, 2026
    4 min read

    What they're not telling you: The Declassification Trap: Why Trump's UFO Order May Release Nothing At All President Trump's directive to release government files on aliens and UFOs contains no legal mechanism to actually declassify anything. On Thursday evening, Trump posted to Truth Social that he was "directing the Secretary of War, and other relevant Departments and Agencies, to begin the process of identifying and releasing Government files related to alien and extraterrestrial life, unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP), and unidentified flying objects (UFOs)." The statement triggered immediate headlines about government disclosure, but a closer read of the language reveals a critical gap: Trump did not order declassification. The distinction matters in ways that bypass headline writers and social media entirely.

    What the Documents Show

    Under the National Security Act and executive order protocols, "identifying and releasing" is procedurally different from "declassifying." A declassification order removes legal restrictions. A direction to "begin the process" of identifying material for release delegates authority to the same agencies—the Department of Defense, the CIA, the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency—that have controlled UAP information for decades. Those agencies retain redaction authority, can claim national security exemptions, and can delay indefinitely under the guise of inter-agency coordination. The White House statement also does not specify a timeline, funding mechanism, or accountability measure. It does not name a single official responsible for execution.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    It does not define what qualifies as "alien and extraterrestrial life" material versus classified intelligence on foreign military capabilities, which could easily overlap in practice. And it contains no language that overrides existing classification stamps or orders agencies to waive security reviews. Trump's language—"to begin the process"—is procedurally significant because it implies bureaucratic workflow, not immediate action. Agencies will form working groups, draft protocols, review existing inventories, and coordinate across departments. Each step creates a checkpoint where material can be flagged as sensitive and removed from the release pile. Congress has been requesting UAP records formally since at least 2020.

    What Else We Know

    The Pentagon created a UAP Task Force in 2022. No comprehensive, unredacted disclosure has followed. The timing compounds the confusion. Days before Trump's post, former President Obama told podcaster Brian Tyler Cohen that UFOs are "real" but that he "hadn't seen them." Obama's casual remark went viral and primed public expectation for disclosure. Trump's statement followed, creating the appearance of responsiveness to momentum. But appearance and action diverge sharply when the actual tool—a declassification order with enforcement language—is absent.

    The Casey North Take
    Unexplained & Emerging Tech

    The pattern here is institutional capture masquerading as transparency. What I find striking is not that Trump issued a directive—it's that the language was carefully constructed to appear responsive while preserving agency discretion.

    The Pentagon, the CIA, and the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency benefit enormously from controlled, slow-roll disclosure. They get to claim compliance with a presidential order while maintaining classification authority. Congress gets symbolic movement without substantive oversight. The public gets headlines disconnected from legal reality.

    This reveals a larger structural failure: without explicit declassification language with enforcement teeth, any presidential directive on classified information becomes advisory. Agencies review themselves. Bureaucracy eats urgency.

    What you should watch is whether, within 90 days, the Secretary of War or any named official publicly announces specific, unredacted records entering the public domain with exact dates and file counts. If that announcement never comes, you'll know the process was always the destination.
