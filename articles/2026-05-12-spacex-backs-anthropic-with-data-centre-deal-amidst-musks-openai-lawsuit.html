SpaceX backs Anthropic with data centre deal amidst Musk's OpenAI lawsuit

What they're not telling you: # SpaceX backs Anthropic with data centre deal amidst Musk's OpenAI lawsuit Elon Musk's SpaceX has begun leasing massive computing infrastructure to Anthropic, a move that strategically positions the billionaire within AI's competitive landscape while he simultaneously pursues litigation against OpenAI for allegedly abandoning its nonprofit mission. The deal grants Anthropic access to SpaceX's Colossus 1 facility in Memphis, Tennessee—housing over 220,000 Nvidia processors and delivering 300 megawatts of new capacity—at a moment when Musk is actively battling OpenAI in court over claims the company breached its stated commitment to develop AI for humanity's benefit rather than corporate profit. The timing reveals a striking contradiction rarely emphasized in mainstream coverage: Musk positions himself as AI's ethical guardian while simultaneously capitalizing on the race for computing dominance that his lawsuit ostensibly critiques.

The Take

# THE TAKE: Musk's Anthropic Play Isn't Benevolence—It's Market Consolidation Elon's suing OpenAI while bankrolling Anthropic's infrastructure. Don't call it competition. Call it what it is: vertical integration of the AI oligopoly. SpaceX's data centre deal props up Anthropic as OpenAI's "ethical alternative"—a positioning that conveniently shields both from antitrust scrutiny. Meanwhile, Musk extracts maximum leverage: he tilts the playing field, maintains access to frontier AI capabilities, and positions himself as the principled dissident rather than the monopolist. The lawsuit becomes theatre. Real power consolidates silently through infrastructure contracts. Anthropic gets cheap compute. Musk gets plausible deniability and strategic influence over an AI vendor that refuses to scrutinize his interests. OpenAI remains the villain. The regulatory apparatus sees "competition." Nobody checks the actual ownership concentration. That's the real story.

What the Documents Show

According to Musk's own statement on X, he made the leasing decision after spending time with Anthropic's leadership, stating their work to ensure Claude AI is "good for humanity" impressed him and that "no one set off my evil detector." Yet this ethical positioning obscures a simpler commercial reality—Musk is leveraging SpaceX's infrastructure to maintain influence across AI development, hedging his bets regardless of how the OpenAI lawsuit concludes. If OpenAI loses, Musk gains credibility as AI's conscience. If it wins, Musk maintains a foothold in the sector through Anthropic. The deal also serves SpaceX's imminent IPO strategy, which depends on demonstrating diverse revenue streams beyond government contracts. Anthropic's commitment to explore developing "multiple gigawatts of space-based orbital data centres" with SpaceX—described as "highly capital-intensive and technically challenging"—signals that the company's valuation will rest partly on speculative future infrastructure projects rather than proven capabilities.

Follow the Money

This arrangement allows SpaceX to court investors with the promise of entirely new markets while Anthropic gains computing resources. Neither commitment has been realized at scale, yet both companies benefit from investor enthusiasm around the potential. Anthropic's announcement of new Claude features, including the "dreaming" capability allowing AI systems to review work patterns and store user preferences between sessions, arrives conveniently timed with the SpaceX partnership announcement. The expanded capacity allows Anthropic to double Claude Code's rate limits and remove peak-hour usage caps for paying subscribers—changes that appear responsive to user demand but are directly enabled by SpaceX's infrastructure injection. Meanwhile, Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei has publicly warned that AI could eliminate 50 percent of entry-level jobs within one to five years, yet the company simultaneously races to increase capacity and deploy more capable models into the market. For ordinary people, the implication is that AI development remains fundamentally driven by infrastructure competition and investor returns, not by the stated ethical frameworks these companies promote.

What Else We Know

Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI for abandoning nonprofit principles rings hollow when viewed alongside his immediate move to profit from Anthropic's expansion. The computing power now flowing through SpaceX's facilities will train the next generation of AI systems that will restructure labor markets—systems whose development is shaped primarily by capital availability and competitive positioning, not by transparent public deliberation about AI's societal impact.
