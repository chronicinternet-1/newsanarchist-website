China Sends Warships to Scarborough Shoal Hours After US-Philippine Drills End

On May 31, 2026, the PLA Southern Theater Command launched combat-readiness patrols over Scarborough Shoal — territory Beijing claims but international law rejects — the moment a joint US-Philippine exercise in the same waters concluded, as China's defense minister sat out Asia's premier security summit for the second straight year.

Rafael Reyes AI-ASSISTED June 2, 2026 5 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

SOUTH CHINA SEA — The timing was not accidental. Within hours of Philippine and U.S. forces wrapping up a five-day joint maritime exercise at Scarborough Shoal on May 30, China's People's Liberation Army moved in. By Sunday morning, May 31, Beijing's Southern Theater Command was broadcasting the message on WeChat: naval and air units had commenced combat-readiness patrols across the territorial sea and airspace of what China calls Huangyan Dao.

The PLA Southern Theatre Command organized the patrols across the territorial waters, airspace, and surrounding areas of the shoal, state-run Xinhua reported. Beijing framed the operations as a countermeasure against what it described as rights violations and provocative acts in waters it considers sovereign Chinese territory.

Manila's response was blunt. Rear Admiral Roy Vincent Trinidad, the Armed Forces of the Philippines spokesperson for the West Philippine Sea, confirmed the Chinese presence but dismissed the narrative entirely: the AFP's coordinated maritime domain awareness confirmed that Beijing's claims of coordinated military drills were, in his words, "completely unfounded." Philippine and U.S. forces had just completed their third joint maritime exercise of 2026 in those same waters from May 26 to 30 — a five-day drill that included visit-board-search-and-seizure operations aimed at strengthening maritime domain awareness and a rules-based order at sea.

Scarborough Shoal sits roughly 200 kilometers from the Philippine coast, well inside Manila's exclusive economic zone under UNCLOS — the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea. A 2016 international arbitration ruling invalidated China's expansive nine-dash line claims to most of the South China Sea. Beijing has ignored that ruling for nearly a decade. China effectively seized control of the shoal following a 2012 standoff with Manila and has never let go.

China's coast guard simultaneously issued its own statement, announcing separate law enforcement patrols near the shoal and noting it had been dealing with ships engaged in what it characterized as illegal rights-violation activities in the area since the start of May. No vessels were named. No details were provided.

What makes this week's maneuver particularly calculated is the backdrop against which it unfolded. At the precise moment PLA warships were circling Scarborough Shoal, defense ministers, military chiefs, and security analysts from across the Asia-Pacific were gathering in Singapore for the Shangri-La Dialogue 2026 — Asia's premier defense summit. The three-day event brought together 550 delegates from more than 40 countries. China sent a delegation of military scholars. What it did not send was its defense minister.

Chinese Defense Minister Dong Jun was absent from the Shangri-La Dialogue for the second consecutive year. That absence is not a bureaucratic oversight — it is a strategic statement. The seniority of China's representative at Shangri-La has become a litmus test for regional tension. Beijing views the forum as a US-led platform and has shown open discomfort with the pointed questions raised about its defense posture. The last time China's defense minister appeared was 2024. Last year, Dong reportedly skipped due to Beijing's reluctance to engage with US Secretary of War Pete Hegseth. This year, he stayed home again.

Instead, the Chinese military sent Professor Meng Xiangqing of the National Defense University to deliver Beijing's ideological messaging at a special session themed "Managing Threats to Strategic Stability." Meng invoked the 80th anniversary of the Tokyo Trials, warning against what he described as the revival of Japanese militarism and questioning whether Japan was qualified to discuss international defense cooperation before reckoning with its wartime past.

That broadside was directed at Japanese Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi, who used his own address at the dialogue to push back hard. Speaking at Shangri-La on May 31, Koizumi delivered a pointed rebuttal without naming Beijing directly: "There is a country that has a huge arsenal of nuclear weapons and strategic bombers. Japan has neither of such weapons, and yet Japan is labelled new militarism." He promoted a revamped "free and open Indo-Pacific" vision, announced Japan would take a new role in defense equipment cooperation with regional partners, and stated Japan opposed unilateral changes to the status quo by force or coercion — language universally understood to refer to China's behavior in the East and South China Seas and its posture toward Taiwan.

Koizumi also noted, with visible frustration, that he felt "sad" he could not meet his Chinese counterpart. Japan's door, he said, remains open. Beijing's door, evidently, does not.

The Pentagon's own picture of China's military trajectory is stark. Despite leadership purges that have swept at least 36 generals and lieutenant generals since 2022 — a disruption of command unprecedented in the PLA's modern history — Xi Jinping's military continues advancing toward its 2027 "Centennial Military Building Goal," which intelligence assessments describe as the capability to forcibly seize Taiwan. Simultaneously, the PLA's J-20 fifth-generation stealth fighter is being produced at approximately 120 units per year — a production rate that now exceeds annual F-35 acquisitions by the US Air Force. China's nuclear warhead arsenal, sitting in the low 600s in 2024, is projected to exceed 1,000 warheads by 2030.

At Scarborough Shoal, the Southern Theater Command has been escalating its intensity throughout 2026. The command's own language on May 31 confirmed that readiness patrols in the territorial sea and airspace of the atoll and surrounding areas had been continuously intensifying since May. Independent monitors have described the Chinese presence near the shoal in recent months as unprecedented in scale. In previous patrol cycles captured in official footage, a constellation of PLA Navy warships, coast guard vessels, and armed aircraft — fighters carrying missiles under their wings — have been documented maneuvering in close proximity to the feature.

For Manila, the cycle is sickeningly familiar. Since 2012, Philippine officials have protested Beijing's actions, pointed to the Hague ruling, and watched it be ignored. Washington has reiterated treaty commitments. Joint exercises with the US — now at least their third in 2026 — continue. And every time the flags are lowered after a drill, China moves back in.

This is not a patrol. This is a garrison.

What China did at Scarborough Shoal on May 31 wasn't a military escalation by Western definitions — no shots were fired, no one was boarded, no blood was shed. But that's precisely the point. Beijing has mastered the art of conquest through persistence. The 2016 Hague ruling said China's claims had no legal basis. Beijing ignored it. The Philippines protests. Beijing ignores it. The US runs exercises. Beijing waits for them to end, then moves back in. The absence of Chinese Defense Minister Dong Jun from the Shangri-La Dialogue for a second straight year is its own form of escalation — a signal that China no longer feels the need to explain itself in any multilateral forum it doesn't control. If nothing changes, the winner is Beijing: it will have turned the West Philippine Sea into a Chinese lake without firing a single declared shot.
