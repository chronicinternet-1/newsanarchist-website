Lawmakers Demand UAP Whistleblower Protections as Trump Files Stall

Three months into President Trump's promised 'maximum transparency' on government UAP files, whistleblowers and a bipartisan group of House members declared the rollout insufficient and demanded legal protection for those willing to expose what they claim are covered-up encounters with non-human intelligence.

Casey North AI-ASSISTED June 11, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

On the steps of the Capitol on June 9, 2026, a coalition that included former intelligence officer David Grusch and four House members from the UAP Caucus drew what investigative journalist Ross Coulthart called "a line in the sand" on government secrecy around unidentified anomalous phenomena.

The timing matters. President Trump had issued a directive in February ordering federal agencies to identify and declassify UAP-related records. Three months later, the Department of War launched the Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters (PURSUE) on May 8, publishing what the Pentagon called a rolling disclosure. A second batch of 222 documents followed on May 22, including declassified audio recordings and videos. The latest release, which included what one source described as a "UFO being shot down," was supposed to prove the administration's commitment to transparency.

But according to those gathered on Capitol Hill, the process has become a bottleneck. Representatives Anna Paulina Luna, Eric Burlison, Jared Moskowitz, and Tim Burchett—along with Grusch, who has testified under oath to Congress multiple times about UAP evidence he encountered in classified channels—called the disclosure incomplete. The core complaint: intelligence officials within the government are obstructing the release of files that Trump has already declassified.

"There are people wanting to come forward who don't feel safe about coming forward," Coulthart told reporters, according to NewsNation coverage of the event. The group demanded passage of the UAP Disclosure Act, legislation designed to strengthen legal protections for whistleblowers and mandate that federal agencies declassify and publish UAP records with full congressional oversight.

This is not abstract bureaucratic friction. The National Security Agency, following a Freedom of Information Act appeal by the Disclosure Foundation just weeks earlier, had released hundreds of pages of historical UAP records previously classified at the "TOP SECRET UMBRA" level—one of the most sensitive clearances in the intelligence community. That release alone suggested material had been withheld. The April appeal bore fruit within weeks. Yet the Capitol Hill coalition suggested the PURSUE system itself was being used as a filter, with career intelligence officials deciding which declassified materials would actually see public light.

The second batch of released documents did include a development that moved the needle slightly: Disclosure Foundation Executive Director Jordan Flowers highlighted a firsthand account from a senior intelligence officer who had encountered unexplained phenomena, plus newly declassified records from nuclear facilities. But Flowers' own framing—that "many of the records were previously classified TOP SECRET UMBRA"—underscored the scale of what remained sealed.

What the files actually contained proved underwhelming to mainstream science. Harvard-affiliated researcher Avi Loeb, commenting on the June 9 press conference, noted that so far he had "not seen clear evidence for a technological object that is not human made." David Whitehouse, an astrophysicist and former BBC science journalist who reviewed the materials, concluded they showed "optical artefacts, others fuzzy blobs, and some light smears. Some obviously balloons. No hint, no evidence whatsoever of anything artificial and alien."

Yet absence of evidence is not the point driving the Capitol Hill push. The real argument is about government credibility and legal process. Grusch emphasized the need for records to be reviewed and publicly released while "maintaining national security protections." This is the crux: lawmakers say the criteria for withholding material are being applied selectively and without congressional review.

The June 25 Capitol Hill forum on UAP transparency, already scheduled, suggests this confrontation will not dissolve quietly. Luna, Burchett, and others have been pushing for UAP disclosure since 2023. Luna herself had set deadlines for Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth to produce videos, which the Associated Press noted came and went with no material delivered—behavior that fueled the June 9 demand for legal consequences.

The administration's PURSUE system was designed to be a "rolling basis" release, suggesting this would be a years-long process. But whistleblowers and their congressional allies are now signaling they will not wait for career bureaucrats to decide what the public gets to know. The next test: whether the UAP Disclosure Act gains traction in a Republican-controlled Congress, or whether the stalling continues behind the veneer of transparency.

I've covered enough government disclosure processes to recognize the pattern. You launch a website, you make noise about transparency, but the actual material gets filtered through the same officials who wanted it classified in the first place. The June 9 Capitol Hill moment matters because it names the problem explicitly: not Trump's directive, but the implementation gap. Grusch is staking his credibility on evidence he claims to have seen in classified briefings. Lawmakers are now demanding whistleblower protections and congressional oversight of the declassification process itself—which is the real battle. The loser if nothing changes? The public's trust. Once you prove the system can be gamed by career intelligence officials who ignore presidential directives, you've essentially admitted the entire PURSUE framework is theater.
