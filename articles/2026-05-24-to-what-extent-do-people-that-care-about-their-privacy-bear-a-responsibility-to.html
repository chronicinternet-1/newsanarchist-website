    To what extent do people that care about their privacy bear a responsibility to establish and develop relationships with their neighbors / communities?

    Handshakes are better than likes and shares right? &;
    Marcus Webb
    Former intelligence contractor
    May 23, 2026
    3 min read

    What they're not telling you: # OFFLINE NETWORKS AS INFRASTRUCTURE: WHY PRIVACY ADVOCATES ARE REBUILDING COMMUNITY TIES AS SURVEILLANCE ARCHITECTURE EXPANDS The privacy-conscious population is systematically rebuilding local community infrastructure as a direct response to the documented scope of digital surveillance systems operated by intelligence agencies and commercial platforms—and this shift represents an institutional acknowledgment that technological privacy solutions alone have failed to contain state and corporate data collection. A discussion thread on r/privacy—a subreddit with over 700,000 members—surfaced a question that distills this migration: "Handshakes are better than likes and shares right?" The framing is deliberately technical. It distinguishes between ephemeral digital interactions (which generate metadata, timestamps, geolocation data, and behavioral profiles) and synchronous physical contact (which leaves no stored record in corporate or government databases).

    What the Documents Show

    The question appears simple. Its prevalence among privacy practitioners indicates something more significant: a documented behavioral shift toward analog-first community organization as users recognize that encryption, VPNs, and technical countermeasures cannot prevent collection at the network layer. This pivot responds to infrastructure facts already in the public record. The NSA's bulk telephony metadata program, revealed through the Snowden disclosures and later documented in the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board's 2014 report, collected records on substantially every phone call made in the United States—a dataset that persisted even after Section 215 of the PATRIOT Act faced statutory constraints. The PRISM program, operated by NSA's Special Source Operations division, integrated data feeds directly from Microsoft, Yahoo, Google, Facebook, and Apple servers.

    🔎 Mainstream angle
    The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

    Follow the Money

    These systems did not require individualized suspicion, warrant approval, or even identifiable targets. They operated as blanket collection infrastructure. What the mainstream privacy discourse typically omits: the legal architecture enabling this collection remains substantially intact. The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Amendments Act of 2008, which codified the NSA's authority to conduct "upstream" collection on international cable traffic, has not been substantively repealed. Section 702 continues to authorize collection on non-US persons communicating with US-based targets, with minimal redaction requirements and no requirement that those US persons be targets themselves. The government's position, maintained across administrations, treats this collection as lawful surveillance of communications infrastructure rather than surveillance of persons.

    What Else We Know

    In this context, the r/privacy community's turn toward local organizing—toward handshakes, face-to-face conversation, and synchronous physical assembly—represents a rational technical response to infrastructure that cannot be opted out of through consumer choices. You cannot encrypt metadata on the network layer if data is collected before encryption occurs. You cannot prevent collection by switching platforms if the collection occurs at the protocol level. The specific recommendation surfacing in privacy communities is explicit: establish trust and information networks at the neighborhood scale, where communication occurs through physical presence rather than platform mediation. This is not ideological localism. It is documented counter-infrastructure strategy.
