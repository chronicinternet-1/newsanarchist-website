FISA 702 Lapses

Congress lets key US spy tool expire amid surveillance debate

Marcus Webb AI-ASSISTED June 14, 2026 4 min read

As a former NSA contractor turned privacy watchdog, I have been closely monitoring the developments surrounding the expiration of Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, a key US spy tool. On June 12, 2026, Congress failed to pass an extension of this program, allowing it to lapse for the first time since its inception in 2008. This means that the intelligence agencies' authority to conduct warrantless surveillance of foreigners abroad has ceased to exist, at least on paper.

According to reports, the House and Senate left town without passing an extension, effectively allowing the program to expire at the end of the day on Friday, June 12, 2026. While a legal quirk may allow the program to continue operating well into next year, technology companies that cooperate with the government may resist, citing the lack of explicit authorization. This raises significant concerns about the potential for unchecked surveillance and the impact on civil liberties.

The expiration of FISA 702 has significant implications for foreign surveillance, and it is unclear how the government will navigate this new landscape. As Kevin Carter, a photographer for Getty Images, captured the empty halls of the US Capitol on June 4, it is clear that the lack of action on this issue is a cause for concern. With the program's expiration, the government may be forced to rely on other, more targeted methods of surveillance, which could potentially be more costly and resource-intensive.

It is worth noting that the expiration of FISA 702 does not necessarily mean that all surveillance activities will cease immediately. However, it does create uncertainty and raises questions about the government's ability to conduct foreign surveillance in the absence of explicit authorization. As the situation continues to unfold, I will be closely monitoring developments and advocating for transparency and accountability in the use of surveillance powers.

In the meantime, the American public is left to wonder what the implications of this lapse will be, and how the government will balance national security concerns with the need to protect civil liberties. As the National Weather Service warns of severe thunderstorms and hazardous heat in various parts of the country, it is clear that the expiration of FISA 702 is a storm of a different kind, one that requires careful attention and scrutiny to ensure that the rights of citizens are protected.
