We have a digital regime that governs through invisible architecture instead of legislation, courts, or votes.

What the Documents Show

This is not hyperbole about corporate power or surveillance capitalism as brand critique. This is structural description. The shift is evolutive, almost invisible in its completion: we've moved from a state theoretically "organized on the principle that it exists to protect the sovereign rights of individuals" to what researcher Siegel calls "a digital leviathan that wields power through the systems that mediate nearly every dimension of public life." The digital leviathan doesn't need Congress. It doesn't need your vote. It doesn't need judges to legitimize its decisions because its decisions aren't decisions — they're algorithms, automated by machines, deployed at scale, invisible to inspection.

🔎 Mainstream angle
The corporate press either ignored this story entirely or buried it in a 3-sentence brief. The framing, when it appeared at all, focused on process rather than impact.

Follow the Money

Consider what this means concretely. When Google's search algorithm determines what news you see, that's governance. When Meta's recommendation system decides which political content reaches which demographic, that's governance. When Amazon's logistics network determines which businesses survive and which fail, that's governance. When payment processors like Stripe or Square decide which merchants they'll serve — sex workers, cannabis dispensaries, disfavored political organizations — that's governance without due process, without transparency, without appeal. The architecture doesn't ask permission.

What Else We Know

It doesn't require legislation. The Federal Communications Commission doesn't regulate algorithmic sorting. The Department of Justice doesn't prosecute invisible discrimination when a machine learning model denies credit or employment based on proxies for race. The Securities and Exchange Commission doesn't require disclosure of the consequential code that determines market structure. I spent years examining banks under the Dodd-Frank regime; we had more power over a loan officer's lending criteria than regulators have over a neural network that affects millions. What's the beneficiary structure?
