23% of Americans Report NDEs—Here's What Scientists Now Know

A sweeping new poll from the International Association for Near-Death Studies reveals that nearly one quarter of American adults claim to have experienced a near-death experience—upending decades of assumptions about how rare these phenomena actually are.

Vera Solano AI-ASSISTED June 7, 2026 4 min read
ORIGINAL REPORTING

The data hit just as the research community marked its first-ever Near-Death Experience Week. For consciousness researchers, the timing felt inevitable: if one in four Americans has encountered the threshold between life and death, then the field's long isolation from mainstream science may finally be ending.

The poll, conducted by IANDS and Centiment, tracked responses from a representative sample of American adults. The headline number—23 percent claiming some form of near-death experience—is staggering when extrapolated to the nation's 260 million adults. That's roughly 60 million people carrying memories of phenomena that neuroscience still struggles to explain.

The revelation arrives amid a remarkable scientific moment. For decades, NDE research occupied an uncomfortable liminal space: too rigorous for casual spirituality communities, too metaphysically tinged for mainstream neuroscience. But recent advances are dissolving that boundary. Researchers at the University of Liège in Belgium, particularly neuroscientist Charlotte Martial, have begun conducting the first-ever EEG recordings of patients actually experiencing NDEs during cardiac arrest. This represents a methodological breakthrough that could fundamentally reshape how science approaches consciousness itself.

Martial's emerging model suggests that NDEs may represent "natural, adaptive mental states"—essentially a sophisticated survival mechanism the brain deploys when it senses imminent collapse. This frames the phenomenon not as hallucination or afterlife glimpse, but as a neurobiologically coherent response to existential crisis. The brain, facing its own failure, generates vivid, structured, life-changing experiences. The question isn't whether they're real—they demonstrably are to the person experiencing them. The question is what layer of consciousness produces them.

But established NDE researchers aren't ready to accept preliminary explanations. At the University of Virginia, renowned consciousness investigator Bruce Greyson, MD, and researcher Marieta Pehlivanova, PhD, recently published a detailed critique of the NEPTUNE model (Neurophysiological Evolutionary Psychological Theory Understanding Near-Death Experience)—an international team's attempt to consolidate NDE science into a unified framework. Their fundamental objection: hallucinations typically involve single sensory channels, whereas NDEs consistently involve multisensory immersion. Experiencers report what they saw, heard, smelled, and touched. These memories persist for decades, burning into consciousness with a stability that contradicts hallucination models. The encounters often involve deceased relatives or unknown figures—interactions with apparent agency and intentionality that standard neurology struggles to accommodate.

The scale of the phenomenon has forced institutional recognition. Since the 1980s, Greyson developed the Near-Death Experience Scale (NDE Scale), which has anchored hundreds of peer-reviewed studies. In April 2026, Greyson and Pehlivanova published analysis of two major NDE assessment tools, recommending continued use of the original instrument while acknowledging both instruments require refinement. This feels like professional consensus crystallizing: NDEs are real, measurable, and deserve systematic investigation.

What makes the new polling data particularly significant is its implication for how widespread NDE-triggered consciousness shifts actually are. Near-death experiencers consistently report radical worldview transformation. These aren't minor experiences. According to researchers at UVA, experiencers frequently describe renewed purpose, desire to serve others, and a felt sense of connection to a greater whole. If 60 million Americans have undergone such transformations, the collective psychological and spiritual impact is genuinely population-scale.

Yet there's a shadow side that emerging research is documenting. While NDEs are typically described as profoundly positive, some survivors report distressing emotions and adjustment difficulties upon return. Greyson himself noted in 2023 that NDEs don't necessarily improve quality-of-life metrics, contrary to decades of anecdotal reporting. One explanation: survivors may unconsciously compare their current earthly existence against memories of transcendental states, inadvertently diminishing their satisfaction with ordinary life.

The IANDS Conference 2026, happening August 27-30 in Bellevue, Washington, will draw researchers, clinicians, and experiencers to examine these questions with unprecedented scope. The organization's announcement of Near-Death Experience Week (running through June 7) included a riveting panel discussion and special podcast content. This represents a major shift toward public education and research visibility.

What remains unclear is whether the 23 percent figure includes milder dissociative episodes or rigorous NDEs meeting Greyson's diagnostic criteria. The poll's methodology matters enormously. A true NDE—with out-of-body perception, encounter with beings, light phenomena, and profound life transformation—is a specific phenomenon. A fleeting sense of detachment during medical crisis is different. Without methodological detail, the number could represent anything from 60 million full NDEs to a much smaller core experiencing true transcendental encounters, with the remainder reporting borderline cases.

What's undeniable: consciousness research is shifting. The taboo is dissolving. Whether through Martial's EEG data, Greyson's rigorous scale development, or IANDS' mainstream polling, the field is moving from anecdotal isolation toward systematic, replicable investigation. The brain's capacity to generate profound experiences at the edge of death is no longer a question of belief. It's a question of mechanism.

The Vera Solano Take

I've spent fifteen years interviewing near-death experiencers, and what struck me most was how consistent their descriptions were—sensory richness, emotional coherence, lasting transformation. The mainstream scientific establishment treated these accounts with polite skepticism. Now we're learning that roughly 60 million Americans may have had these experiences. That changes everything. If consciousness researchers don't seize this moment to establish rigorous, well-funded investigation programs, we'll squander a unique opportunity. Who wins if nothing changes? The skeptics protecting materialist orthodoxy. They benefit from treating consciousness as a solved problem. The experiencers lose—their insights remain marginalized. Science loses. The public loses the chance to understand what consciousness actually is.
